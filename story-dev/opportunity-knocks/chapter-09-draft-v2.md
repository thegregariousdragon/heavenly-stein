# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 9 Draft v2

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## Near Services

Crew Support had given the Andersens soup, towels, a quiet table, and a sentence that did not pretend to be kinder than it was.

Repair investigation.

Not race prep.

Not miracle.

Not disaster.

Repair investigation.

Keisha Bell had taught her people to use words with handles.

Families arriving from bad ships, bad debts, bad news, or bad choices did not need comfort shaped like fog. They needed words they could carry without dropping them. They needed to know which door was theirs, which form mattered first, where the child could sleep, who had authority to say no, and whether the food was theirs to keep if they cried into it.

The Andersens had not cried into the soup.

Nora had considered it.

Moon-bear had apparently advised noodles.

Sven Andersen stood just outside the quiet waiting room near the controlled Services route, holding a Crew Support slate in one hand and a folded towel in the other because someone had handed it to him ten minutes earlier and he had not yet remembered towels could be put down.

Behind the half-frosted privacy glass, Freya sat with Ingrid and Nora at a table meant for crew families who needed to become people again before the next form. Ingrid had one hand around a mug of broth and the other on Nora’s shoulder. Nora was explaining something important to moon-bear with the exhausted authority of a child who had eaten half a roll and therefore returned to governance.

Sven watched them through the glass.

Not directly.

Directly would have felt like wanting too much.

He watched the reflection in the corridor panel.

Freya looked up from her slate and caught him doing it anyway.

Of course she did.

She lifted two fingers.

A question.

He lifted one.

A postponement.

Her mouth tightened.

Not anger.

Accounting.

Then Calder Yim from Crew Support appeared beside Sven with the soft-footed practicality of a man whose job involved finding people before their bravery wore through.

“Shipmaster Andersen?”

Sven turned. “Yes.”

“Freya Andersen asked whether the repair update has a family-safe version.”

Sven looked at the slate in his hand.

He had written one.

He had written three.

The first sounded like a maintenance report had swallowed grief.

The second sounded like grief had found a wrench.

The third began with The ship is not cleared and ended with I am useful now, maybe, which was not a family update so much as a confession wearing boots.

“I’m working on it,” he said.

Calder nodded as if this were a normal category. “Good. Working is allowed. Stalling is also allowed briefly if labeled.”

Sven almost smiled.

Der Himmelskrug staff kept doing that. Naming shame as process. Naming panic as routing. Naming food as doctrine. It was manipulative in a way he was beginning to respect.

Calder held up a small access tab. “Keisha wants Freya to review the temporary lodging language before we assign a room. Nothing dramatic. Crew-support holdover. Privacy lock. No public guest registry. It needs family consent.”

Sven looked through the glass.

Freya was already rising.

“She’ll want to read every line.”

“Good,” Calder said. “Forms respect fear when fear reads carefully.”

“That sounds like something Keisha says.”

“It is something Keisha says after someone else says it wrong.”

Freya reached the door. It opened only far enough for the authorized route, then closed behind her with a soft sound that made privacy feel less like a wish and more like hardware.

She looked at Sven first.

Then the towel.

“Are you planning to negotiate with that?”

Sven looked down.

The towel had been twisted into a rope.

“No.”

“Good. We cannot afford legal representation for towel crimes.”

Calder, to his credit, did not laugh loudly.

Freya took the Crew Support slate from him and read the lodging note.

Her face changed as she moved through it. Not softer. More organized.

“Privacy lock,” she said.

“Yes,” Calder said.

“No public guest registry.”

“Yes.”

“No media access. No race-public status. No financial disclosure beyond required station support accounting.”

“Yes.”

“Temporary room, not charity framing.”

“Yes.”

Freya’s hand tightened slightly on the slate.

Calder said, “Keisha’s instruction was: do not make the family feel purchased by kindness.”

Freya blinked once.

Sven watched that sentence land somewhere deeper than food.

“She is very good,” Freya said.

“Yes,” Calder said. “It is occasionally difficult for the rest of us.”

Freya looked back through the glass at Ingrid and Nora. “I need to sign?”

“Review and consent. You can decline or ask changes.”

Freya gave him a look.

Calder accepted correction. “You can ask changes.”

“I’ll come to the counter.”

Sven moved to follow.

Freya touched his wrist.

Briefly.

“Tell them the accurate version,” she said.

He looked at the quiet room.

Nora was making moon-bear nod solemnly over the soup.

“The ship is not cleared,” Sven said.

“Yes.”

“There is a repair investigation.”

“Yes.”

“I was useful.”

Freya’s eyes sharpened.

Not at the words.

At the risk inside them.

“And?”

“And useful does not mean cleared. Useful does not mean racing. Useful means I can help find maintenance records under livestock insults.”

Freya stared at him.

Calder looked carefully at a wall panel.

Freya said, “I am going to need that sentence again later.”

“I know.”

“Do not tell Nora the livestock part yet.”

“I was not planning to.”

“You were considering it as a joke to make the hard part easier.”

Sven shut his mouth.

Freya nodded once, satisfied.

Then she followed Calder toward the crew-support counter at the end of the controlled-edge corridor.

Sven watched her go.

She did not leave him.

He knew that.

She went six meters away, through a sightline, to read a lodging form with a staff member whose name was on the slate and whose badge had opened the door.

That was not leaving.

Still, the corridor grew around him.

Services was not public. Not exactly. It was not secret either. It was a controlled working layer with staff doors, pilot support counters, laundry and shower signage, medical-direction arrows, crew recovery rooms, maintenance transitions, and route lights that changed politely when a person did not belong somewhere. It smelled faintly of broth, soap, clean fabric, sealant, and people who had been awake too long.

It was quieter than the public levels.

That made every step easier to hear.

Sven turned back toward the waiting room door.

A man stood three paces away.

He looked ordinary.

That was the first wrong thing.

Ordinary people in controlled corridors usually looked mildly lost, apologetic, busy, authorized, or escorted. This man looked like he had been placed.

Mid-thirties, maybe older. Dark jacket. Event visitor pin clipped at a careful angle. No visible tools. No luggage. No badge.

He smiled.

Not warmly.

Professionally.

“Shipmaster Andersen?”

Sven’s hand tightened around the towel.

The man noticed.

His eyes flicked once, recording it.

“Do I know you?” Sven asked.

“No,” the man said. “Marek Sova.”

The name meant nothing.

That did not help.

“Are you with Crew Support?”

“No.”

“Garage Ops?”

“No.”

“Then you should speak to the counter.”

“I’m here on behalf of parties with a preservation interest in collateral currently under your control.”

The corridor narrowed.

Not physically.

Sven felt it happen anyway.

The words came dressed like paperwork, but underneath them something old and ugly had taken off its shoes and entered the room.

Northstar.

His mouth went dry.

The man saw that too.

“Good,” Marek Sova said softly. “Then we can save time.”

Sven looked toward the counter.

Freya stood with Calder six meters away, head bent over the lodging slate. Calder was pointing out a clause. A staff member passed between them carrying folded blankets. For two seconds, Sven could not see Freya’s face.

A woman stepped into the edge of his sightline.

Tilen Voss.

He did not know the name yet.

He knew the placement.

She stopped beside a public information panel at the corridor edge, turned slightly as if reading pilot-support signage, and made the space between Sven and Freya feel like it had acquired a door.

She did not block him fully.

That would have been obvious.

She blocked him socially.

Enough that leaving would require making a scene.

Sven had been raised by freight margins, sickroom bills, old race history, and a father who had written LISTEN FIRST. CORRECT SECOND. BRAG NEVER.

Making scenes still felt expensive.

Marek Sova knew that kind of person.

“Northstar Collateral Services remains willing to accept voluntary resolution,” the man said.

Sven’s pulse thudded once.

“Der Himmelskrug has a Compliance office,” Sven said. “If Northstar has a station-side issue, they can file through proper channels.”

“That would be one option.”

“Then use it.”

“It may not be the best option for you.”

The woman near the information panel shifted half a step.

Not closer.

Worse.

More precise.

Sven looked at her.

She did not look back.

Marek lowered his voice. “Your current choices are narrowing, Mr. Andersen.”

“Shipmaster,” Sven said.

It came out before he thought.

Marek smiled as if he had been given something useful. “Shipmaster, then. You arrived with a disputed preservation asset, requested operational privacy, placed the asset under third-party inspection, and opened a preliminary eligibility path tied to a public event with a material prize purse. Northstar has concerns about asset modification, registration interference, value impairment, and third-party process capture.”

Sven heard the repair bay’s rules return in fragments.

No powered movement.

No practice.

No public status.

Owner may assist under Garage Ops supervision.

He tried to put those words between himself and Marek.

They did not reach far enough.

“The ship is under Garage Ops,” Sven said. “No one is moving her.”

“That is one interpretation.”

“It is the station’s interpretation.”

“Stations have interpretations. Creditors have interests.”

“Flight Control and Garage Ops do not care about your interests.”

Marek’s smile thinned.

There.

A small place where the paper tore.

“No,” he said. “But you do.”

Sven’s hand tightened around the towel again.

Marek’s voice softened. “You have a wife at a counter reading lodging language because you cannot pay for ordinary rooms. You have a daughter eating station soup because station staff have decided to be kind in ways that become forms later. You have a mother watching all of this and pretending not to understand how close the edge is because mothers often prefer dignity to arithmetic.”

Sven stopped breathing properly.

The woman near the information panel did look at him then.

Briefly.

Assessment.

Not pleasure.

Not pity.

“Do not talk about my family,” Sven said.

“I am talking about exposure,” Marek said. “You may keep this quiet now. You may voluntarily resolve the disputed collateral issue before public status changes. You may prevent Northstar from filing emergency preservation objections. You may prevent a private debt problem from becoming a race-week spectacle.”

Sven looked toward Freya.

The woman shifted again.

The sightline narrowed.

Marek opened a slim slate.

No Northstar logo.

Of course not.

“This is a temporary cooperation acknowledgment,” he said. “Not surrender. Not confession. It preserves remedies while allowing asset access discussion before deterioration—”

“No.”

The word surprised Sven.

It had arrived from behind him.

Freya.

She stood three paces beyond the woman at the information panel, Calder half a step behind her, lodging slate still in her left hand.

Her face was calm.

That was when Sven knew she was furious.

Marek turned.

The woman near the panel turned too, smoothly enough that the movement looked casual unless someone was watching for it.

Calder was watching.

His eyes moved from Marek to the woman to Sven to the corridor route light above the Services door.

Then he looked down at his own slate.

One thumb moved.

Small.

Precise.

Freya saw it and did not react.

“What are you asking him to sign?” she said.

Marek gave her the smile people gave spouses when they had mistaken them for obstacles instead of systems.

“Freya Andersen.”

“No,” she said. “You do not get to make my name sound like part of your file. What are you asking him to sign?”

“A temporary cooperation acknowledgment.”

“On whose authority?”

“Interested parties.”

“Which interested parties?”

“Northstar Collateral Services.”

“Are you Northstar?”

“I represent aligned preservation interests.”

“Are you Northstar?”

Marek’s smile held.

Barely.

“No.”

“Are you Mirador Asset Protection Group?”

The corridor became very still.

Sven looked at Freya.

Marek looked at the woman.

The woman did not move.

Freya nodded once, as if a column had balanced.

“Good,” she said. “That answered something.”

Marek recovered first. “Mrs. Andersen, I understand this is emotional.”

“No. You understand that the word emotional is often cheaper than answering questions.”

Calder’s thumb moved again on the slate.

Marek glanced at him.

Calder looked up with the expression of a Crew Support worker reading an ordinary scheduling note.

“Can I help you find a public area?” Calder asked.

Marek did not turn his body fully toward him.

Wrong again.

People who were merely lost turned toward staff.

People who were holding pressure kept their angle.

“We are having a private conversation,” Marek said.

Calder smiled politely. “This is a controlled Services-adjacent crew-support route.”

The woman said, “We are not beyond the threshold.”

Her voice was even.

Professional.

Calder looked at her visitor pin.

Then at the floor line.

Then at the route light.

“No,” he said. “You are not.”

That was not agreement.

It was measurement.

Freya stepped closer to Sven.

The woman shifted to keep the triangle narrow.

Freya looked directly at her.

“Do not do that.”

“I’m standing,” the woman said.

“You are positioning.”

The woman’s eyes changed.

Not much.

Enough.

Marek said, “There is no need to escalate.”

“You approached my husband in a controlled support area under false visitor posture and tried to get him to sign creditor-adjacent paperwork outside station process,” Freya said. “Escalation began before I arrived.”

Marek’s voice cooled. “You may want to consider whether refusing voluntary cooperation improves your position.”

“There it is,” Freya said.

Sven felt the old shame rise, eager and trained.

This was his fault.

He had brought the ship.

He had opened the file.

He had let the station touch a debt that should have stayed in the cargo bay, in the family, in the long cold math of what Lars’s illness had cost and what the freight business could not survive.

Marek looked at him and knew exactly where the thought had gone.

“Shipmaster Andersen,” he said, “you still have a chance to keep this manageable. Once Northstar files formal emergency preservation objections, the station will have to respond. Garage work may freeze. Eligibility review may become impossible. Your family’s debt structure may become public record through dispute filings. Your wife will spend the next week answering questions instead of protecting your daughter from them.”

Freya said, “Stop.”

Marek did not.

“Your cooperation now may prevent that.”

Sven’s hand went numb around the towel.

Freya took one step toward him.

The woman moved.

Not touching.

Blocking.

That was when another voice entered.

“Hey.”

Not loud.

Worker loud.

The kind of voice that carried because it expected machinery to listen.

Pia Stern stood at the maintenance transition fifteen meters down corridor with a tool case in one hand and a Guild safety tag clipped to her sleeve. She had grey at one temple, sealant on her boot, and the posture of someone who had not crossed half a service level to admire visitor etiquette.

The woman turned toward her.

Marek did not.

Pia looked at Calder. “You seeing this?”

Calder said, “Yes.”

Pia looked at Freya. “You want these people in your route?”

“No,” Freya said.

Marek’s smile returned, thinner than before. “This is a private legal matter.”

Pia set the tool case down.

It made a soft, heavy sound.

“No,” she said. “Private is quieter. Legal has badges. This is two visitors narrowing a crew-family corridor while using creditor words.”

The corridor heard that.

Not the whole station.

Enough.

A staff member at the laundry desk looked up.

A pilot waiting near the shower queue stopped pretending not to listen.

The woman near the panel adjusted her stance.

Exit calculation.

Calder’s slate chimed once.

He did not look at it.

A route light above the Services threshold shifted from soft amber to amber-white.

Sven noticed because he had spent the last day learning that Der Himmelskrug used color the way serious people used verbs.

Marek noticed too.

Freya moved beside Sven.

This time the woman did not block her.

Good.

Or worse.

Marek said quietly, “Mrs. Andersen, making a spectacle will not help your family.”

Freya’s voice was just as quiet.

“You are not concerned with my family.”

“I am concerned with preventing irreversible consequences.”

“You are concerned with getting a signature before station process protects the evidence.”

Marek’s eyes hardened.

For one second, the words stopped wearing gloves.

“Station process will not erase seven and a half million credits.”

Sven flinched.

There it was.

The number.

Not shouted.

Worse.

Placed into the corridor like a tool on a table.

The pilot by the shower queue looked away quickly, trying to become kind by pretending not to hear. The laundry-desk staffer’s mouth tightened. Pia’s face did not change at all.

Freya went still.

Marek had made a mistake.

Not because the number was private.

Because he had used it like a hand on someone’s throat.

Freya looked at Calder. “I want that noted.”

“It is,” Calder said.

Marek turned toward him. “What is?”

Calder lifted his slate slightly.

Not high.

Not dramatic.

Enough.

“Possible unauthorized creditor contact near Services threshold. Public visitors engaging controlled crew-family party. Blocked movement concern. Coercive lien language. Private financial detail spoken in semi-public support corridor. Station Security intake notified. Crew Support supervisor notified.”

The woman said, “That is an overstatement.”

Pia said, “No. It is tidy.”

Marek’s smile vanished.

The absence made him look more honest and less ordinary.

“Ilya,” he said.

The woman’s eyes moved.

Sven heard the name.

So did Freya.

So did Calder.

So did Pia.

The false corridor shifted again.

Names mattered.

Marek knew it and disliked that he had given one away.

A calm station voice spoke from the ceiling.

“Crew Support corridor eight, please maintain current positions. Station Security response is en route. Public visitors Marek Sova and Tilen Voss, remain visible and keep hands clear of bags and slates.”

The pilot near the shower queue whispered a curse.

Pia picked up her tool case and moved one step to the side.

Not toward Marek.

Toward the sightline.

Keeping it open.

The woman—Tilen, Ilya, whoever she was under the visitor pin—looked at the route ahead.

The route lights had changed.

Not lockdown red.

Not yet.

But the corridor had learned about them.

Doors that had been neutral now looked attentive. The Services threshold held closed. A staff-only side door that had been cycling for laundry carts stopped accepting casual approach. The public-edge sign brightened with a gentle instruction to remain calm, which was how stations said they had become very serious and did not want anyone to make them prove it.

Marek looked at Sven one last time.

The pressure was still there.

Old debt.

Old fear.

Seven and a half million credits.

A ship not cleared.

A family eating station soup because kindness had found them before money did.

Sven felt all of it.

Then Freya’s hand found his.

Not gripping.

Not rescuing.

Present.

He looked at her.

She did not say Do not sign.

She did not have to.

He looked at Marek.

“No,” Sven said.

It was not loud.

It did not need to be.

Marek’s face closed.

“Unfortunate,” he said.

Calder said, “That word is also noted.”

Pia almost smiled.

Almost.

The station voice spoke again.

“Security response arriving from Medical/Emergency Core and Garage/Service Core. Crew Support personnel, hold current witness positions. Andersen family party, remain by the marked blue line.”

A blue line appeared along the floor beside Sven and Freya.

Soft light.

Clear instruction.

Sven stepped to it.

Freya stepped with him.

Behind the privacy glass, Ingrid had risen.

She stood with one hand on Nora’s shoulder, watching through the frosted panel as if she could see every shape anyway.

Nora’s face was turned toward her grandmother, not the corridor.

Good.

Keisha’s doing, probably.

Or Ingrid’s.

Or both.

Marek and Ilya stood where the corridor had left them.

Not seized.

Not tackled.

Not yet accused of everything they had ever done.

Just visible.

Held by route lights, witness positions, staff attention, and the terrible fact that Der Himmelskrug had noticed the shape of the room.

Sven looked down at the blue line.

Then at the towel still in his hand.

He let go.

The towel unfolded slowly, ridiculous and clean, between him and the floor.

Freya looked at it.

Then at him.

Despite everything, her mouth moved.

“Evidence of towel crimes,” she said softly.

Sven laughed once.

It was too small to be joy.

But it was not shame.

At the far end of the corridor, Security arrived without running.

That was somehow worse.

Janelle Okonkwo was not with them.

Not yet.

The first responding officers wore station blue-black with Security markings and faces trained into calm. Behind them came a Crew Support supervisor and a Medical/Emergency route officer who looked at the corridor the way engineers looked at pressure leaks.

One Security officer addressed the space, not the loudest person in it.

“Everyone stay where you are. We are sorting access, identity, and safety status.”

Marek’s expression became paperwork again.

Ilya’s became empty.

Freya’s hand stayed in Sven’s.

Pia shifted her tool case to her other hand and kept the sightline clear.

Calder’s slate continued recording.

The blue line held.

And somewhere below them, behind more doors than Mirador could reach with visitor pins and borrowed names, *Opportunity Knocks* remained in Garage Ops custody, powered down, not cleared, not promised, not surrendered.

For the first time since Kestrel Minor, Sven understood that a debt collector could still frighten him inside a safe place.

He also understood the difference.

A safe place was not where danger failed to enter.

A safe place was where, when danger tried to make a private room out of you, the walls answered back.

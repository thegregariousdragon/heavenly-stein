# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 8 Draft v2

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## The Repair Becomes Real

Rolf Petersen arrived with the bad kind of quiet.

Oskar Teague had known several kinds.

There was ordinary quiet, which meant a competent person had entered a room and did not need to announce a personality before doing work.

There was angry quiet, which meant a senior engineer had found a bracket doing philosophy where geometry had been requested.

There was management quiet, which meant someone with a title had discovered a problem and was deciding whether to look important near it.

Rolf Petersen brought none of those.

He brought structural quiet.

That was worse.

Structural quiet meant the room had stopped being about whether something was interesting and had become about what would happen when mass, speed, and arrogance met a line they did not respect.

He stepped onto the Starship Garage observation deck with a tablet under one arm, two styluses clipped to his sleeve, and a face that had not yet forgiven anyone for interrupting whatever honest load path he had been working on before Oskar’s message arrived.

He did not look at *Opportunity Knocks* first.

Oskar respected him for that.

Rolf looked at the handling pads.

Then the tow cradle interface.

Then the station-side anchor points.

Then the passive model envelope.

Then, finally, at the ship.

“Port-forward,” Rolf said.

“Good morning to you too,” Oskar said.

“It is not.”

“Fair.”

Rolf enlarged the structural map on the observation display. The old racing craft rested below them on Berth C-Gamma-12, powered down, vanes folded, red-gold Andersen stripe under the working lights. Garage staff moved around her with the careful rhythm of people who had been reminded that legends did not reduce crush hazards.

Sven Andersen stood behind the owner boundary at the diagnostic console, hands clasped behind his back, shoulders held in the shape of a man trying not to be a problem while everyone studied the most expensive problem his family owned.

Heinz Schmitt arrived twenty seconds after Rolf.

Feli Brandt arrived two steps behind Heinz, which meant the twenty seconds had probably included negotiation, physical interception, or both.

Heinz stopped at the observation rail without touching it.

That was new.

Oskar noticed.

Feli noticed Oskar noticing.

Heinz noticed both of them noticing and looked offended with great discipline.

“I am behaving,” Heinz said.

“You announced it,” Feli said.

“I am providing useful context.”

“That remains under review.”

Rolf did not look up. “If either of you makes this display smaller, I will become unreasonable.”

Heinz folded his hands behind his back.

Feli smiled very slightly.

Oskar pointed at the display. “Passive model reproduced the late return under combined attitude profile. No actuator-side failure confirmed. Feedback delay appears when port-forward historical asymmetry is included. Current smoothing hides vibration better but returns later.”

Rolf grunted.

Heinz’s eyes sharpened.

Not fan eyes now.

Engineer eyes.

Oskar appreciated the distinction. He also appreciated that Feli remained close enough to prevent either category from becoming weather.

Rolf dragged the model apart into layers.

Hull geometry.

Repair seam.

Frame stiffness.

Handling pad contact.

Cradle load.

Tow interface.

Simulated attitude transition.

He ignored the dramatic parts with admirable cruelty.

“Your model asks the ship a question,” Rolf said.

“Yes.”

“But the station is holding the ship while you ask it.”

“Yes.”

“Then this is not only a craft problem. It is a hosted-test problem.”

Oskar nodded. “That is why you are here.”

Rolf gave him a look that suggested being correct did not make him welcome.

“The port-forward seam may be harmless under ordinary handling,” Rolf said. “It may be harmless under careful tow. It may be harmless under most passive profiles. But if the frame memory excites the feedback path under combined attitude load, an active test could send bad assumptions into station hardware before anyone has time to call the assumption a mistake.”

Sven’s face tightened.

Oskar saw it from below the rail.

“Shipmaster Andersen,” Oskar said without turning fully away from Rolf, “that sentence is about test design, not blame.”

Sven nodded once.

It was the kind of nod people gave when the words reached them but had to wait in line behind fear.

Rolf looked toward Sven for the first time.

“You declared early,” he said.

Sven blinked. “Yes.”

“Good.”

Then Rolf looked back at the model, apparently finished with emotional support for the day.

Heinz leaned forward by one centimeter and stopped himself.

Oskar said, “Schmitt.”

“I am within my centimeter.”

“That is not a unit recognized by Garage Ops.”

“I withdraw the centimeter.”

Feli whispered, “Growth.”

Heinz ignored her with dignity and looked at Rolf’s display. “May I?”

Rolf did not look away from the model. “You may ask a flight-handling question. You may not bless anything. You may not declare anything safe. You may not say ‘through the Teeth’ in the tone you use for dangerous memories.”

Heinz opened his mouth.

Closed it.

Considered.

“Accepted.”

Feli made a small mark on her slate.

Heinz saw it. “Was that praise or evidence?”

“Yes,” she said.

He turned back to the display. “The passive transition is too clean.”

Oskar raised an eyebrow.

Rolf said, “Explain.”

“It shows the fault, but it is still polite,” Heinz said. “A pilot in a high-performance craft does not meet one perfect combined transition and then go home. They meet correction, then correction to the correction, then the part where the ship’s answer arrives during the pilot’s next question.”

Sven stopped breathing for half a second.

Not dramatically.

Enough that Oskar saw it.

Heinz did too, and changed his angle so he was speaking to the display, not at Sven.

“If the feedback return is late in a combined pitch, yaw, and roll transition, the first danger is not that the ship ignores the pilot. The command path appears clean. The danger is that the pilot begins correcting against an echo. A good pilot can survive one echo. Maybe several. But if the next gate asks for another shape before the first answer has honestly arrived, skill starts feeding the error.”

Amara Reid’s voice entered from the observation comm.

“Flight Control will not accept ‘a good pilot can survive it’ as mitigation.”

No one startled this time.

Everyone was learning.

Heinz nodded toward the comm. “Nor should it.”

“Say that twice,” Amara said.

“Nor should it.”

“Good.”

Oskar looked at the comm pickup. “Were you waiting to enter dramatically?”

“I was waiting for someone to say something that sounded like pilot heroics.”

“That was fast.”

“It usually is.”

Sven looked down at his hands.

Oskar turned back to the display. “No one here is clearing heroics.”

“Good,” Amara said. “Then show me why this matters to my course without making the course a love letter.”

Oskar pointed at Meera.

Meera brought up a restricted internal course-risk display.

Not the public Himmelskrug Run map.

Not the bright festival version with animated lines, sponsor-safe labels, and a cheerful rendering of The Last Pour that made civilians believe physics had agreed to be festive.

This was the working version.

The Knotward Run appeared as controlled volumes, vector tolerances, abort corridors, telemetry gates, exclusion zones, remote-hold triggers, recovery windows, and enough red boundary geometry to ruin anyone’s souvenir mood.

Sven stared at it.

He was not the only one.

Even Oskar, who had seen most things that could be done badly near a station, felt the old professional respect rise in his chest. A sanctioned racecourse was a dare wearing law.

Amara said, “Generic profiles first.”

Meera overlaid the passive combined attitude fault on a neutral handling model.

Late return under stacked correction.

Tiny delay.

Small hesitation.

A line arriving after the pilot expected it.

Then she layered in course-risk categories.

Not race prediction.

Not eligibility.

Risk categories.

Kaltmond came first.

The barren moon appeared as a gravity field, legal corridor, exit window, and several stern shapes that meant: arrive wrong here and people who do not care about your dream will become busy.

Heinz kept his voice even. “Kaltmond is not only speed. It is exit timing. If the ship gives the pilot a late answer during the slingshot exit correction, the pilot may widen the line, then tighten late, then overcorrect into a corridor boundary.”

“Disqualification if lucky,” Amara said.

“Abort if luckier,” Rolf said.

Oskar looked at him.

Rolf shrugged. “Impact if not.”

Sven’s face went still.

Feli watched him but did not soften the sentence.

Some sentences protected people by refusing to become softer.

Meera moved to the next risk profile.

The Twins’ Teeth.

No one in the room spoke for a moment.

The internal model did not render them romantically. It showed offset mass, angled clearance, approach vector, required presentation, telemetry cones, and abort geometry that was mostly a polite way of saying there were places where a ship should not discover disagreement inside itself.

Heinz’s hands stayed behind his back.

Good.

“Here,” he said quietly, “late feedback becomes presentation risk. The pilot is not just turning. The ship has to present the narrowest safe profile through an angled offset gap. Pitch, yaw, and roll have to agree. If the pilot feels the old answer instead of the current body, the craft may be physically capable of the passage and still arrive in the wrong shape.”

Amara said, “Flight Control translation: no.”

Heinz nodded. “Yes.”

“No as in not cleared.”

“Yes.”

“No as in not a pilot-confidence problem.”

“Yes.”

“No as in fix or stay out.”

“Yes.”

Feli gave Heinz one brief look.

This time it was praise.

He did not react visibly, which was also praise-worthy.

Meera moved to The Gauntlet.

The display became uglier.

Not because any one motion was worse than the Teeth.

Because there were so many.

Repeated corrections.

Tight sequencing.

Recovery gates.

Small attitude changes under pressure, one after another, all pretending to be separate until a ship taught a pilot otherwise.

Oskar folded his arms.

“There,” he said.

Sven looked at him.

Oskar pointed at the model. “This is where a small lie breeds.”

Heinz nodded. “The Gauntlet turns delay into accumulation. If feedback is late, the pilot may make a correct correction too late, then correct the correction while the ship is answering the previous question. The craft starts feeling haunted when it is actually behind.”

Sven’s jaw tightened.

“Haunted” had been a bad word.

Heinz caught it immediately.

“Mechanically behind,” he corrected. “Not haunted.”

Sven nodded.

Small, but real.

Amara said, “The Gauntlet does not permit haunted.”

“Flight Control has no category?” Feli asked.

“We have several. None are flattering.”

Rolf moved the display to the chicanes and The Last Pour.

“Less catastrophic individually,” he said.

Amara said, “Do not say less catastrophic where pilots can hear you.”

“Lower peak severity under normal abort conditions,” Rolf said.

“Better.”

“But still unforgiving if feedback timing is inconsistent.”

“Accepted.”

Oskar looked at Sven. “What did you hear?”

Sven blinked. “Me?”

“Yes.”

The whole room had been talking about his ship like a body on a table. Oskar needed him back inside the work before fear turned him into furniture.

Sven looked at the display.

Then at *Opportunity Knocks* below.

“I heard that the fault is not only the delay,” he said slowly. “It is when the delay arrives. Single correction, maybe manageable. Repeated or stacked corrections, not manageable. Kaltmond exit, Teeth presentation, Gauntlet accumulation.”

Oskar nodded.

“What else?”

Sven swallowed. “I heard that pilot skill can hide the problem until it can’t.”

Amara said, “Good.”

Sven looked toward the comm pickup, surprised.

Amara continued, “That is exactly the sentence Flight Control wants pilots to understand before they become memorial signage.”

No one laughed.

Then Heinz said, very softly, “Amara has excellent motivational materials.”

Feli closed her eyes.

Oskar allowed himself one breath of almost amusement before pointing at the repair display.

“Enough course romance.”

Pavel, from the lower diagnostic station, raised one hand without looking away from his console. “Is that a formal category?”

“No.”

“Good, because I had concerns.”

“Work.”

“Yes, Chief.”

Oskar turned to the team. “Repair path. Not dreams. Not race eligibility. Repair.”

Meera split the display into work lanes.

Rolf took the first stylus.

“Lane one,” he said. “Port-forward historical seam. Full structural map. No assumption that asymmetry is fault by itself. We need stiffness behavior across simulated load, thermal drift, and pad-supported vibration. I want old repair material composition if records exist.”

Pavel nodded. “Already pulling.”

“Lane two,” Heinz said, and stopped.

Oskar looked at him.

Heinz lifted both hands slightly. “Suggesting.”

“Proceed with a useful verb.”

“Lane two suggestion: pilot-feedback smoothing. The current profile hides vibration better but returns late under certain combined transitions. We need to separate useful damping from dishonest delay. The ship should not punish the pilot with every old vibration, but it also cannot flatter them into flying yesterday.”

Oskar considered that.

Then nodded.

“Kiri, write that without poetry.”

Kiri typed. “Rebuild or recalibrate pilot-feedback smoothing to preserve necessary vibration discrimination while reducing delayed return under combined attitude load.”

Heinz looked pleased.

Feli looked relieved that he did not say so.

Oskar pointed at the display. “Lane three. Actuator-side path remains clean until proven otherwise. Keep verifying. Do not let the sexy fault make us ignore boring failure.”

Pavel said, “Boring failure has entered a complaint.”

“Tell it to take a number.”

Meera added the lane.

Rolf said, “Lane four. Station-safe test envelope. Passive first. No pad-load increase until Engineering approves the handling-pad model. No active simulation until Garage Ops and Engineering agree on fixture limits and Flight Control agrees the profile has no traffic/race implication outside the closed test.”

“Add Flight Control telemetry compatibility,” Amara said.

Meera added it.

Oskar said, “Lane five. Owner archive. Maintenance-only. Supervised. Sven identifies family shorthand; garage verifies before using.”

Sven looked up.

“Me?”

“No,” Oskar said. “The other Andersen shipmaster standing behind the owner line with the useful shame problem.”

Sven blinked.

Pavel coughed.

Feli made the expression of a woman deciding whether “useful shame problem” was reportable.

Oskar ignored all of them.

“You know the archive better than our index,” he said. “Your father wrote for himself. Your grandfather wrote for an audience of himself. Your family records may contain usable information disguised as insults, jokes, or evidence of poor emotional regulation.”

Sven’s mouth moved.

“Yes,” he said. “That is accurate.”

“Then be accurate back.”

“I do not know if I can find what you need.”

“Neither do I. That is why we call it work.”

Something shifted in Sven’s face.

Not hope.

Not yet.

Permission to attempt.

Oskar liked that better.

Kiri opened the maintenance archive on the owner-side console with a mirrored Garage Ops readout. Access remained read-only. Every file carried a privacy boundary. Private family correspondence stayed locked. Maintenance logs, race telemetry, repair notes, calibration profiles, parts lists, and tagged structural images opened in controlled panes.

Sven stepped closer to the console.

Still behind the line.

Good.

He scanned the index.

His face changed from frightened to irritated.

That was promising.

“Oh,” he said.

Oskar waited.

Sven leaned in. “The archive is sorting my grandfather’s notes by formal tag.”

“And?”

“That will be useless.”

Kiri looked personally offended on behalf of sorting.

Sven winced. “Sorry. I mean, not useless generally. Useless for my grandfather.”

Oskar said, “Explain before Kiri turns you into metadata.”

Sven pointed at the index. “These entries marked ‘profile notes’ are mostly race bragging. The useful ones are under insults.”

Heinz whispered, “Of course they are.”

Feli did not even look at him. “Quietly.”

Sven selected an old category.

COMPLAINTS / SHIP BEHAVIOR / IDIOTS

A list opened.

Pavel made a sound of reverent recognition.

Oskar pointed at him.

Pavel said, “That was not race awe. That was archival awe.”

“Allowed provisionally.”

Sven scrolled.

“Here,” he said. “My grandfather used ‘lazy’ for transition smoothing. Not pilot laziness. Interface laziness. If a profile says ‘lazy cow,’ it means feedback smoothing too heavy after mixed-axis correction.”

Kiri typed quickly.

Heinz leaned forward, then stopped at Feli’s glance.

Sven opened another entry.

“‘Bites like a priest at a sausage festival’ means over-reporting vibration through the hand.”

Feli looked up slowly.

Rolf said, “I refuse to put that in an Engineering note.”

Kiri said, “Translating to excessive vibration feedback.”

“Thank you,” Rolf said.

Sven kept reading.

His voice steadied as the archive became a thing he knew instead of a room judging him.

“My father cleaned some of the labels later,” Sven said. “He changed ‘lazy cow’ to ‘delayed return under compound transition.’ But not everywhere. Some of the old profiles kept my grandfather’s names because the race team knew them.”

Oskar said, “Can you map your grandfather’s names to your father’s names?”

“Maybe.”

“Maybe is allowed if labeled.”

Sven nodded and opened a second pane.

For several minutes, the garage changed shape.

Not physically.

The same lights held. The same ship waited. The same fault sat in the display, small and stubborn.

But Sven was no longer only watching professionals decide his family’s fate.

He was working.

Carefully.

Nervously.

Usefully.

He found three “lazy cow” entries that matched combined pitch/yaw/roll handoff.

He found one note from his father that referred to “old forward ghost under Teeth-style transition” and then immediately added, “Not ghost. Do not let Nils name things after midnight.”

He found a parts photograph of the port-forward seam before his father’s last major repair cycle.

He found a brace record under a folder called FURNITURE / DO NOT LET FATHER SEE.

Sven stopped at that one.

Oskar saw his hand still.

“What?”

Sven opened the file.

A scanned note appeared.

LARS: port-forward microbrace removed from rejected stiffening set. Do not discard. Father wants to turn it into galley shelf support. If I die, this is not permission.

NILS: cowardice. Excellent shelf angle.

INGRID: both of you are banned from the galley tools.

Sven laughed.

It came out wrong.

Half laugh.

Half wound.

Then, to his credit, he did not apologize.

Oskar let the sound stand.

Feli looked away in the kind way.

Heinz read the note once and put both hands behind his back harder.

Rolf enlarged the attached part image.

“Microbrace,” he said.

Oskar looked at him. “Useful?”

“Maybe.”

Sven wiped one eye with the heel of his hand and leaned closer. “It was rejected?”

Rolf studied the image. “Rejected for installation, maybe. Not necessarily rejected as diagnostic clue.”

Pavel pulled the current seam model beside the old image.

The geometry aligned badly.

Then well.

Then badly again as he rotated it.

“Wait,” Sven said.

Pavel stopped.

“Not that orientation. My father photographed parts the way he removed them if he wanted to remember shame.”

Oskar stared at him.

Sven flushed. “Sorry. Family phrase. I mean—”

“I know what shame means. Point.”

Sven pointed.

Pavel rotated the image ninety degrees and mirrored it.

The old microbrace lined up with the port-forward seam’s current wrinkle.

The garage became quiet again.

Rolf leaned in.

Heinz did not speak.

That was how Oskar knew the image mattered.

Rolf said, “That brace was not installed.”

Pavel said, “No.”

“But the seam was prepared for it.”

“Looks that way.”

“Then someone changed the repair plan.”

Sven’s face had gone pale again, but this time his eyes stayed sharp.

“My father,” he said.

“Maybe,” Oskar said.

“Maybe,” Sven corrected.

Good.

Rolf added the image to the structural lane. “We need the reason it was rejected.”

Sven was already searching.

“My father may have written it under bills.”

Everyone looked at him.

Sven did not look up. “He hated putting expensive decisions in maintenance because he said maintenance should tell you what happened, not what hurt.”

Feli’s expression changed.

Keisha Bell’s voice entered softly from the side channel. “That is a family-support note if I have ever heard one.”

Sven froze.

Oskar looked toward the comm. “Keisha?”

“Still crew-support language only. Also soup has arrived for Freya, Ingrid, and Nora. Nora has declared moon-bear in favor of noodles.”

Sven closed his eyes briefly.

“Thank you,” he said.

Keisha answered, “You are welcome. Keep working with the people who are not five.”

She clicked out.

Sven breathed.

Then opened a folder labeled ACCOUNTS / DO NOT IGNORE / SHIP.

“There,” he said.

A repair invoice appeared.

Then a deferred-work note.

Then his father’s handwriting, sharper than the typed forms around it.

Port-forward stiffening set rejected for race handling. Corrects seam resonance but makes hand return too dead through fast mixed transitions. Acceptable for museum queen. Not acceptable for a ship that still answers.

Below it, his mother had added a note in a different hand.

If you cannot afford the right repair, do not buy the wrong one and call it caution.

Sven sat back.

No one spoke.

Oskar read the note twice.

Rolf read it three times.

Heinz read it once and then looked at the ship, not like a fan.

Like a pilot hearing a dead mechanic refuse an easy lie.

Oskar spoke first.

“Shipmaster Andersen.”

Sven did not look away from the note. “Yes.”

“That is useful.”

Sven swallowed.

“It does not solve the repair,” Oskar said.

“I know.”

“It does not clear the ship.”

“I know.”

“It does change the question.”

Sven looked up then.

Oskar pointed at the old note. “Your father rejected a stiffening repair because it made the ship less honest through fast mixed transitions. That means our repair cannot be ‘make the seam stiffer and declare victory.’”

Rolf nodded slowly. “A simple stiffening patch could reduce the resonance and worsen the pilot-feedback truth.”

Heinz said, “It might make the ship easier to test and worse to fly.”

Amara said, “Flight Control hates that sentence.”

“Everyone should,” Heinz said.

Oskar looked at Kiri. “Add lane six. Preserve handling honesty.”

Kiri typed.

Lane six: repair must reduce delayed pilot-feedback return without masking necessary handling information or deadening craft response through combined attitude transitions.

Rolf said, “That will be harder.”

Oskar said, “Reality often fails to consider our schedule.”

Pavel stared at the old microbrace image. “We may need to rebuild the rejected path as a diagnostic fixture, not install it. Test what his father refused.”

Rolf nodded. “Maybe. Station-side fixture only. No permanent craft change until we know.”

Heinz said, “And we compare against the old profiles. Nils ugly-but-sooner. Lars cleaner-but-still-honest. Current smoother-but-late.”

“Context only?” Feli asked.

Heinz looked at her. “Context only.”

Oskar pointed at Sven. “Can you identify your father’s cleaner-but-still-honest profile?”

Sven looked down at the archive.

“I think so.”

“Not think. Work.”

“Yes.”

The answer came faster this time.

Not fear-fast.

Ready-fast.

Oskar accepted it.

Below them, a junior mechanic whispered, “Lazy cow profile,” with the hushed awe of someone who had just learned scripture had been filed under livestock.

Oskar turned his head.

The mechanic straightened.

“The phrase is not a religious object,” Oskar said.

The mechanic nodded solemnly. “The phrase is not a religious object.”

“And?”

“It is insulting metadata over useful maintenance context.”

Pavel whispered, “Beautiful.”

Oskar pointed at him too.

Pavel returned to work.

Sven looked at the exchange.

This time the smile arrived without almost.

Brief.

Tired.

Real.

Oskar pretended not to see, which was sometimes the kindest available maintenance procedure.

The work order took shape over the next hour.

It did not look heroic.

Heroic documents were often dangerous.

This one looked like several departments had argued until the paper became safer.

GARAGE OPS CONTROLLED WORK ORDER  
CRAFT: OPPORTUNITY KNOCKS  
STATUS: PRIVATE / OPERATIONAL PRIVACY  
MOVEMENT STATUS: NO POWERED MOVEMENT AUTHORIZED  
RACE STATUS: PRELIMINARY ELIGIBILITY FILE INACTIVE / CONDITIONAL  
PUBLIC STATUS: NONE

Oskar owned the floor sequence.

Rolf attached station-side structural review gates.

Amara attached Flight Control restrictions.

Heinz attached an aerospace handling-context note labeled, in all caps because Feli had leaned over his shoulder, CONTEXT ONLY / NO CLEARANCE AUTHORITY.

Feli attached a privacy and public-status note that used no adjectives and somehow sounded like a locked door.

Keisha attached family-support language: owner may assist under Garage Ops supervision; breaks recommended; family update should use “repair investigation” and “not cleared” rather than “race prep.”

Oskar liked that.

Race prep was a phrase that made fools multiply.

The work order listed the next steps:

1. Complete full port-forward seam structural map.
2. Build diagnostic-only model of rejected microbrace path.
3. Reconstruct Lars cleaner-but-still-honest feedback profile from archive.
4. Compare against Nils-era rough/early profile and current smooth/late profile.
5. Verify actuator-side command path remains clean across passive profiles.
6. Define station-safe passive test envelope.
7. Do not expand to active simulation without Garage Ops, Engineering, and Flight Control agreement.
8. Maintain privacy lock.
9. Permit owner archive assistance under direct supervision.
10. No powered movement, no practice, no registration escalation, no public reveal.

Oskar read it aloud.

Not for drama.

For the room.

Good rooms heard their rules.

When he finished, he looked at Sven.

“Questions?”

Sven stared at the work order.

Then at the ship.

Then back at the work order.

“I can help with item three,” he said.

“Yes.”

“And maybe item two, if the rejected brace has related notes.”

“Under supervision.”

“Yes.”

“You do not touch tools without assignment.”

“Yes.”

“You do not climb into the cockpit because you remembered something emotionally persuasive.”

Sven winced. “Yes.”

“You do not decide silence is noble and withhold uncertainty.”

That one landed harder.

Sven’s jaw moved.

“Yes,” he said.

Oskar nodded.

Then he picked up a small diagnostic pointer from the console and held it out across the owner boundary.

Sven looked at it as if Oskar had offered him a weapon.

“It is a pointer,” Oskar said. “It points. If you manage to injure someone with it, I will be impressed and angry.”

Sven took it carefully.

His fingers closed around the tool.

Oskar pointed toward the archive display.

“You know where she hides things. That does not make you clearance. It makes you useful. Be useful.”

Sven looked from the pointer to *Opportunity Knocks*.

The ship waited under the lights.

Not cleared.

Not promised.

Not condemned.

Surrounded by people who refused to love her carelessly.

Sven turned back to the archive.

“Yes,” he said.

Not loud.

Not triumphant.

Useful.

Oskar returned to the work order. Rolf began muttering about fixture tolerances. Heinz began writing a flight-handling note so carefully that Feli only had to delete two sentences. Amara told someone in Flight Control that no, preliminary eligibility did not mean practice access, and yes, she would personally haunt the form queue if anyone pretended otherwise. Keisha sent soup status. Meera and Kiri rebuilt the archive mapping. Pavel labeled a file LAZY COW - TRANSLATED, then changed it to COMBINED TRANSITION DELAY PROFILE before Oskar could see.

Oskar saw anyway.

He allowed it.

For now.

On the handling pads, *Opportunity Knocks* remained powered down while the work began around her.

Not a shrine.

Not a miracle.

Not a prize.

A dangerous, beloved, repairable machine.

And this time, Sven Andersen was not outside the glass waiting to be judged.

He was inside the process, helping the truth answer on time.

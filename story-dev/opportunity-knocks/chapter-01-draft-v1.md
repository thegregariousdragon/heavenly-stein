# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 1 Draft v1

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## Last Good Haul

Sven Andersen had learned to tell the difference between a ship that was old, a ship that was tired, and a ship that was lying.

*Last Good Haul* was doing all three.

The freighter sat in Berth C-19 of Kestrel Minor's western small-hauler yard with her belly panels open and her berth clamps sunk into scarred deck plating. She had been painted blue once. Someone had cared, once. The name on her flank still showed in proud white block letters if you stood at the right angle and ignored the burn-scored seam where the second L had peeled halfway off.

From most angles, she looked like a bad decision with navigation lights.

Sven stood under the port-side cargo seal with a diagnostic wand in one hand and a maintenance slate tucked under his elbow. The wand hummed, blinked amber, and gave him the same reading it had given him three times already.

Cargo-bay seal wear: tolerable.

He stared at the word until it became insulting.

"Tolerable," he said.

The freighter answered with a soft metallic tick from somewhere above his head.

"Don't start."

The tick became two ticks. Then nothing.

Sven closed his eyes and listened.

Kestrel Minor's hauler yard was never quiet. Fuel pumps thudded. Load cranes squealed along their rails. Somewhere across the row, a crew chief yelled at someone named Mako to stop using a pry bar on anything that had once belonged to a pressure lock. Above it all, station traffic control murmured from half a dozen open comm panels, steady and bored and mercilessly alive.

Under that noise, Sven heard *Last Good Haul* breathing wrong.

Not failing. Not yet.

Wrong.

He shifted the diagnostic wand two centimeters along the seal and ran another scan. Amber again. Same numbers. Same little bureaucratic shrug from a device that did not have to put his wife, daughter, and mother aboard the ship it was politely calling tolerable.

The seal would hold under ordinary departure stress. It would hold under a cautious burn. It would probably hold under a bad docking vibration if the bay stayed powered and the pressure regulators did not stutter.

Probably was a word engineers used when they were ashamed.

Sven entered the reading into the slate anyway. He did not change it. He did not round it upward. He did not tell the ship, the dock, or himself a prettier story.

Cargo-bay seal wear: declared. Monitor during departure. Avoid hard yaw until pressure stabilizes.

His thumb hovered over the next checklist item.

Docking-bay power stability.

From inside *Last Good Haul*, a bank of lamps flickered once, then recovered with the guilty brightness of a child caught stealing sugar.

Sven looked up at the open cargo hatch.

"Really?"

The lamps held steady.

"That was not an apology."

He ducked under the seal lip and climbed the short ramp into the bay. The air changed from yard stink to freighter stink: old coolant, warm insulation, dust trapped in panel seams, and the faint copper bite of wiring that had run too hot too many times. He had grown up around small haulers. He knew the smell of a ship that worked for a living.

This was not that.

This was the smell of a ship that had been asked to keep working after the living was gone.

Inside the cargo bay, tied down under a web of restraint lines and a cradle that Sven had rebuilt twice and distrusted three times, sat the only thing aboard *Last Good Haul* that did not look tired.

*Opportunity Knocks* rested in shadow.

Even here, under bad berth lamps and beside crates of spare filters they could no longer afford to replace twice, the racer kept her own shape. Lean nose. Narrow waist. Control vanes folded in like a sleeping bird's bones. The hull was not glossy. It had the soft, worked finish of something polished by hands instead of money.

Sven did not let himself look at her for long.

Not yet.

First, the freighter.

He crossed to the bay power junction and pulled the cover. The panel resisted. Of course it did. He gave it the flat stare his father used to give stubborn machines, waited three seconds, then tried the release from underneath. The cover came loose with a clack and a puff of gray dust.

"Thank you," Sven said, because Lars Andersen had raised him to be polite to machines until they made that impossible.

The junction's readouts were worse than the seal and better than disaster. The power feed had a pulse in it, a tiny rhythmic sag every twelve seconds. Sven watched it cycle four times.

Unstable docking-bay power: declared. Noncritical systems restricted. Do not run cradle lift and bay pressure correction simultaneously.

He wrote it down.

His slate chimed as soon as the entry saved.

Not an engineering alert. A creditor alert.

Sven did not open it.

The slate chimed again.

He turned it face down on a crate marked FILTERS / DO NOT SELL in his mother's handwriting, which was funny because they had sold two cases yesterday and repacked the empty space with towels.

A third chime came through the freighter's internal comm instead.

"Sven?" Freya's voice.

He straightened too quickly and cracked the back of his head on the open junction cover.

For a moment, every lamp in the bay turned white.

Then red.

Then normal.

Sven bent forward with one hand on the panel frame and the other pressed to his skull.

"Fine," he said, before she could ask.

"I didn't ask if you were fine."

"You were going to."

"I was going to ask whether the cargo-bay seal is actually tolerable or inspection-form tolerable."

Sven looked at the seal, then at the slate, then at *Opportunity Knocks* waiting in the cradle like a question his family had been asking for three generations.

"Both," he said.

There was a pause over the comm. Freya had several kinds of silence. This one had paperwork in it.

"Sven."

"It will hold if we leave gently."

"Will Kestrel Minor let us leave gently?"

"No," Sven said. "But I can."

A loader horn blared outside. Someone swore. The deck shuddered as a neighboring hauler released clamps too fast and made the berth complain.

Freya did not answer immediately.

He pictured her in the forward compartment, dark hair pinned back badly because she only pinned it well when they could afford time, one hand on the comm, the other hand sorting notices by due date, threat level, and whether the sender had used the word final before. Freya had started sorting threats the way Sven sorted system faults: immediate, latent, catastrophic, lying.

She had become very good at it.

That was one more thing he owed the universe an apology for.

"I'm coming back up in five," he said.

"Three."

"I have to check the coolant routing."

"You checked it last night."

"I hated it last night."

"You'll hate it in three minutes too."

"Yes, but with current numbers."

Another silence. Softer.

"Three minutes," Freya said. "Then we talk about Northstar."

The comm clicked off.

Sven stood still.

Northstar.

The name moved through the cargo bay like a draft.

Northstar Collateral Services did not own *Opportunity Knocks*. Sven knew that. Freya knew that. Lars had known that when he signed the refinancing packet with hands made clumsy by medication and rage. The racing ship had been excluded. Family property. Separate title. Separate registration. Separate from the freight business. Separate from the debts that had eaten the freight business one delayed payment at a time.

That was what the clean copy said.

Northstar had other copies.

Northstar had addenda. Northstar had clerks who could sound wounded over comms. Northstar had language about bundled operational assets and disputed reach and preservation of collateral pending review. Northstar had Mirador Asset Protection Group, whose people did not sound wounded at all.

Sven picked up the slate and cleared the creditor notification without reading it.

Then he read it anyway.

Final pre-departure notice.

Of course.

His laugh came out once, small and ugly.

*Last Good Haul* ticked overhead.

"Not you too," he said.

The coolant routing panel was aft of the racer's cradle. To reach it, Sven had to pass within arm's length of *Opportunity Knocks*.

He tried to keep his eyes on the deck.

Failed.

The racer's port flank caught the berth light, and for half a second the old Andersen stripe showed through: red-gold, dulled by time but still exact. Nils had chosen that stripe. Lars had hated it, loudly, for twenty-three years, and had repainted it every time it needed repainting.

Sven's hand lifted before he told it to.

He stopped just short of touching the hull.

"Not yet," he said.

The ship gave no answer. Ships did not answer unless people built systems that let them. Ships did not forgive. Ships did not remember.

But people did, and people put memory everywhere.

On the access panel near the cockpit, Lars's old maintenance marks still sat under the clearcoat: tiny etched dots that meant check the secondary actuator before believing the primary. Nils's original emergency release label remained beside a newer one because Lars had refused to peel it off. A strip of simulator tape still clung to the inside of the canopy frame where Sven had placed it when he was sixteen, after Lars said he kept looking too high on narrow gates.

LOWER YOUR EYES, SVEN.

He had hated that note.

He had obeyed it every time.

The slate chimed again.

Sven shut his eyes.

Three minutes.

He moved past *Opportunity Knocks* and crouched at the coolant routing panel. The bolts were mismatched. One had been replaced with a mining-grade fastener that technically fit if the person writing the manual had given up on hope. He opened the panel and ran the wand along the routing cluster.

Old coolant routing: declared. Manual pressure watch during departure. No automated compensation above safe drift threshold.

The numbers were not good, but they were honest.

That would have to be enough.

He saved the entry.

A small handprint smudged the lower edge of the panel frame.

Sven stared at it.

Nora's.

Probably from yesterday, when she had escaped Freya's packing zone and come looking for "Grandpa Lars's fast ship." She had been carrying a stuffed moon-bear by one leg and wearing one sock. She had asked whether *Opportunity Knocks* was sleeping. Sven had told her yes.

Nora had whispered, "Good night, Opportunity," and patted the air because Freya had told her not to touch anything in the bay.

Sven had nearly come apart right there between the cargo straps and the coolant panel.

He rubbed the handprint with his thumb. It only smeared.

"Good," he said quietly. "Stay."

The freighter-wide comm crackled. Not Freya this time. Berth control.

"*Last Good Haul*, this is Kestrel Minor Yard Control. Departure window begins in twelve minutes. Confirm readiness for clamp release and route insertion."

Sven looked at the open coolant panel, the amber seal warning, the unstable bay power, the rough cradle, the half-gutted family finances sitting in the forward compartment, and the legendary racing ship strapped inside a freighter that had no business carrying her anywhere important.

Then he toggled the bay pickup.

"Yard Control, *Last Good Haul* acknowledges. Final internal checks underway. We'll confirm readiness inside the window."

"Copy, *Last Good Haul*. Do not miss this window. Yard congestion will push next assigned slot by thirty-one hours."

Thirty-one hours.

Sven's jaw tightened.

They did not have thirty-one hours. They barely had twelve minutes.

"Understood," he said.

The comm clicked back to yard chatter.

He closed the coolant panel, tightened the bad bolt, and marked it with a red tag that meant watch me or regret it. Then he stood and finally let his palm rest against *Opportunity Knocks*.

The hull was cool.

Not dead cool. Waiting cool.

His father would have told him not to be dramatic.

His grandfather would have told him drama was acceptable if the ship was fast enough to justify it.

Sven leaned close enough that the berth noise fell away for one breath.

"I'm sorry," he said.

He did not know whether he meant for the ship, his father, his wife, his daughter, his mother, or the version of himself that had once believed skill would be enough.

*Last Good Haul* ticked overhead.

The lamps flickered.

*Opportunity Knocks* waited in the dark, held down by straps, debt, history, and a cradle Sven did not trust.

Three minutes had become six.

Freya was going to kill him.

He grabbed the slate, tucked the diagnostic wand into his belt, and started for the forward corridor. Halfway there, he stopped and looked back once.

The racer did not look like salvation.

Not really.

She looked like one more thing he could lose.

Then the slate chimed again, and Sven made it twelve steps toward the forward corridor before he turned around.

It was not responsible. It was not efficient. It was not the sort of decision a man made when his wife had said three minutes and the ship's departure window was already closing around them like a badly maintained hatch.

He turned around anyway.

The cargo bay lights hummed above *Opportunity Knocks*, flickering at the edges every time *Last Good Haul* remembered it was older than several current safety codes. The racer did not seem to notice. She sat in her cradle with the particular arrogance of well-made machinery: not proud, not alive, not waiting to be admired, simply shaped so exactly for her purpose that the rest of the bay looked embarrassed around her.

Sven climbed the service step built into the cradle frame and keyed the cockpit release.

For half a second, nothing happened.

His stomach tightened.

Then the canopy seal sighed and lifted three centimeters.

"Don't do that," he whispered.

The canopy opened the rest of the way.

Warm stale air slipped out, carrying the smell he had been avoiding since they loaded her: old flight foam, sealed wiring, hand oil, and the faint mineral bite of cockpit scrubbers that had not run a full cycle since Lars was alive.

Sven stayed outside the cockpit for one breath longer than necessary.

Then he climbed in.

*Opportunity Knocks* had never been comfortable. Nils Andersen had believed comfort was what happened after you won, and Lars had preserved that superstition while replacing nearly everything around it. The pilot seat held Sven tightly at the ribs and hips. The restraint web pressed against the exact place on his shoulder where he had bruised himself at seventeen by taking a simulator gate too sharply and pretending he had meant to.

The cockpit knew him.

That was worse than if it had rejected him.

He set the maintenance slate into the side bracket and woke the passive systems. The panels came alive one by one, low-power only. No engine readiness. No flight-control handshake. No external broadcast. Just the private pulse of a ship checking herself in the dark.

The central display blinked, cleared, and loaded the Andersen maintenance shell.

NILS MODE / LARS MODE / SVEN MODE

Sven stared at the options.

"I told him not to name it that," he said.

The ship did not care.

He selected **SVEN MODE** because the alternative was sitting there until the departure slot died of embarrassment. The display rearranged itself into the layout Lars had helped him build when Sven was twenty and still pretending the freight business could recover if everyone just worked harder, slept less, and developed a personal grudge against arithmetic.

Lower left: actuator response map.

Upper right: heat history.

Center: manual trim sensitivity.

Along the bottom, in Lars's plain block script, was a note burned into the interface skin:

LISTEN FIRST. CORRECT SECOND. BRAG NEVER.

Sven exhaled through his nose.

"Hypocrite."

Lars had bragged all the time. Not to customers. Not to creditors. Not to people who would misunderstand and call it arrogance. But at the galley table, after Nora had fallen asleep against Freya's side and Ingrid had pretended not to be listening, Lars could make a routine freight hop sound like a duel with physics and poor manners.

Nils had been worse, according to Ingrid. Nils had once described a legal race line as "the universe offering a dare with paperwork." Sven had believed that story completely until he was old enough to realize Ingrid only repeated it when she missed him.

He reached up and touched the old emergency release label beside the new one.

The old label was ugly. Too large. Slightly crooked. Nils had applied it by hand and then sealed it under three layers of protective film, apparently to prevent taste from removing it.

Lars had hated it. Lars had also replaced the film twice.

A soft knock came from below.

Sven jerked and nearly slammed his knee into the underside of the console.

Not Freya.

Nora stood at the base of the cradle in one sock, clutching her moon-bear by the neck. The bear was purple, lopsided, and technically a Kaltmond souvenir despite the fact that none of them had ever been to Kaltmond. Lars had bought it secondhand from a hauler who claimed that made it more authentic.

"Nora," Sven said carefully.

She looked up at him with the grave suspicion of a child who knew she had broken a rule but had not yet decided whether the rule had deserved it.

"Mama is doing the angry papers," she said.

"That is not what we call them."

"That is what Grandma calls them."

Sven made a note to have a word with Ingrid that neither of them would respect.

"You're supposed to be strapped in soon."

"Not yet. Grandma said soon means after my other sock."

"That sounds like Grandma creating a legal loophole."

Nora considered this. "Is that bad?"

"Usually it's expensive."

She nodded, though she clearly had no use for the answer. Her gaze moved past him into the cockpit.

"Is Opportunity awake?"

"Only a little."

"Can she hear us?"

Sven looked at the dark panels, the soft-running passive bus, the maintenance shell waiting under his father's handwriting.

"No," he said, because that was true.

Nora's face fell.

He added, "But people can hear for ships."

That seemed acceptable. She lifted the moon-bear toward the racer with both hands.

"We're going to the big station," she informed *Opportunity Knocks*. "You have to be good."

Sven's throat tightened so fast he had to look down at the actuator map.

"Good advice," he said.

"She should not be scared."

"No."

"Are you scared?"

The passive systems hummed.

Sven looked at his daughter standing in the bad light of a failing cargo bay, one sock on, one sock missing, trusting him to answer questions in a universe that had recently proven it did not care how carefully adults filled out forms.

"No," he said.

Nora narrowed her eyes.

Freya's eyes.

Sven corrected. "A little."

Nora nodded with immediate satisfaction. "Me too. But Grandma says ships like when you tell the truth."

Of course she did.

From somewhere forward, Ingrid called, "Nora?"

Nora leaned closer to the cradle, dropped her voice, and whispered, "Don't tell."

"Too late," Sven said. "Grandma has station sensors in her bones."

"No she doesn't."

"You don't know that."

Nora giggled. It was a small sound, almost swallowed by the berth noise, but it changed the bay for half a second. Made it less like a place where creditors hunted them and more like a place a family might actually depart from.

Then Ingrid appeared at the cargo bay threshold, leaning on the old rail with one hand and holding a small gray sock in the other.

"There," Ingrid said. "The fugitive pilot."

Nora spun. "I'm not a pilot."

"Not with one sock, no."

Sven started to climb out.

Ingrid's eyes moved from Nora to him, then to the low-lit cockpit around him. She did not scold. That would have been easier. Ingrid Andersen had an old racer's stillness despite never having raced anything except medical billing departments and men who thought grief made women easier to ignore.

"Your wife is counting," she said.

"I know."

"She is using the quiet voice."

Sven winced.

Nora whispered loudly, "That's the bad one."

"It is a very informative one," Ingrid said.

Sven powered down the nonessential displays but left the actuator map up. Something in it had caught his eye, or maybe his nerves were inventing ghosts because the alternative was thinking about Northstar's notice.

He tapped the response-history panel.

The passive diagnostic showed a simple low-power surface check from the last time he ran the control vanes. Nothing dramatic. No failure warning. No red. No error code that would make a garage tech sit forward and swear.

Just a tiny hesitation in the port-side feedback curve.

Sven frowned.

Ingrid saw it. Of course she did.

"What?"

"Nothing."

She said his name without speaking it. Just lifted her chin in the way Lars had inherited and Sven had unfortunately failed to.

He ran the check again.

The port-side curve lagged by a fraction. Barely enough to be a line instead of a thought.

In normal handling, it would disappear under pilot compensation. In docking, irrelevant. In light corrections, harmless. A racer like *Opportunity Knocks* had enough manual personality that any ordinary inspector would call it age, preference, maybe a calibration habit.

But Sven's fingers went cold.

He set his hand on the manual trim and gave the smallest possible correction.

The display answered.

Late.

Not by much.

Not enough to prove anything.

Enough for the cockpit to feel suddenly too tight.

Nora had gone quiet. Ingrid stepped down into the bay and guided her back from the cradle with one hand resting gently on her shoulder.

"Sven," Ingrid said.

"I saw it."

"Saw what?"

"I don't know yet."

That was the most honest answer and therefore the least comforting.

The comm panel near the hatch crackled before Ingrid could respond.

"Sven Andersen," Freya said.

Full name.

Nora sucked in a delighted little breath, because full names were interesting when they belonged to other people.

Sven closed his eyes.

Freya continued, calm and lethal. "If you are in that cockpit, I need you to climb out of three generations of Andersen denial and come to the galley."

Ingrid's mouth twitched.

Sven powered down the maintenance shell. Lars's note vanished last.

LISTEN FIRST. CORRECT SECOND. BRAG NEVER.

"I may need to run another check," he said into the bay pickup.

"You may need to eat paperwork until you understand compound penalties."

"That seems unsafe."

"Then come here before I escalate."

Nora looked up at Ingrid. "Mama is doing jokes."

"No," Ingrid said. "Mama is issuing terms."

Sven climbed out of the cockpit slowly, partly because the cradle step was narrow and partly because his knees had remembered the tiny lag in the controls and decided to file a formal complaint. He sealed the canopy and waited for the pressure line to confirm.

Good seal.

At least someone was telling the truth.

He stepped down beside Ingrid.

For a moment, neither of them looked at Nora. They looked at *Opportunity Knocks*.

Ingrid held the missing sock in both hands now, twisting it once.

"Your father used to say she answered before he asked," she said.

Sven kept his voice low. "She's answering."

Ingrid looked at him.

He swallowed.

"Maybe late."

The words sat in the cargo bay with the debt notices, the flickering lamps, the old freighter, and the child who knew ships liked the truth.

Ingrid nodded once. Not panic. Not disbelief. Just acceptance of a new problem joining the old ones.

"Then listen carefully," she said.

Nora tugged Ingrid's sleeve.

"Do we still go?"

Sven looked at *Opportunity Knocks*, strapped down in the dark. Then toward the forward corridor, where Freya waited with numbers sharp enough to cut through every comforting lie he had left.

"Yes," he said. "We still go."

The answer felt less like courage than arithmetic.

He took Nora's missing sock from Ingrid, crouched, and put it on his daughter's foot. He did it badly. The heel twisted sideways.

Nora inspected his work with pity.

"Papa."

"I'm an engineer, not a sock specialist."

"That's not hard."

"Many great engineers have been defeated by fabric."

She sat on a cargo tie-down bracket and fixed it herself.

Ingrid touched Sven's arm once as she passed him. A small contact. A command, maybe. Or forgiveness. With Ingrid it was usually both.

"Galley," she said.

Sven nodded.

Behind him, *Opportunity Knocks* sealed herself into silence.

Ahead of him, the slate chimed again.

This time Freya appeared at the cargo bay threshold before he could pretend not to hear it. She carried a folder under one arm and the maintenance slate in her other hand, which was impossible because he had left the slate on a crate beside the junction panel.

He looked at his empty hands.

Freya looked at him.

"I confiscated it," she said.

"That was evidence."

"It was avoidance with a battery."

Nora giggled again. Ingrid guided her toward the forward corridor, one hand on the child's back.

Freya waited until they were out of earshot. Then her face changed, not softer, exactly, but less protected.

"Sven," she said, "Northstar updated the claim packet."

He glanced back at the racer.

Freya did too.

The folder in her arm looked very thin for something that could take a ship.

"How bad?" he asked.

Freya's quiet voice returned.

"Bad enough that you need to stop checking what we might lose and come look at how they're trying to take it."

The cargo bay lamps flickered.

*Opportunity Knocks* waited behind him.

Sven followed his wife forward.

The galley table had not been designed for a family, a business, a legal defense, and a slow financial execution.

Freya had made it hold all four.

She had cleared breakfast containers to the counter, stacked Nora's travel cards in the seat webbing, pinned three handwritten lists to the overhead cabinet, and spread the Northstar claim packet across the table in a fan of polite threat. Each page sat squared to the table edge. Each corner had been weighted with something practical: a mug, a filter cap, Ingrid's sewing kit, a stripped relay housing.

Freya Andersen did not let paper look chaotic unless chaos helped her.

Today it did not.

Sven stopped in the galley doorway.

The forward compartment was too small for hesitation. *Last Good Haul* had been built in an era when small-hauler designers believed corridors were a luxury and elbows were a moral failing. Freya stood on the far side of the table, dark hair pinned up with a stylus, sleeves rolled, one knee braced against the bench because the deck had started its departure-window vibration.

She looked tired.

Not sleepy. Sleepy had been six months ago. Tired now meant her face had learned to save motion for emergencies.

Ingrid had taken Nora forward to finish securing the family bags. Sven could hear Nora explaining, with great seriousness, that Papa was not a sock specialist. Ingrid answered something too low to catch. Nora laughed.

The sound made Freya close her eyes for half a second.

Then she opened them and tapped the top page.

"Northstar amended the preservation claim."

Sven stepped inside.

The galley hatch slid half-closed behind him, stuck, reconsidered its life, and remained open by seven centimeters.

Freya did not look at it.

"The disputed lien?" Sven asked.

"Yes."

"They can amend a claim twelve minutes before departure?"

"They can send paper whenever they like. Whether it holds is different."

"But they sent it now."

"Yes."

Sven came to the table and looked down.

Northstar Collateral Services used a serif font.

That bothered him more than it should have. The letters looked calm and old-fashioned, as though the company were not trying to reach through a freighter's hull and wrap one clean hand around his father's ship.

He scanned the first paragraph.

Pursuant to preservation of collateral pending review-

He skipped ahead.

Bundled operational assets-

Skipped again.

Racing craft known as Opportunity Knocks-

His eyes stopped there.

"They named her."

Freya's mouth tightened. "Yes."

"They didn't name her before."

"No."

"Where did they get the registration?"

"I don't know yet."

Sven looked toward the corridor, toward the cargo bay beyond it, as if the racer might have become more visible by being written down.

Freya slid one page toward him.

"They are claiming she functioned as a business asset because she was stored in *Last Good Haul* and because some historical race winnings were used to support Andersen Freight operations."

"That was before my father separated the title."

"I know."

"He separated it."

"I know."

"Freya."

"I know."

He heard himself breathing too hard.

Freya's voice stayed level, which meant she was using both hands to hold it there.

"They are not arguing clean title," she said. "They are arguing reach. They're saying the title is under review because the ship was materially connected to the freight business and therefore must be preserved from transfer, sale, concealment, damage, modification, relocation, or removal from creditor access."

"Creditor access."

"Yes."

"To a privately titled racing ship."

"Yes."

"That they don't own."

"Yes."

"That they want to preserve by taking it away from us with an asset contractor."

Freya's eyes flicked up.

"There you are," she said.

It was not praise. Not exactly. It meant he had finally arrived at the same terrible room she had been standing in for hours.

Sven put both hands on the table. The relay housing rolled under his palm. He caught it before it knocked the pages out of square.

The page had a signature block from a Northstar claims officer named M. Ellery. Sven wondered whether M. Ellery had ever set foot in a cargo bay, ever gotten coolant under a fingernail, ever listened to a dying man explain which ship belonged to the business and which ship belonged to the family because the distinction was the last dignity he could still defend.

He doubted it.

"What do they want?" he asked.

Freya moved the sewing kit and uncovered another page.

"They want acknowledgement of preservation status. They want us to agree not to sell, move, repair, modify, transfer, pledge, display, hide, or otherwise alter the asset pending review."

Sven stared at her.

"We're already moving."

"Yes."

"And she needs repair."

"Yes."

"And if we sign this-"

"We give them the hook they don't quite have yet."

The deck shuddered softly. Departure systems waking.

Somewhere forward, *Last Good Haul* coughed through a pressure adjustment.

Freya glanced at the ceiling. Sven knew she was not checking the sound. She was checking him checking it.

"It's fine," he said.

"Inspection-form fine?"

"Freya."

She held his gaze.

He gave up first. "It will hold if I baby the departure."

"Then baby it."

"I was planning to seduce it with reckless turns."

She did not smile.

The attempt died alone.

Sven looked back at the packet. "What happens if we don't sign?"

Freya pointed to a lower paragraph.

"Escalation to field preservation action."

"Mirador."

"They don't say Mirador in this paragraph. They say approved asset-protection partner."

"Mirador."

"Yes."

The name had weight now. Not because Mirador owned it. Because Northstar had put it in the spaces between words.

Sven remembered the first call with Mirador: a man with a soft voice asking whether Sven understood the consequences of damaging recoverable property. Freya had taken the comm before Sven could answer. She had asked for claim number, jurisdiction, supervising attorney, and proof of authority. The man had become less soft after that.

"They can't board us here," Sven said.

"Not lawfully without yard cooperation or a recognized order."

"Do they have one?"

"No."

"Would the yard cooperate?"

Freya looked down.

That was answer enough.

Kestrel Minor's small-hauler yard had rules. It also had fees, creditors, favors, berth debts, and clerks who could decide not to notice a contractor standing in the wrong corridor for twelve minutes.

Sven pushed away from the table.

"We leave."

"We were already leaving."

"Now we leave faster."

"No," Freya said.

He stopped.

She put one hand flat on the claim packet.

"We leave correctly."

The words landed harder than he wanted them to.

"Sven, listen to me. We do not run like we stole her. We do not dodge departure control. We do not skip declarations. We do not give Northstar the story they want."

"They already have a story."

"They have paper. Don't give them behavior."

He hated how much sense that made.

He hated that she had to be the one to say it.

He hated the page with *Opportunity Knocks* on it most of all.

The slate chimed from under Freya's elbow. She had indeed stolen it from him.

She glanced down.

"Yard Control ping," she said. "Eight minutes."

"I need to get forward."

"In a minute."

"Freya-"

"In a minute."

The quiet voice again.

Sven closed his mouth.

Freya removed the stylus from her hair. A strand fell loose across her cheek. She did not tuck it back.

"I need you to tell me the part you didn't tell me in the bay."

His first instinct was to lie. Not a full lie. An engineering lie. A staging lie. A lie shaped like I need more data, which was true enough to pass through his teeth.

Freya watched the thought cross his face and looked unimpressed by its craftsmanship.

Sven rubbed both hands over his mouth.

"She may be answering late."

Freya went still.

Not frightened still. That would have been easier. This was the stillness she had developed in palliative rooms when doctors used careful voices and left whole futures between sentences.

"How late?" she asked.

"Fractional."

"How fractional?"

"Barely visible on low-power passive."

"Is that bad?"

"Not for ordinary handling."

"Would it be bad if someone else tried to move her?"

He looked at her.

She looked back.

The table between them held the answer in neat Northstar pages.

"Yes," he said. "If they didn't know her."

Freya's fingers tightened once on the packet.

"And if we reach Der Himmelskrug?"

"Then I declare it at proper intake."

"To whom?"

"Garage intake. Flight Control if she ever leaves the cradle. Anyone who needs to know before she's powered for movement."

"No pride."

"No pride."

"No Andersen mystic nonsense."

"That is culturally insensitive."

"Sven."

He nodded. "I'll declare it."

Freya watched him long enough to make sure the promise had weight.

Then she slid a smaller page across the table.

"What is this?" he asked.

"Our current remaining funds after berth fees, fuel allotment, emergency food credit, and the minimum payment I refused to make because it would validate the wrong line item."

Sven looked at the number.

For a moment, he honestly thought the slate had failed to render the rest of it.

"That's all?"

"That's all that is cleanly ours."

He sat down because his legs had become theoretical.

The bench creaked under him.

Freya sat opposite, finally. The table looked too large with the paper between them and too small for what it meant.

Nora laughed again somewhere forward. Ingrid murmured something. A buckle clicked. A bag zipper complained.

Sven lowered his voice.

"Why didn't you tell me it was that low?"

"Because every time I tried, you went to fix something that could be fixed."

The sentence opened him neatly from throat to stomach.

He stared at the number until it blurred.

Freya reached across the table and turned the slate face down.

"I need you here," she said.

"I'm here."

"No. You are in the coolant routing. You are in the cradle. You are in your father's cockpit. You are in every system that can still be persuaded by someone competent. I need you here, where competence may not be enough."

He wanted to defend himself.

He wanted to say that ships were how he kept them alive, that every hour he spent in systems was an hour between Nora and vacuum, that someone had to make the old freighter tell the truth because the creditors certainly wouldn't.

Freya knew all of that.

So he said none of it.

"I don't know how to fix this," he said.

Freya's eyes shone. Only for a second. Then she blinked, and the shine became another thing filed away for later.

"I know."

The deck vibrated harder. The internal comm chimed.

"*Last Good Haul*," Yard Control said, "confirm crew secured and readiness for clamp release in five minutes."

Sven stood.

Freya stood with him.

He reached for the slate. She handed it over, but kept the Northstar packet.

"What are you doing with that?" he asked.

"Documenting refusal without signature."

"Can you do that in five minutes?"

"I did it in three. I'm using the other two to be angry."

Something like a smile twitched at his mouth.

There she was.

Not untouched. Not unafraid. But there.

Freya tucked the packet under her arm.

"And then?" Sven asked.

"And then we get to Der Himmelskrug with clean records and find someone who has the authority to tell Northstar no without caring whether they sound disappointed."

That hit him strangely.

Der Himmelskrug had been a destination before: route hub, job hope, repair chance, maybe a place to disappear into station traffic long enough to negotiate. In Freya's mouth, it became something else. Not rescue. She was too smart for rescue. A test.

He nodded once.

The motion felt like signing something.

The hatch chose that moment to finish closing the seven centimeters it had been considering and bumped Sven gently in the hip.

He looked down at it.

Freya looked down too.

The hatch retracted with a sad little whine.

"Even the doors are making arguments," she said.

"It's a very participatory ship."

"It is a rusted committee."

"She can hear you."

"Good. She owes me two working latches and an apology."

This time he did smile.

Not much.

Enough.

From forward, Ingrid called, "Departure straps, both of you."

Nora added, "Grandma says soon is now!"

Freya gathered the last of the documents. Sven took the slate and checked the departure list one more time.

Cargo-bay seal declared.

Docking-bay power instability declared.

Old coolant routing declared.

Rough handling cradle declared.

Stored racing craft declared.

Possible control-response lag-

His thumb hovered.

Not on *Last Good Haul*. Not part of departure safety unless the ship left the cradle, which it would not. Not something Yard Control needed for clamp release. But at Der Himmelskrug, at intake, under proper garage systems, with people who could verify instead of speculate-

He added a private note.

Opportunity Knocks: possible low-power feedback delay. Full diagnostic required before any powered movement or release from cradle.

Freya watched him type it.

He saved the note.

Then he looked at his wife, the claim packet under her arm, the exhausted precision of her face, the person who had learned to sort threats because he had kept believing engines were the only things that could strand a family.

"I'm sorry," he said.

Freya's expression did not break.

But it bent.

"Apologize by getting us out clean," she said.

So Sven went forward to do that.

Nora was trying to strap the moon-bear into a crash web.

She had chosen the aft-facing jump seat nearest Ingrid, which was sensible, and the smallest restraint loop, which was not. The bear had one purple arm caught under the webbing and one glassy eye pressed hard against the buckle as if it had seen the family accounts and understood everything.

Sven stopped in the forward compartment hatch.

For a moment, he let himself watch.

Nora worked with her whole body when she concentrated. Tongue caught between her teeth. One sock twisted slightly wrong because he had put it on badly and she had repaired his repair only to the standards expected of fathers. Her hair had escaped its clip on one side. The moon-bear slumped patiently under her hands.

Ingrid sat beside her with her own harness loose over one shoulder, pretending not to supervise.

"You're strangling him," Sven said.

Nora looked up, offended. "He has to be safe."

"He also has to breathe."

"He's a moon-bear. They breathe moon."

"Right. My mistake."

Nora gave the buckle a determined shove. It clicked shut around the bear's middle.

"There," she said. "He can't fall out."

"No," Sven agreed. "He is extremely arrested."

"Restrained," Ingrid corrected.

"Restrained," Sven said. "Professionally."

Nora patted the bear's head. "He doesn't like departure."

"Did he say that?"

"No. His face says."

The bear's face, stitched crooked years before by a manufacturer with either poor quality control or strong artistic convictions, did indeed suggest a private objection to thrust.

Sven crouched in front of Nora's seat and checked the strap. Too tight for comfort, not tight enough for safety. Perfect child work. He loosened it, retucked the bear's trapped arm, and secured the webbing properly.

"Moon-bear is ready."

"What about Opportunity?"

The question came so cleanly that it went through him before he could brace.

Ingrid looked down at her own hands.

Sven kept his face on Nora.

"She's secured in the cargo bay."

"With straps?"

"With straps."

"Good straps?"

"The best ones we have."

Nora considered that. Children, Sven had learned, understood the cruelty hidden in adult qualifiers faster than adults wanted them to.

"Are they good-good straps," she asked, "or Papa says good because we don't have better straps?"

Behind him, Freya made a small sound.

Sven turned. She stood in the corridor with the Northstar packet under one arm, held flat against her side so Nora could not see the pages. Her face was composed again, but not enough to fool anyone who loved her.

Nora looked between them.

Sven chose the truth he could safely give.

"They are good enough for this departure," he said. "And I checked them myself."

Nora nodded. That mattered to her. His hands still counted as reliable in at least one jurisdiction.

"Is *Last Good Haul* sick?"

Freya's fingers tightened once around the packet.

Sven sat back on his heels.

The old freighter hummed around them, warm and uneven. A pressure regulator clicked twice overhead. The forward lights brightened, dimmed, then decided the first answer had been better.

Nora waited.

"She's old," Sven said.

"That's not sick."

"No."

"Grandma is old."

Ingrid lifted an eyebrow. "Brave comparison."

Nora leaned against her harness. "But Grandma is not sick."

"No," Ingrid said. "Grandma is offensively durable."

Sven felt a smile tug at his mouth and let it come, because Nora was watching.

"Ships can be old and still go," he said. "But old ships need more listening."

Nora accepted this with the solemnity of someone who had recently advised a legendary racing craft to be good.

"Are you listening?"

"Yes."

"To *Last Good Haul* or Opportunity?"

"Both."

"Is Mama listening?"

Freya stepped fully into the compartment. She tucked the packet into the side pocket of the emergency kit bag, behind a folded thermal blanket and two ration sleeves. Hidden, but reachable. That was Freya: even concealment organized for access.

"Mama is listening to the papers," Freya said.

Nora made a face. "Papers don't talk."

"They do," Ingrid said. "They just lie more quietly than people."

Freya looked at Ingrid.

Ingrid looked serenely at the wall.

Sven coughed into one hand.

Nora frowned. "Are the papers why we have to go?"

The compartment shrank.

Not physically. It had no room left to shrink physically. It shrank in the way rooms did when adults discovered a child had walked to the edge of the real conversation and was peering down.

Sven glanced at Freya.

Freya gave the smallest nod.

Together, then.

"We have to go because Kestrel Minor isn't working for us anymore," Sven said.

Nora looked at the floor. "Did we break it?"

"No, sweetheart," Freya said immediately. She crossed the compartment and crouched beside Sven, close enough that their shoulders touched. "We did not break it."

"Did Grandpa Lars?"

Sven's breath caught.

Ingrid closed her eyes.

Freya's voice stayed steady. "No. Grandpa Lars did not break it either."

"Then who did?"

The Yard Control comm murmured from the cockpit, too low to make out. A neighboring hauler's thrusters shivered through the deck. Somewhere in the cargo bay, a strap creaked against load.

Sven thought of Northstar's serif font. Mirador's soft voice. Yard clerks who might look away. Lars signing papers with pain in his bones and fury in his hand.

"No one thing," he said.

Nora disliked that. He could see it. Children preferred causes with names.

He tried again.

"Sometimes grown-up problems get stacked badly. Like cargo. One box is heavy, but fine. Then someone puts another heavy box on top, and another, and another, and nobody fixes the stack until it starts to fall."

Nora looked toward the cargo bay. "Can you fix the stack?"

Freya's shoulder pressed against his.

"We're trying," Sven said.

"Is Der Himms-" Nora stopped and wrinkled her nose. "Der Himmelskroog?"

"Der Himmelskrug," Ingrid said.

"Der Himmelskrug," Nora repeated carefully, making the middle syllables into a little mountain range. "Is it where stacks get fixed?"

Sven looked at Freya.

Freya looked tired enough to be honest and stubborn enough not to be cruel.

"It is where there are more people with better tools," she said. "And rules that are supposed to matter."

"Rules matter here," Nora said.

"In theory," Ingrid murmured.

"Ingrid," Freya said.

"What? I am strapped in. I am practically a model citizen."

Nora giggled.

The sound helped.

Sven reached for Nora's harness and checked it properly this time. Shoulder straps flat. Lap restraint snug. Emergency release accessible but not inviting. He had done this a hundred times on small ships. Cargo, crew, children. Secure what matters before motion. Confirm. Reconfirm. Then trust the system you had, because no one got to depart in the system they deserved.

Nora watched his hands.

"Papa?"

"Yes?"

"If the papers are lying, do ships know?"

That was not a fair question.

Freya's face softened and hurt at the same time.

Sven tightened the last strap and sat back.

"Ships know when people lie to them," he said. "They know if you skip a check or fake a reading or pretend a bad sound is normal."

"Do they know paper lies?"

"No," he said. "That part is people's job."

Nora seemed disappointed by this distribution of labor.

"Then you and Mama have to listen to the papers."

"Yes."

"And Grandma listens to me."

Ingrid nodded. "With heroic patience."

"And Opportunity listens to you."

Sven looked toward the corridor, toward the cargo bay where *Opportunity Knocks* sat strapped in darkness with a hesitation in her hands.

"I hope so," he said.

Nora frowned. "You said ships like truth."

"They do."

"Then say a true thing."

Freya did not stop her.

Ingrid did not rescue him.

Sven could have said they were safe. He could have said everything would be all right. He could have said *Last Good Haul* was fine, *Opportunity Knocks* was fine, Northstar was paper, Mirador was far away, Kestrel Minor was behind them, Der Himmelskrug ahead, and fathers knew how to keep the stack from falling.

Instead he put one hand on Nora's knee, light enough that she could move away if she wanted.

"I'm scared," he said. "And I'm listening."

Nora studied him.

Then she reached down, took his thumb in her small hand, and squeezed it once.

"Good," she said. "Now the ship knows."

Freya looked away.

Ingrid looked up at the ceiling as if accusing it of dust.

The cockpit comm snapped clear.

"*Last Good Haul*, Yard Control. Confirm crew secured and ready for clamp release in two minutes."

Two minutes.

The words moved through the compartment like a hand down the spine.

Sven stood.

"Freya?"

Freya rose beside him and wiped both hands down her trousers once. Not nervous. Preparing.

"I'll secure the packet and take the rear jump."

"Ingrid?"

"In harness as soon as someone stops implying I predate safety."

Nora lifted one hand. "Moon-bear is secured."

"Best news all day," Sven said.

He started toward the cockpit, then turned back.

Freya had pulled the Northstar packet from the emergency kit pocket and was sliding it into a flat document sleeve marked MEDICAL / LARS - KEEP. She hesitated before sealing it.

Sven saw the label.

So did Ingrid.

For a second, grief stood in the compartment with them, not dramatic, not new, simply still aboard.

Freya turned the sleeve over so the label faced down and sealed it.

Nora did not notice. She was adjusting the moon-bear's head so it could "see departure."

Sven met Freya's eyes.

She nodded once.

Get us out clean.

He nodded back.

The forward hatch stuck halfway open when he approached. Sven slapped the side panel with two fingers. It opened the rest of the way with a wounded sigh.

Behind him, Nora whispered to the moon-bear, "Don't be scared. Papa is listening."

Sven had one hand on the cockpit hatch when Ingrid said his name.

Not loudly.

Ingrid Andersen had never needed volume to stop a room. She had raised a racer, buried a racer, helped raise the son of one, and negotiated with enough dockmasters, doctors, insurers, and men in clean jackets to understand that force was not the same thing as command.

"Sven."

He turned.

Freya was securing herself into the rear jump seat with the Northstar packet sealed flat against her side, tucked between the emergency kit and the hull. Nora sat beside Ingrid, moon-bear strapped in across from her, both of them facing aft. Nora had one hand on the bear's purple foot and the other wrapped around her own harness.

Ingrid had not finished buckling in.

Of course she had not.

"Ingrid," Freya said.

"I am aware of the concept of straps."

"You are not wearing them."

"I am arranging a meaningful pause."

"Arrange faster."

Nora giggled, but it was nervous now.

The cockpit comm pinged again. Yard Control did not speak this time. Just a soft reminder tone. One minute and closing.

Sven should have gone forward.

He stayed.

Ingrid drew the harness across her lap, then stopped with the buckle in her hand. The motion had uncovered the inside of her left wrist. There, almost hidden among age spots and the pale lines of old kitchen burns, was a small faded tattoo: three dots and a slanting line.

Sven had seen it all his life. As a child, he had thought it was a star map. At twelve, he learned it was the old course mark from Nils Andersen's first sanctioned win. At sixteen, he learned Ingrid had gotten it the same week Nils tried to talk her out of it and failed so badly he paid for the second half.

Lars had hated tattoos.

Lars had also traced that one with his thumb in the hospital when he thought no one was watching.

Ingrid noticed Sven looking and closed her fingers over the buckle.

"She is not your grandfather's ship," Ingrid said.

Sven's throat tightened before he understood why.

"She is not your father's either."

Nora looked up sharply. "She's ours."

Ingrid's face softened. "Yes, little moon. She is ours."

Freya went very still.

Sven let his hand fall from the hatch.

Ingrid clicked the lap buckle into place. Then she began working the shoulder strap with the deliberate patience of someone who knew exactly how much time she was spending.

"Nils used to say *Opportunity Knocks* liked bold hands." She gave a small, dry sound that might have been a laugh if it had been younger. "He said many things when he wanted people to buy him fuel."

Despite himself, Sven smiled.

"Grandpa lied about the ship?"

"Nils embroidered. It is different. More decorative."

Nora whispered to the moon-bear, "Embroidered means fancy lying."

"It means no such thing," Ingrid said, without looking away from Sven.

Freya's mouth twitched. Only once.

Ingrid tightened the shoulder strap across her chest.

"Your father knew better," she said. "Lars never trusted fast hands first. Fast hands only make mistakes happen sooner."

The cockpit reminder tone pinged again.

This time Yard Control came with it.

"*Last Good Haul*, final confirmation required. Crew secured. Clamp release in forty seconds pending your mark."

Sven reached for the cockpit pickup, but Ingrid lifted one finger.

A terrible woman. Always had been.

Sven waited.

Ingrid looked past him, toward the cargo bay. Not because she could see *Opportunity Knocks* from here. Because everyone aboard knew exactly where the racer sat.

"Your father trusted the hand that listened," she said.

The words did not sound like advice.

Advice could be accepted or rejected.

This was inheritance.

Sven felt the cockpit hatch frame under his palm. He felt the freighter's old vibration through the deck plates. He felt, absurdly, the tiny lag in *Opportunity Knocks*' response curve as if it had moved from the maintenance display into his own tendons.

"I don't know if I'm enough for her," he said.

Freya looked at him.

He had not meant to say it. Not there. Not with Yard Control counting down and Northstar in a sealed sleeve and his daughter strapped beside a moon-bear that looked braver than he felt.

Ingrid's gaze did not waver.

"Good," she said.

Sven blinked.

Nora blinked too. "Good?"

"Yes," Ingrid said. "Men who are certain they are enough for ships should be kept away from anything with thrust."

Freya made a small choked sound and hid it badly as a cough.

"That is not a formal safety guideline," Sven said.

"It should be."

The comm crackled.

"*Last Good Haul*, confirm readiness."

Sven lifted the cockpit pickup.

"Yard Control, *Last Good Haul*. Crew securing final straps. Stand by five seconds."

"Five seconds, *Last Good Haul*. Then your slot collapses."

"Understood."

He lowered the pickup.

Ingrid finally clicked her shoulder strap into place and tugged it once, hard, proving to Freya, Yard Control, the universe, and possibly the moon-bear that she understood safety when it became personally convenient.

"There," she said. "Model citizen."

Nora raised one hand. "Grandma is secured."

"Thank you, crew chief," Sven said.

Nora sat taller.

Freya looked at Sven over Nora's head. The Northstar packet was hidden now, but he could still see where it pressed against the emergency kit fabric. Paper pretending not to be a weapon.

Get us out clean.

He nodded once.

Then he looked at Ingrid.

"The hand that listens," he said.

Ingrid leaned back into her harness. Her face was lined, pale with fatigue, and steadier than the ship around her.

"And the mouth that does not brag," she added.

"That part may be harder."

"Then practice under thrust."

Nora giggled again, less nervously this time.

Sven stepped into the cockpit.

Behind him, Freya said, "Hatch."

The cockpit hatch slid halfway closed, stopped, and emitted a low mechanical complaint.

Sven stared at it.

"Not now."

The hatch remained where it was.

From the jump seat, Ingrid said, "Listen to it."

"I am listening to it. It's being rude."

"Perhaps it has unresolved feelings."

Freya said, "The hatch is not joining the family meeting."

Sven pressed the manual assist, eased the rail upward with two fingers, and felt the catch slip properly into the track. The hatch closed the rest of the way with a tired sigh and sealed green.

Not perfect.

Enough.

He sat in the pilot-engineer's chair and pulled the restraint over his chest. The cockpit wrapped around him: cracked armrest, warm console, old flight yoke polished by Lars's hands, his own, and a dozen repair crews who had sworn at it without improving it.

Outside the forward viewport, Kestrel Minor's small-hauler yard stretched in rust colors and warning lights. Load cranes moved like tired insects against the berth glare. Beyond them, route traffic blinked in disciplined lines toward the dark.

Sven set his hands on the controls.

Not fast.

Listening first.

He opened the comm.

"Yard Control, *Last Good Haul* confirms crew secured. Ready for clamp release on your mark."

The berth clamps below them groaned awake.

Behind him, through the closed hatch and the old hull and the freight bay and the straps holding history down, *Opportunity Knocks* waited.

Sven listened.

The berth clamps released like they resented being asked.

First came the low hydraulic groan beneath *Last Good Haul*'s belly. Then the answering shudder through the deck. Then three green indicators lit along Sven's console in a row that should have been reassuring and somehow looked sarcastic.

Clamp One: clear.

Clamp Two: clear.

Clamp Three: delayed.

Sven did not move.

He listened.

The third clamp had always released late in cold weather, high humidity, yard dust, or bad moods. Kestrel Minor's western yard currently had all four.

Behind the cockpit hatch, Nora said something too low to catch. Ingrid answered. Freya did not speak. Sven could feel her silence pressing through the ship more clearly than Yard Control's comm signal.

"Clamp Three," Yard Control said, "shows delayed release. Confirm status."

Sven watched the pressure readout.

Not stuck. Slow.

"*Last Good Haul* confirms delayed release. Holding thrust at zero."

"Window is active. Traffic slot tolerance is narrowing."

"I see it."

"We need clean release or abort."

Sven set his left hand on the stabilizer trim and his right on the manual feed. He did not touch thrust. Not yet.

Clamp Three's indicator flickered from amber to green, then back to amber, like a liar practicing honesty.

"There you are," Sven murmured.

The ship ticked beneath him.

He felt the vibration in the chair frame, not through the console. Lars had taught him that: instruments told you what the ship had already admitted. Structure told you what it was still considering.

The delayed clamp was not holding them.

The freighter was leaning on it.

Old load habit. Slight port sag. Cargo bay mass was heavier than the yard profile expected because the profile did not understand that a legendary racing ship in a cradle was not the same as "general freight."

Sven adjusted the internal balance compensator by half a point.

The deck trembled.

Clamp Three went green.

Yard Control's voice came back. "Clean release. Departure path C-19 to West Spur. Maintain low-angle exit. Do not exceed posted yard thrust until outer marker."

"Copy. Low-angle exit. Yard thrust until outer marker."

"And *Last Good Haul*?"

"Yes?"

"Try not to shed anything expensive."

Sven looked at the ceiling.

"Define expensive."

There was a pause.

Yard Control, to its credit, did not laugh on an official channel.

"Anything we have to file."

"Understood. We will retain all paperwork-generating components."

Freya's voice came through the internal comm from the rear jump seats. "That includes the doors."

"The doors are aspirational."

"Sven."

"Retaining doors."

He eased power into the maneuvering thrusters.

*Last Good Haul* lifted from the berth with the dignity of a chair being dragged across a floor.

Not smooth. Not terrible. Honest.

The small-hauler yard dropped below the forward viewport by centimeters, then meters. Berth C-19 slid away under them: scarred plating, warning paint, the fuel umbilical curling back into its reel, one tired dockhand in an orange vest looking up without interest because people departed badly from Kestrel Minor every day and usually came back poorer.

Sven kept the thrust low.

The freighter wanted more correction than she should have. Her port side dipped, not enough for alarm, enough for memory. The cargo-bay seal readout held amber. The docking-bay power pulse sagged once as the lift load transferred fully to internal systems.

Twelve seconds.

Recover.

Twelve seconds.

Recover.

He counted the rhythm under his breath.

"Papa?" Nora's voice came over the internal channel.

He glanced at the family comm indicator. Freya must have opened it.

"Yes, little moon?"

"Moon-bear says this is not smooth."

"Moon-bear has high standards."

"He says it feels like stairs."

Sven checked the vertical drift and corrected gently.

"Tell Moon-bear we are using the budget stairs."

Nora relayed this with great seriousness.

Ingrid said, faintly, "I object to the budget stairs."

"Objection noted," Sven said.

Freya came on. "How is the seal?"

"Amber and holding."

"That is not a category I love."

"It is a category I am respecting."

"Power?"

"Pulsing on schedule."

"Coolant?"

"Old, annoyed, obedient."

"Pilot?"

Sven almost answered with a joke.

Then he looked at the route overlay, the traffic queue, the warning boundary between yard control and open route management, and the shrinking icon that marked the place where their berth debt had lived until five minutes ago.

"Listening," he said.

Freya did not answer immediately.

"Good," she said.

The freighter cleared the first row of haulers.

Kestrel Minor's western yard opened beneath them like an old machine that had forgotten which parts were supposed to be beautiful. Cargo rails cut across service platforms. Fuel towers leaned behind safety fences patched with newer fences. Small ships waited in crowded rows, some proud, some desperate, some stripped for parts and still registered as "temporarily inactive" because hope was cheaper than disposal.

Sven knew half the yard by sound.

The twin-cylinder tug near Bay Nine had a cracked induction baffle. The red courier with the black nose always overfed her starboard thrusters on lift. The old ore skiff parked three rows over had a pump whine he had offered to fix twice before its owner stopped meeting his eyes because offers became debts when nobody had money.

He was leaving all of it.

Not escaping.

Leaving.

There was a difference, and Freya would make him define it later if he tried to blur the two.

Yard Control guided them into the West Spur departure lane. Traffic lights crawled across the viewport, red to amber to green, each one reflected in scratches on the glass. The freighter's frame complained at the first lateral correction.

Sven softened the angle.

The route overlay updated.

Too soft, and they would drift wide. Too hard, and the cargo-bay seal would feel it. The maneuver was simple. The kind of thing young pilots overcorrected and old pilots stopped noticing. Sven treated it like a negotiation with an exhausted animal.

"*Last Good Haul*, your lateral response is below filed profile," Yard Control said.

"Filed profile is optimistic."

"Current registry lists your lateral response as compliant."

"Current registry also lists our galley hatch as fully functional."

Another pause.

"Is it?"

"No."

"We will pretend this channel remained professional."

"Appreciated."

He fed in a second correction, smaller than the ship wanted and larger than his nerves preferred. The port sag returned, then settled. In the cargo-bay monitor, the cradle restraints around *Opportunity Knocks* took a slight load increase and held steady.

Sven watched that number longer than the others.

Too long.

The coolant pressure drifted.

"Focus," he muttered.

He brought the coolant back into range manually before the warning could chime.

Lars's voice rose in memory, not mystical, not comforting, just irritatingly useful.

One problem at a time, Sven. The ship does not care which problem scares you most.

He checked the seal.

Amber. Holding.

Power.

Sag. Recover.

Cradle.

Load stable.

Coolant.

Manual watch.

Route.

Legal.

He breathed.

The outer yard marker came into view: a ring of blue-white beacons hanging above the last traffic gantry. Beyond it, Kestrel Minor's near-space lanes opened into darker distance, crowded with freighters, shuttles, ore haulers, passenger packets, private couriers, and all the other small acts of motion that kept route towns alive while making most of the people in them tired.

Yard Control said, "*Last Good Haul*, you are cleared to outer marker. Transfer to Route Control after beacon line. Maintain declared limitation: no hard yaw, no high-thrust lateral, monitor bay pressure."

"Copy. No hard yaw, no high-thrust lateral, monitor bay pressure."

"Destination filed as Der Himmelskrug."

"Yes."

"Long run for that frame."

Sven kept his eyes on the marker.

"Yes."

"Any undeclared hazard or cargo risk before transfer?"

Northstar's packet seemed to exist in the cockpit with him for a second, even though Freya had it sealed behind him. *Opportunity Knocks* sat in the bay, declared as stored racing craft. Possible feedback delay, private note, no powered movement. Not a hazard while secured. Not a lie.

"No undeclared hazard," Sven said. "Stored craft secured. Known freighter limitations filed and active."

"Copy."

The blue-white marker slid past the viewport.

For one foolish second, relief rose in Sven so quickly he nearly trusted it.

Then the cargo-bay pressure fluttered.

Tiny.

One amber flicker toward red.

Sven's hand moved before thought. Not fast. Listening. He eased thrust down two percent, adjusted bay pressure compensation manually, and flattened the exit angle. The freighter's protest moved through the deck like a cough held behind teeth.

The pressure warning steadied.

Amber.

Holding.

Behind him, Freya's voice came over the internal comm.

"Sven?"

"Handled."

"What was it?"

"Seal flutter. Still holding."

"Do I want details?"

"Not while strapped in."

"That means yes."

"That means later."

Nora's voice followed, smaller. "Are the budget stairs back?"

"No," Sven said. "That was the ship clearing her throat."

Ingrid said, "Tell the ship to use a handkerchief."

Sven smiled despite the sweat cooling under his collar.

"Grandma says use a handkerchief," Nora reported.

"I'll pass it on."

The cockpit comm shifted tone as Route Control accepted the transfer. The yard channel dropped into background chatter.

Kestrel Minor fell behind them.

It did not shrink dramatically. No cinematic sweep. No final golden flare through the viewport. The yard became one cluster of lights among other clusters, one tired industrial knot in the local dark. A place where Lars had died, where Freya had learned the grammar of threats, where Ingrid had packed her grief into travel bags, where Nora had learned not all grown-up silences meant bedtime.

A place where *Opportunity Knocks* had been hidden in a cargo bay because owning one beautiful thing made people think they were entitled to take it.

Sven kept the thrust conservative until Route Control cleared them into the outbound lane.

Only then did he let himself sit back by two centimeters.

Not relax.

Never that.

Just acknowledge that the first piece had not broken.

Freya opened the internal comm again.

"Clean?"

Sven checked the log.

Departure filed.

Limitations declared.

Crew secured.

No skipped steps.

No evasive behavior.

No story for Northstar except the truth.

"Clean," he said.

The word felt fragile. He said it again anyway.

"Clean departure."

Freya exhaled audibly.

Ingrid murmured something Sven could not catch.

Nora said, "Does that mean we won?"

The cockpit went quiet.

Sven looked out at the outbound route, at the blue guide lines pointing them away from Kestrel Minor and toward a station that might or might not have room for one more family's bad arithmetic.

"No," he said gently. "It means we started properly."

Nora thought about that.

"Is properly good?"

Sven looked at the amber seal indicator, the pulsing power feed, the coolant panel, the cradle load, the declared limitations, the route ahead, and the old freighter carrying his whole family and every remaining thing they could not afford to lose.

"Yes," he said. "Properly is very good."

*Last Good Haul* moved into the lane.

No triumph.

No rescue.

No miracle.

Just a damaged ship, a clean log, and Sven Andersen listening hard enough to keep them moving.

Callum Dresk watched *Last Good Haul* leave Kestrel Minor from a maintenance alcove that smelled of old sealant, hot dust, and somebody else's lunch.

On the monitor in his left hand, the freighter was not a ship.

It was a moving dot.

Small. Amber. Tagged.

He waited until the dot crossed from Yard Control into outbound route authority before he touched the comm stud in his collar.

"Asset is mobile," he said.

Beside him, Ilya Voren leaned against a wall panel marked AUTHORIZED SERVICE ACCESS ONLY and ate a protein strip with the resigned expression of someone punishing both herself and the protein strip.

"Clean departure?" she asked.

Callum glanced at the log feed.

"Clean enough."

"That's irritating."

"Families under pressure often make irritating choices."

"Competent ones do."

The amber dot continued along the outbound lane.

No hard yaw. No evasive thrust. No missed declaration flag that Callum could see from the yard-side commercial feed. No useful panic. No helpful stupidity. No little gift wrapped in procedural misconduct.

Sven Andersen had left like a man who knew someone would inspect the shape of his exit later.

That was going to make the next step less convenient.

Not difficult.

Convenience was not a requirement.

Callum opened the claim note Northstar had pushed to Mirador's field account forty-six minutes earlier. Preservation status disputed. Racing craft identified. Debtor relocation in progress. Field review recommended. Maintain visual or telemetry continuity where lawful and commercially practical.

Lawful and commercially practical had always been a generous phrase.

It could hold a great deal if folded correctly.

Ilya finished the protein strip and crumpled the wrapper into a perfect little square.

"Do they know?" she asked.

"The family?"

"The yard."

Callum looked through the alcove slit toward the departure gantries. Kestrel Minor's western small-hauler yard had resumed being what it was: overbooked, under-maintained, and full of people who had enough trouble of their own to avoid borrowing anyone else's.

"No," he said. "The yard knows a vessel with overdue berth fees departed inside its assigned slot after filing limitations. That is all the yard needs to know."

"And Northstar?"

"Northstar knows the tracker is live."

Ilya's mouth moved in something too small to be called a smile.

The tracker was not large. It did not need to be. It was not military-grade, not station-grade, not any of the dramatic devices that made inspectors excited and prosecutors theatrical. It was a commercial continuity tag with modified reporting behavior, mounted where a tired crew doing departure checks would be least likely to spend time they did not have.

Callum appreciated boring tools.

Boring tools entered reports as misunderstandings.

Dramatic tools entered evidence lockers.

The amber dot passed the outer route marker and held course.

Destination: Der Himmelskrug.

Ilya pushed away from the wall.

"That station complicates collection."

"Yes."

"Founder has a reputation."

"Founders usually do."

"Flight Control has authority there."

"Flight Control has authority everywhere pilots survive long enough to complain about it."

She gave him a look.

Callum closed the Northstar note.

He knew Der Himmelskrug's profile. Everyone in asset protection knew Der Himmelskrug's profile, although most pretended they did not because admitting you had studied a Bavarian orbital beer-stein station for enforcement risk made certain meetings feel less dignified.

Public station. High traffic. Strict approach control. Layered jurisdiction. Security that liked logs. Compliance that liked definitions. A founder with money, political absurdity, and an inconvenient habit of treating dockworkers as people.

Not ideal.

But stations were made of doors, counters, queues, waiting areas, passenger flows, temporary credentials, tired staff, festival crowds, shift changes, and humans who assumed business language meant business authority.

Callum had worked with less.

"We follow by passenger route," he said.

Ilya's eyebrows rose. "Commercial?"

"Fastest clean arrival."

"Under our names?"

Callum looked at her.

She sighed. "I miss simple debtors."

"Simple debtors do not hide valuable racing craft inside failing freighters."

"They also don't make me ride passenger transport."

"Consider it professional variety."

The amber dot shrank as *Last Good Haul* moved deeper into route traffic.

For a moment, Callum imagined the family inside it.

The young engineer at the controls. The wife with the paperwork. The child. The widow. The legendary little ship strapped in the dark like an heirloom pretending not to be collateral.

He did not dislike them.

Dislike was inefficient.

What mattered was claim pressure, disputed reach, access timing, and whether Sven Andersen could be made to understand that possession was not ownership when the other side could afford more patient language.

Ilya stepped to the alcove entrance and checked the corridor.

"Northstar wants seizure?"

"Northstar wants preservation."

"Of course."

"Seizure is an impolite word."

"So is theft."

Callum pocketed the monitor.

"Theft requires clarity. This is why paperwork exists."

Ilya laughed once, without warmth.

A yard worker in a stained orange vest walked past the alcove, saw the service access sign, saw two people who looked as if they knew exactly why they were there, and kept walking.

Callum waited until the worker had turned the corner.

Then he sent Northstar the update.

TRACKER ACTIVE. ASSET IN TRANSIT. DESTINATION CONFIRMED: DER HIMMELSKRUG. FIELD TEAM REPOSITIONING.

The reply came faster than he expected.

MAINTAIN CONTINUITY. AVOID PUBLIC DISPUTE WHERE POSSIBLE. PRESERVATION PRIORITY REMAINS HIGH.

Where possible.

Another generous phrase.

Ilya looked over his shoulder. "They always say avoid public dispute."

"They mean avoid losing public dispute."

"Can we?"

"At Der Himmelskrug?" Callum watched the amber dot until it became one point among many. "That depends on how frightened Andersen is before anyone official starts listening."

Ilya nodded.

Not approval. Not excitement.

Assessment.

Outside the alcove, Kestrel Minor's western yard swallowed *Last Good Haul*'s absence without ceremony. Another ship was already being guided toward Berth C-19. Another crew. Another debt. Another name painted on a hull with more hope than money.

The amber dot kept moving.

Callum closed the monitor.

"Book transport," he said.

Ilya unfolded from the wall.

"To the beer stein."

"To the station," Callum said.

"Same thing."

"No," he said, starting down the corridor. "That is the mistake we do not make."

Behind them, in the route traffic beyond Kestrel Minor, *Last Good Haul* carried the Andersens toward Der Himmelskrug.

The tracker followed.

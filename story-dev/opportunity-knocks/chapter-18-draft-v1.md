# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 18 Draft v1

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## A Place to Stay

Oskar Teague began the meeting by looking at Sven Andersen and saying, “You remain inconvenient.”

Sven, who had spent the walk from Crew Support to the private meeting room imagining at least six ways a formal offer could turn into a mistake, found this oddly calming.

Freya did not.

She sat beside him with her hands folded over a blank slate and gave Oskar the look she used on predatory clauses, unsafe school equipment, and anyone who said “standard practice” without proof.

Ingrid sat on Sven’s other side with moon-bear’s paper crown in her coat pocket because Nora had insisted the bear’s legal representative attend “in spirit,” then been distracted by Learning Center preview materials and stayed with Keisha’s staff aide two doors down.

Across the table sat Oskar, Rolf Petersen, and Keisha Bell.

No Heinz.

No cameras.

No public-feed anything.

No congratulatory banner.

The room had water, chairs, a privacy light, and one small sign near the door that read:

PRIVATE MEETING  
NO MEDIA ACCESS  
NO UNSUPERVISED PASTRY

Sven suspected Keisha had added the last line for morale.

Oskar slid a formal packet across the table.

“Future nuisance classification,” he said.

Freya looked at the packet.

Then at Oskar.

“That is not a category.”

“It is on my floor.”

Rolf Petersen said, “It is not an official Human Resources category.”

Oskar did not look at him. “It should be.”

Keisha smiled without helping either of them.

Sven did not touch the packet yet.

If he touched it, it might become real.

If it became real, he might want it.

Want was dangerous around paperwork. It made people skip lines. Freya had taught him that by example, intimidation, and one unforgettable afternoon with a warranty form.

“What is it?” Sven asked.

Oskar leaned back.

“A probationary senior-track Starship Garage engineering role, high-performance craft diagnostics focus. Onboarding supervised. Authority limited. Pay listed. Review interval listed. Behavioral tolerance narrow.”

Freya reached for the packet before Sven did.

This was correct.

Sven placed both hands in his lap and pretended he had not needed them for anything.

Freya opened the packet.

Rolf spoke while she read.

“The proposed role sits under Starship Garage engineering support, not Flight Control, not Race Ops, not station structural authority. High-performance craft diagnostics, historical repair profiles, pilot-feedback/control-path anomaly reproduction, safed testing, and owner-declared fault review. You would not clear your own ship for race activity. You would not override Garage Ops. You would not make Flight Control decisions. You would not touch Restricted Works authority.”

Oskar added, “You would not romanticize machines on my clock.”

Sven looked at him.

Oskar held the stare.

“Privately, keep a diary. On my floor, describe the fault.”

Sven nodded.

Freya turned a page.

“Probationary.”

“Yes,” Keisha said. “Standard for senior-track external hires, especially when the candidate’s public profile is temporarily absurd.”

“Temporarily?” Ingrid asked.

Keisha considered that.

“Optimistically.”

Freya read another page.

“Family transition support is separate.”

“Yes,” Keisha said. “Housing transition, Learning Center intake, privacy routing, document support, and relocation logistics are not conditional on Sven accepting employment. They are handled through Crew Support and station administration.”

“Declining the job does not affect the title protection for *Opportunity Knocks*.”

“No,” Rolf said.

“Does not affect Northstar restrictions.”

“No,” Keisha said.

“Does not affect investigation status.”

“No,” Oskar said. “Unless he commits a new crime during the meeting.”

Sven blinked. “Why would I commit a crime during the meeting?”

Oskar looked at his hands.

Sven put them flat on the table where everyone could see they were not reaching for tools.

Freya continued reading.

The room waited.

Sven had learned three things about waiting in the last month.

First, waiting did not always mean helplessness.

Second, Freya reading was a safety system.

Third, Oskar Teague was capable of standing perfectly still while somehow radiating disapproval at air.

Freya reached the compensation page and went motionless.

Sven saw the shift.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

“That is not a nothing face.”

“It is a number.”

“That is also not a nothing category.”

She angled the page away from him.

“We will discuss the number after I understand it.”

Oskar made a noise.

Freya looked up.

He stopped.

Rolf said, “The salary reflects senior-track responsibility, hazard-adjacent diagnostic environment, high-performance craft specialization, race-week surge expectation, and probationary review protections.”

Freya read the benefits page.

Then the probation page.

Then the conflict-of-interest page.

Then the section about *Opportunity Knocks*.

Her face tightened.

She read it again.

Then she nodded once.

“That clause is good.”

Sven could no longer stand it.

“What clause?”

She read aloud.

“Employee acknowledges that privately owned craft *Opportunity Knocks* remains Andersen family property. Employee shall not serve as sole diagnostic authority, clearance recommender, or inspection approver for *Opportunity Knocks* in any race, commercial, public display, or powered-movement context where conflict of interest exists. Employee may participate as owner-representative only under Garage Ops, Flight Control, and Compliance-approved boundaries.”

Sven stared at the page.

Oskar said, “You can love your ship or inspect your ship. You cannot be the only person doing both in my house.”

Sven nodded.

“That is fair.”

“It is more than fair. It is survivable.”

Freya made a note.

Then she looked at Oskar.

“Why?”

Oskar blinked once.

“Because my floor is understaffed in exactly the kind of problems that make junior technicians overconfident and senior technicians tired.”

“No,” Freya said. “Why Sven?”

Sven stopped breathing the easy way.

Oskar’s expression became more irritated, which Sven was beginning to understand meant more honest.

“Because he found a fault ordinary intake could have missed.”

Freya did not move.

Oskar continued.

“He declared it before powered movement. He did not hide behind fame, grief, debt, or owner pride. He helped reproduce it without overclaiming. He accepted no as a diagnostic condition. He knew old records mattered but did not mistake family legend for evidence. During the repair, he listened when told to stand behind the line. During the race, Flight Control notes show he listened when the course became expensive. Those are employable traits.”

Rolf added, “His technical instincts are real. Not complete. Not station-certified yet. Real.”

Sven looked down.

The table had become very interesting.

Oskar said, “Also, Garage Ops needs people who can hear old ships without worshipping them.”

Sven looked up.

Oskar’s face remained entirely Oskar.

No softness visible.

No rescue.

No pity.

Just a door, ugly and useful and real.

Sven said, “This is not because I won.”

Oskar snorted. “Winning made you famous. Fame is mostly a spill hazard.”

Rolf said, “Race performance confirmed some observations under live load, but the offer is not a prize supplement.”

Keisha said, “And Crew Support is not paying employment debt with job-shaped sympathy.”

Freya looked at her.

Keisha smiled slightly. “I wrote that sentence for you.”

“I appreciate it.”

Sven breathed out.

The thing he was afraid to want shifted shape.

Not smaller.

Safer.

He looked at the packet.

Then at Freya.

Then Ingrid.

“I cannot answer this alone.”

Oskar made a satisfied grunt.

“Good. You are trainable.”

Freya closed the packet.

“We need to discuss it as a family.”

“Yes,” Keisha said.

“Without anyone waiting outside the door timing our gratitude.”

“Yes.”

“Without public announcement.”

“Yes.”

“Without Heinz finding out before we decide and becoming weather.”

Keisha’s smile widened by a fraction.

“Feli has him contained.”

Rolf looked at Keisha. “Does she?”

“Contained enough.”

Oskar stood.

“Take the packet. Read it. Ask annoying questions. If you bring tools to the next conversation, offer withdrawn.”

Sven almost smiled.

“Understood.”

Oskar pointed at him.

“Also, if you accept, you start by learning my floor’s documentation system.”

Sven’s smile died.

Oskar looked pleased.

“There. Reality.”

The family took the packet back to Crew Support.

No one spoke for the first corridor.

The station moved around them in its usual impossible seriousness: public visitors on the wrong level corrected by polite signage, crew with service carts, a musician arguing quietly with a permit reader, two children wearing race pins and being gently redirected away from a controlled elevator by a staff member who had clearly said the word “no” in three languages already that morning.

Der Himmelskrug did not pause for family decisions.

Freya liked that.

A place that kept working while your life changed was either indifferent or stable.

This one, she was beginning to think, was stable.

In the Crew Support room, Nora was drawing a map of the station with Keisha’s staff aide.

The map included:

- the Garage, labeled PAPA MAYBE WORK.
- the Learning Center, labeled SNACK LAW UNKNOWN.
- Flight Control, labeled VERY NO.
- Communications, labeled FELI SAYS WORDS.
- a large purple region labeled MOON-BEAR EMBASSY.

Freya looked at the last one.

“Moon-bear has an embassy?”

Nora nodded. “For privacy.”

“Of course.”

Sven sat at the table and placed the offer packet in the middle.

Nora looked at it.

“Is that Papa’s job papers?”

Sven blinked.

“How do you know?”

“Adults say ‘meeting’ and then make job faces.”

Ingrid took off her coat.

“Accurate.”

Nora came to the table. “Do we live here now?”

Freya’s answer caught in her throat.

There it was.

Not paperwork.

Not salary.

Not title protection.

The actual question wearing a child’s voice.

Sven looked at Freya.

Freya looked at Ingrid.

Ingrid sat slowly.

“That,” Ingrid said, “is the question.”

Nora climbed into a chair with moon-bear.

“I asked it.”

“Yes,” Ingrid said. “You did.”

Freya opened the packet again.

Not because she needed to read it.

Because paper was easier than hope.

Sven said, “I want the work.”

Freya looked up.

He did not rush.

“I want it,” he said again. “The diagnostics. The old ships. The problems people think are ghosts or owner feelings until they become data. I want to learn how this place does it. I want to be useful here.”

Freya waited.

He added, “And I know wanting is not enough.”

“Good,” she said.

He gave a small, broken smile. “I am learning.”

Freya looked at the packet.

“I want time,” she said.

Sven nodded immediately.

Not perfectly.

But immediately.

“I do not want to turn the race into a job for you, a school for Nora, a kitchen for Ingrid, and another ledger for me before I can breathe.”

“No,” Sven said.

“I do not want to leave because staying scares me. I do not want to stay because leaving scares me.”

Ingrid’s eyes softened.

Freya continued.

“I want Nora safe. I want the ship safe. I want the documents where no one can blur them. I want to sleep several nights without expecting a creditor alert. I want to find out who I am when I am not triaging disaster.”

Sven reached across the table.

Not all the way.

Just enough that she could choose.

Freya took his hand.

Ingrid said, “Wanting a safe table is not weakness.”

Freya laughed once.

It surprised her.

“I know.”

“No,” Ingrid said. “You know it for other people.”

Freya closed her eyes.

That was rude and accurate.

Nora looked between them. “Is the table safe?”

Sven looked down at it.

“Structurally, yes.”

Freya said, “Emotionally pending review.”

Nora nodded. “Moon-bear can inspect.”

Ingrid said, “Then we are saved.”

They read the packet together after lunch.

Every line.

Again.

Nora lasted eight minutes before declaring that job papers had too few pictures and asking whether the Learning Center had engineering snacks.

Keisha arranged the visit for that afternoon.

The Learning Center did not look like a school to Sven at first.

It looked like the station had taken a library, a workshop, a language lab, a quiet room, a map office, and a play area, then asked them to stop arguing long enough to become child-safe.

Nora stopped at the entrance.

Moon-bear stopped with her.

A Learning Center coordinator named Tavi Sorn crouched to Nora’s level without making the mistake of pretending adults were not large.

“Hello, Nora. Keisha said you might want to inspect us.”

Nora nodded seriously.

“This is moon-bear. He has a crown.”

“I see that. Does moon-bear require a visitor badge?”

Nora looked at Freya.

Freya said, “That is a fair question.”

“Yes,” Nora said.

Tavi produced a small temporary badge that read:

VISITOR  
MOON-BEAR  
SUPPORT OBJECT / NO PHOTOGRAPHY

Nora stared at it.

Then looked at Freya.

Then Sven.

Then Ingrid.

Then back at Tavi.

“I like this place.”

Sven put one hand over his mouth.

Freya looked away.

Ingrid nodded to Tavi as if a treaty had been signed.

Tavi gave Nora a short tour.

The quiet corner.

The station map wall.

The multilingual story shelf.

The little workshop with blunt tools and very serious labels.

The snack area.

The family contact station.

The emergency drill board, which Nora approached with suspicion.

“Does Flight Control give detention?” Nora asked.

Tavi said, “No. Flight Control gives instructions.”

“What if you ignore them?”

“Then many adults become educational.”

Nora considered this.

“Do race ships count as homework?”

“Only if your adult agrees and the assignment is not just drawing flames.”

Nora looked disappointed.

Tavi added, “Flames may be included if properly labeled.”

Nora brightened.

Freya watched her daughter move through the room with cautious interest instead of crisis obedience.

It did something complicated behind her ribs.

Sven stood beside her.

“She is checking exits,” he said softly.

“Yes.”

“She likes the map.”

“Yes.”

“She asked about snack law before mathematics.”

“She is our child.”

Ingrid had found the home-and-family board. It listed orientation times, guardian meetings, language support, off-shift meals, sensory accommodations, and privacy policy.

She read it twice.

Then she said, “This place has learned from noise.”

Tavi looked pleased.

“We try.”

Nora returned with a printed intake sheet, two stickers, and a drawing prompt titled MY STATION MAP.

“Can I come back?” she asked.

Freya knelt.

“Do you want to?”

Nora looked at the map wall.

Then at Tavi.

Then at moon-bear’s badge.

“Yes. But not every day yet.”

Tavi said, “That is allowed.”

Nora nodded.

“Good. Moon-bear is busy.”

Freya had to stand before her face gave away too much.

The next meeting was not a job meeting.

That mattered.

Keisha took Freya to a transition office with soft chairs, hard documents, and no assumptions.

Sven was not there. Nora was not there. Ingrid had gone with Nora to return moon-bear’s visitor badge “properly,” which Freya suspected would take as long as Ingrid wanted it to take.

Keisha placed three folders on the table.

“Not employment offers,” she said.

Freya looked at her.

Keisha smiled. “I thought we should establish that before your blood pressure armed itself.”

Freya sat.

“What are they?”

“Options.”

The first folder covered legal cleanup support: business closure records, Andersen Freight archive reconciliation, creditor-monitoring alerts, statement storage, and contact-channel filters.

The second covered family finance planning: reserve protection, housing cost projections, school and support services, medical follow-up routing, and ship-maintenance budgeting.

The third covered future possibilities: station administrative training, business-record consulting, port-services paperwork roles, Guild education sessions, and a note that said no decision required.

Freya stared at the last line.

No decision required.

She read it again.

Keisha waited.

Freya said, “This feels like a trick.”

“I know.”

“That was not an accusation.”

“I know.”

Freya opened the first folder.

Keisha said, “You do not have to become useful immediately to justify being safe here.”

Freya’s hand stopped.

Keisha continued, gentle but firm.

“You have spent a long time proving your family deserved room to stand. You can stop proving for a while.”

Freya looked down.

The folder blurred.

She hated that.

Keisha reached across the table, not touching her, and slid a small cloth packet closer.

“For leaking,” she said.

Freya laughed despite herself.

“That word is becoming household language.”

“I have heard worse household language.”

“I am sure you have.”

Freya took the cloth.

“I don’t know what I want to do.”

“Good.”

Freya looked up.

Keisha’s expression was serious.

“That means no one is currently chasing you fast enough to make every choice obvious.”

Freya pressed the cloth under one eye.

“I hate how helpful that is.”

“Common reaction.”

Freya looked at the folders again.

Legal cleanup.

Finance planning.

Future possibilities.

No decision required.

She closed the third folder and placed her hand on it.

“Not yet.”

Keisha nodded.

“Not yet is a decision.”

“For how long?”

“As long as your family plan and station support review allow. We will put dates on it so it does not become fog. Dates can be moved with reasons.”

Freya breathed.

“Dates with reasons,” she said.

“Very popular here.”

“Good.”

By the end of the day, the Andersens had not moved into a permanent home.

This was also important.

The transitional family quarters had two sleeping rooms, one fold-down work surface, an actual kettle, storage that locked, a wall panel that could show station maps or become blessedly blank, and one stubborn corner that Nora immediately identified as moon-bear’s embassy.

“It has natural borders,” Nora explained.

The corner had one wall and a storage bench.

Ingrid inspected it.

“Defensible.”

Freya carried the secure document case to the lockable wall cabinet.

She placed the title records, lien release, payoff confirmation, protective notice, Sven’s job offer, Nora’s Learning Center intake, and the family statement approval in separate folders.

Then she labeled them.

Sven watched.

“Should I help?”

“No.”

“Because you have a system?”

“Because you will put job papers near ship papers and call it thematic.”

He considered objecting.

Could not.

“Fair.”

Ingrid stood in the kitchenette, deciding where the kettle belonged.

There were moments in a family when power changed hands without election.

This was one.

“The kettle goes here,” Ingrid said.

No one argued.

“Emergency food goes there.”

Nora appeared from the embassy. “What is emergency food?”

“Food that prevents adults from making decisions while hungry,” Ingrid said.

Nora looked at Sven.

“Papa needs that.”

“Yes,” Ingrid said.

Sven said, “I am standing here.”

“We know.”

Freya put the last folder in the cabinet and locked it.

The click was small.

It mattered.

Sven opened one of the storage bins he had carried from Crew Support.

It contained three shirts, a maintenance notebook, two diagnostic styluses, a coil of cable he had promised was not technically ship equipment, and one tool grip he had apparently packed by instinct.

Freya looked into the bin.

“Sven.”

“It is just a grip.”

“Why is it here?”

“It has sentimental value.”

“It has torque settings.”

“Those can be sentimental.”

Ingrid called from the kitchenette, “Ship parts do not go near food.”

“It is not a ship part.”

Freya and Ingrid looked at him.

Sven put the grip back in the bin.

“I will ask where tool habits live.”

Nora emerged carrying a drawing.

It showed the room as a map. The kettle had a crown. The documents had a lock. Moon-bear’s embassy had flags. Sven was labeled PAPA MAYBE WORK. Freya was labeled MAMA PAPERS SAFE. Ingrid was labeled GRANDMA KETTLE LAW.

Freya stared at it.

“Where are you?” she asked.

Nora pointed to a small figure in the middle.

“Here.”

The figure was not hiding.

Freya had to sit down.

Sven saw the drawing and stopped pretending not to leak.

Ingrid put the kettle on.

The room was not home yet.

It smelled too clean. The storage was too empty. The walls had not learned their voices. The station beyond still hummed with unfamiliar rhythms, route chimes, pressure whispers, and distant public laughter.

But the documents were locked.

The kettle had a place.

Nora’s drawing was taped to the wall with approved adhesive.

Moon-bear had borders.

Ingrid looked around and nodded once.

“This room is not home yet,” she said. “But it has accepted inspection.”

Freya leaned into Sven’s shoulder.

He did not say anything.

That was wise.

The next morning, Sven visited *Last Good Haul*.

Not alone.

Freya came with him. Ingrid and Nora came too, because Nora wanted to know whether the old ship knew they had a room now. Keisha arranged the route. Garage Ops approved the visit. Oskar sent a note that said:

DO NOT ROMANTICIZE IN ACTIVE WORK LANES.

Sven folded the note and put it in his pocket.

The freighter sat in a controlled berth, patched, logged, and tired in a more honest way than before.

*Last Good Haul* had survived Kestrel Minor, debt notices, frightened departures, and the indignity of being compared to an old mule considering scripture on an open channel. It had carried *Opportunity Knocks* to Der Himmelskrug with its faults declared, its systems complaining, and its cargo bay full of family history.

Now its panels were sealed properly.

Its cargo-bay door no longer groaned like a legal objection.

Its power instability had been repaired enough for safe limited work.

Its faults were listed clearly in a maintenance packet that would not flatter anyone.

Sven stood at the edge of the berth and felt something pull loose in him.

Not grief exactly.

Not gratitude.

A rope that had been around his ribs for so long he had mistaken it for structure.

Nora stood beside him, holding moon-bear.

“Does it come with us?”

Sven looked at Freya.

Freya looked at the old freighter.

“No,” she said softly.

Nora absorbed this.

“Is it sad?”

Ingrid answered before either of them could make it too complicated.

“Ships do not get sad the way people do. But they can be treated badly or well.”

Nora looked at the freighter.

“We should treat it well.”

“Yes,” Sven said.

Freya opened the maintenance packet on her slate.

“We repair what must be repaired. We disclose everything. We do not hide faults in friendly language. We do not sell panic to someone else because we are relieved to be done with it.”

Sven nodded.

“That will cost more.”

“Yes.”

“We can afford it.”

Freya’s face changed at that sentence.

He realized why after he said it.

They could.

Not infinitely. Not carelessly. Not magically.

But enough to not become what had hurt them.

Ingrid looked at *Last Good Haul*.

“Then we let it have a next life without lying about the old one.”

Nora waved moon-bear’s paw at the freighter.

“Goodbye later,” she said.

Sven smiled.

“Goodbye later?”

“Not now. It is still here.”

“Right.”

From a nearby work lane, Oskar’s voice carried without appearing.

“Correct. Emotional farewell not yet authorized. Ship is still under work order.”

Sven looked up.

“I thought you said not to romanticize in active work lanes.”

“I said do not. I did not say I would be absent.”

Freya muttered, “Of course not.”

Oskar stepped into view with a slate under one arm.

“Freighter packet will be ready for prospective review after remaining repairs and safety declarations. It will not be pretty.”

“Good,” Freya said.

Oskar looked pleased.

“Pretty is how bad ships find fools.”

Sven looked at *Last Good Haul*.

The ship that had been their failing business, their emergency shelter, their shame, their bridge, and their proof of honest declaration sat under station lights, becoming simply a ship again.

Not salvation.

Not burden.

Not home.

A ship.

Safe enough, soon, for someone who knew what they were buying.

Sven put one hand on Nora’s shoulder.

Freya stood beside him with the maintenance packet open.

Ingrid watched the freighter like she was deciding where memory ended and responsibility began.

The next life was not ready yet.

But for the first time, *Last Good Haul* was being prepared for one.

# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 4 Draft v1

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## Arrival Control

Der Himmelskrug appeared first as an argument in Sven Andersen's instruments.

Not a picture.

Not the absurd shining beer stein from the public feed.

A contact stack.

Beacon hierarchy. Approach windows. Traffic lanes. Hold corridors. Emergency exclusion volumes. Solar-array advisories. Public transport arrivals. Cargo tugs. Event-route overlays. Race-prep restrictions. Crew status pings. A cheerful civic festival wrapped in enough traffic law to make a frightened pilot feel either reassured or personally accused.

Sven felt both.

*Last Good Haul* shivered around him as the long outbound corridor handed them toward station approach. The old freighter had performed several hours of stubborn competence, which Sven had learned never to praise aloud. Complimenting a ship with three amber systems was how machinery discovered ambition.

The cargo-bay seal still showed amber.

The docking-bay power still sagged on its twelve-second rhythm.

The coolant routing still believed corners were a philosophical disagreement.

And in the bay, *Opportunity Knocks* remained powered down in her cradle, carrying a possible control-response delay, a family history, and more money in disputed paperwork than Sven wanted within one pressure hull.

Freya sat strapped behind him with her slate open and silent.

Ingrid sat beside Nora, who had been awake for forty-two minutes and had used thirty-six of them to ask whether the big station would look more like a cup, a house, or a cake.

"It will look like a station," Sven had said.

"That is not one of the choices," Nora had replied.

Now Der Himmelskrug's first traffic beacon locked properly.

The cockpit display cleaned itself into sharper lines.

Sven's throat went dry.

Freya did not touch him. She did not say be honest or do not minimize or remember the list.

She did not need to.

The list sat open on the side console.

DER HIMMELSKRUG / REVIEW

- Arrive clean.
- Declare freighter limitations.
- Declare stored racing craft.
- Declare possible control-response lag before any powered movement.
- Request independent Starship Garage diagnostic.
- No powered release from cradle before safety signoff.
- No race registration before ship safety, pilot eligibility, and Flight Control clearance.
- No shame decisions.
- Tell the truth around the ship.

Sven opened the approach channel.

"Der Himmelskrug Flight Control, this is freighter *Last Good Haul*, Kestrel Minor registry KM-seven-one-nine, inbound on assigned civilian corridor Blue-Knot Fourteen, requesting approach review and controlled intake."

Static breathed once.

Then a voice answered, crisp and awake.

"*Last Good Haul*, Der Himmelskrug Flight Control. We have you on Blue-Knot Fourteen. Transponder matches preliminary route record. Confirm shipmaster."

Sven swallowed.

"Shipmaster Sven Andersen."

Behind him, Nora whispered, "That is Papa."

Ingrid whispered back, "Flight Control knows."

The controller continued as if five-year-old commentary was not part of the official channel, which was generous.

"Shipmaster Andersen, confirm souls aboard."

"Four. Sven Andersen, Freya Andersen, Ingrid Andersen, Nora Andersen. Family crew/passengers."

"Medical emergency?"

"No."

"Hazardous cargo?"

"No declared hazardous cargo."

"Undeclared cargo?"

"No."

"Restricted cargo, contaminated material, artifact-adjacent material, ordnance, active weapons systems, dirty repair request, quarantine concern, or unknown salvage?"

"No."

Freya's stylus stopped moving.

Sven looked at the cargo-bay status.

Now.

Say it before the station had to ask harder.

"We do have a stored racing craft in the cargo bay," he said. "Powered down, secured in cradle. Clean hull history as far as current records show. No active weapons. No ordnance. No hazardous cargo attached. We are requesting clean-side Starship Garage intake, but with handling restrictions."

There was a short pause.

Not suspicious.

Administrative.

The kind of pause where systems opened doors inside systems.

"Copy stored racing craft," Flight Control said. "Craft identifier?"

Sven felt the old instinct rise.

Do not say the name on an open channel.

Do not let the route net gossip before the ship was safe.

Do not make the cockpit fill with Nils and Lars and every person who had ever said *Opportunity Knocks* like it was not a machine that could kill someone if handled badly.

He breathed once.

"Craft identifier available in attached sealed manifest," he said. "Request operational privacy on public-facing channels. Racing craft name has public recognition and we are not requesting public status at this time."

Freya's stylus moved once.

Not a warning.

A mark.

Flight Control did not laugh.

Flight Control did not ask whether he was famous.

Flight Control did not become curious.

"Operational privacy request noted," the controller said. "Sealed manifest received. Flight Control and Garage Ops access only pending intake status. Be advised: privacy request does not limit safety review, inspection authority, Compliance review, Security access on cause, or race eligibility requirements if later requested."

Sven closed his eyes for half a second.

The first clean no.

A good no.

"Understood and accepted," he said.

"State handling restrictions."

He looked at the list.

Then past it, at the cargo-bay monitor.

"Stored racing craft should not be powered, released from cradle, towed under its own systems, or moved through standard handling without independent diagnostic review. Suspected low-power control-response delay through pilot-feedback interface or related control path. I felt late response on manual trim during maintenance check. Not yet confirmed by instrumentation."

Another pause.

This one was different.

Not alarm.

Attention.

"Repeat suspected control-response delay," Flight Control said.

"Suspected," Sven said. "Not proven. I may be wrong. But I am declaring it before movement."

"Good," said the controller.

Only that.

One word.

Sven's hand tightened on the edge of the console.

Freya looked down at her slate.

Ingrid's humming stopped.

Nora whispered, "Is good good?"

Ingrid whispered, "Sometimes good means the right problem arrived at the right desk."

Flight Control continued.

"*Last Good Haul*, list freighter limitations."

Sven almost smiled.

The station had accepted the large frightening truth and moved directly to the next problem.

He could work with that.

"Cargo-bay seal wear, currently amber but stable. Docking-bay power instability on twelve-second sag cycle, recovers without manual reset. Old coolant routing with drift under trim correction. Outdated maintenance records for aft utility loop. Rough handling cradle installed for stored racing craft. Freighter maneuvering response within conservative tolerance but not pretty."

"Define not pretty."

Sven stared at the console.

Freya covered her mouth.

Ingrid made a small sound that could have been age or laughter.

"Correction lag at low thrust feels like an old mule considering scripture," Sven said before he could stop himself.

Silence.

Then Flight Control said, "Please translate that into operational language."

"Yes. Sorry. Minor delayed response in lateral trim under low thrust, correctable, predictable, not worsening over last two hours."

"Copy. Keep poetry off the active channel unless requested."

"Yes, Flight Control."

Nora whispered, "Papa got traffic-controlled."

Freya whispered, "Moon-bear saw nothing."

A second voice entered the channel, older, lower, and carrying the sort of calm that made Sven sit straighter.

"*Last Good Haul*, this is Himmelskrug Approach Supervisor. You are not cleared for standard public dock approach. Maintain Blue-Knot Fourteen at current velocity. Reduce thrust by four percent at marker nine. Expect controlled lower-handle clean-side garage-adjacent intake pending berth confirmation."

Sven's stomach dropped.

Lower handle.

Starship Garage side.

Not public arrival.

Not a tourist dock.

Not shame, he told himself.

Routing.

The supervisor continued.

"Your family passenger clearance will be handled through Arrival Control after docking and safety review. Stored racing craft remains secured until Garage Ops accepts handling responsibility. No powered movement. No public reveal. No race registration action from this channel. Confirm."

Sven's voice came out steadier than he felt.

"Confirmed. Maintain Blue-Knot Fourteen. Reduce thrust four percent at marker nine. Controlled lower-handle clean-side garage-adjacent intake pending berth. Family passenger clearance through Arrival Control after docking and safety review. Stored craft secured until Garage Ops accepts handling. No powered movement. No public reveal. No race registration action."

"Good readback."

Good again.

A station could be kind with no softness at all.

The channel handed him a new approach overlay.

It was narrow.

Not dangerous narrow. Professional narrow. The kind of narrow that said: we believe you enough to keep you away from people until we know more.

Sven followed it.

Der Himmelskrug grew.

At first the station looked like scale pretending to be shape. Then the shape became undeniable. The Crown rim caught light above the public body. The handle swept back in a pressure-rated arc, not decorative at all now that the working overlays showed traffic flowing through its separated upper and lower access. Radiators and utility trusses glinted along the working side. Lower restricted doors sat dark and serious beneath the public levels, not inviting anyone to ask whether the beer stein had a basement.

Nora breathed in loudly.

"It is a cup," she said.

"Yes," Sven said.

"It is also not a cup."

"Yes."

"That is confusing."

"Yes."

Freya leaned slightly forward against her harness. "That is the station."

Ingrid looked past Sven at the bright public bands and the hidden working geometry around them.

"No," she said softly. "That is a joke that survived because enough people took it seriously."

Sven could not answer.

He was watching approach markers.

Blue-Knot Fourteen narrowed and bent under Flight Control instruction. Public passenger traffic stayed above and outward, moving toward upper-handle arrival. Cargo traffic held elsewhere. Clean-side ship movement ghosted through lower overlay lanes. Restricted-side doors remained far below, not for them, because he had declared enough for Flight Control to decide they were complicated but not dirty.

Probably fine goes below, the old mechanic in his head muttered.

Not today, Sven thought back. Today we were honest early.

The cockpit pinged.

"*Last Good Haul*, reduce thrust four percent at marker nine."

"Reducing four percent."

The freighter complained.

The power sag deepened for one cycle, recovered on the second, and made a noise like an elderly cupboard objecting to stairs.

"Power sag increased and recovered," Sven said. "No manual reset."

"Logged," Flight Control said. "Continue."

No panic.

No scolding.

No sentimental encouragement.

Just logged.

Freya's hand appeared beside his seat, not touching his arm, just close enough that he could see it.

He did not take it.

He was flying.

But he saw it.

The lower handle grew across the forward display. It did not resemble the public feed's welcoming arrival glamour. It looked like infrastructure: pressure collars, guide lights, traffic teeth, service beacons, utility lines, clean-side separation markers, drone rails, and a row of signs that used fewer verbs than Feli's public announcements because pilots were expected to know when a door meant no.

A new voice came on the channel.

"*Last Good Haul*, Garage Ops intake liaison. We have your sealed manifest and handling restriction note. Stored racing craft remains in cradle. We are preparing passive tow cradle and no-power inspection path. Do not cycle cargo-bay power without instruction."

Sven stared at the console.

Passive tow cradle.

No-power inspection path.

They had believed the problem enough to prepare for it before seeing the ship.

"Confirmed," he said, and hated that his voice nearly broke. "No cargo-bay power cycle without instruction."

"Berth collar will take you at conservative capture. Expect hard seal confirmation before family movement. Do you have anyone aboard requiring mobility assistance?"

Sven glanced back.

Ingrid raised one eyebrow at him.

He chose survival.

"No required mobility assistance," he said. "But one elderly passenger may punish anyone who assumes incorrectly."

Ingrid nodded.

Garage Ops paused.

"Logged as: passenger ambulatory, prefers direct address."

Freya made a small, helpless sound.

Nora asked, "Grandma, are you elderly?"

"In dock forms only," Ingrid said.

The lower-handle collar opened ahead.

Not physically like a mouth. Sven disliked that his mind supplied the comparison anyway. It opened in layers of light and permission: outer approach gate, velocity match, collar alignment, utility lock standby, emergency tug standby, passive cradle team staging.

The old freighter moved into the guidance field.

Sven felt every delayed correction.

Not bad.

Not worsening.

Not pretty.

He did not overcorrect.

"*Last Good Haul*, hold current input. Let collar assist."

Sven held.

The ship drifted the last meters into capture.

There was a soft mechanical contact through the hull.

A pressure shift.

A low clunk somewhere aft.

A flicker in the cargo-bay power readout that made Sven's heart jump until the station collar stabilized the line externally.

"Docking contact," Flight Control said. "Hard seal pending."

Sven did not breathe properly until the indicator turned green.

"Hardside seal confirmed," Garage Ops said. "Station power isolated. Freighter power remains internal. Cargo-bay remains no-cycle. Crew and passenger hold until Arrival Control clearance path is assigned."

Sven sat back.

Not far.

Just enough for the harness to catch his weight.

Freya touched his shoulder then.

One brief contact.

"You did it clean," she said.

He looked at the console.

"No," he said. "We arrived clean. That is all."

"That is not all."

"It is enough for this minute."

Ingrid said, "Useful family word."

Nora unbuckled one finger and immediately got it stuck in the harness.

"Am I allowed to see the cup now?"

"No," Sven, Freya, and Ingrid said together.

Nora considered this.

"After paperwork?"

Sven looked at Der Himmelskrug's dock status board, at the green seal, at the amber cargo bay, at the stored craft note now logged inside station systems.

"Yes," he said. "After paperwork."

Far above and around the curve of the same impossible station, BrightSpan Local Passenger Shuttle 88 docked at upper-handle public arrival.

Callum Dresk waited for the line to move.

He disliked passenger lines.

Not because they were slow. Slow could be useful. Slow gave a person time to watch staff rhythms, scanner placement, gate behavior, argument tolerance, family distractions, signage weakness, and which passengers thought rules became optional if they smiled.

He disliked passenger lines because everyone in them believed waiting made them innocent.

Ilya Voren stood three passengers behind him, reading a public safety notice with the mild expression she used when memorizing exits.

They did not look at each other often.

That was habit.

The passenger corridor from the upper handle was clean, bright, and warmer than Callum preferred. Not warm in temperature. Warm in intention. Der Himmelskrug had decided arrivals should feel welcomed after they had been controlled, and therefore the corridor had soft light, good air, clear signage, and wall panels that displayed the station's public levels in cheerful colors.

Observation.

Entertainment.

Hospitality Level 1.

Hospitality Level 2.

Retail.

Below Retail, the display changed tone.

Residential: restricted.

Services: authorized crew, staff, pilot, operational support only.

Starship Garage: controlled ship access only.

Restricted Works: restricted hazardous operations.

No casual descent.

No public shortcut.

No behind-the-beer miracle.

Callum had to admit the signage was good.

That irritated him too.

The line advanced toward Arrival Control.

Arrival Control was not a single desk. It was a process made architectural. Passenger lanes widened into secured arrival zones. Screens separated families from single travelers, residents from visitors, crew from tourists, complicated from simple. Staff stood where staff needed to stand, not where a brochure would have placed them. Security was visible enough to calm the obedient and disappoint the theatrical.

A friendly sign above one arch read:

CLEAR SECURITY. WASH OFF SPACE. ASK BEFORE TRANSLATING GERMAN. DRINK LATER.

The crowd liked it.

Callum did not.

Jokes that gave instructions were still instructions.

He reached the first scanner.

The attendant looked at his documents.

"Marek Sova?"

"Yes."

"Origin?"

"Kestrel Minor outer transit."

"Purpose of visit?"

"The Run."

The attendant's smile did not change. "First time to Der Himmelskrug?"

"Yes."

"Welcome. Step into the identity field, please."

Callum stepped.

The field read his face, stance, public identity, ticket, origin packet, customs declaration, luggage tag, and enough other things that people who believed in privacy would have become educational.

Marek Sova held.

Boring man.

Good.

"No weapons declared," the attendant said.

"No weapons."

"No restricted tools?"

"No."

"No hazardous goods, contaminated goods, salvage, artifact-adjacent material, restricted plant or chemical material, professional cargo, medical import, or third-party delivery?"

"No."

"Any business purpose beyond public festival attendance?"

"No."

The lie settled neatly because it was not the lie they cared about yet.

The attendant nodded him onward.

"Proceed to decontamination mist and customs confirmation. Public release will place you on Hospitality Level 1. Public scenic elevators serve Observation through Retail only. Follow signs for event viewing. Do not attempt to access crew, Services, garage, residential, command, medical, or restricted levels without authorization."

Callum gave the smile ordinary people gave when they wanted staff to know they were harmless and mildly amused.

"Understood."

He moved on.

Three passengers behind him, Ilya became Tilen Voss.

Callum did not watch directly.

He saw enough in reflection.

Ilya stepped into the identity field with the resigned patience of someone who had chosen a false identity that collected race pins and was prepared to suffer for operational needs.

"Tilen Voss?"

"Yes."

"Purpose of visit?"

"Race viewing," Ilya said. "And possibly a historical heat-exchanger exhibit, if one exists."

The attendant blinked.

Callum kept moving.

Ilya was either improvising or punishing him.

Possibly both.

The attendant recovered. "Museum programming can be found on station public listings after release. Any restricted tools?"

"No."

"Weapons?"

"No."

"Hazardous goods?"

"No."

"Professional cargo or contractor purpose?"

"No."

"Enjoy your visit."

"I intend to behave memorably only in retail."

That was unnecessary.

But harmless.

The attendant laughed, which was useful and therefore irritating.

Callum cleared decontamination mist. It smelled faintly of citrus, metal, and institutional optimism. Customs confirmation took his bag scan, found clothing, toiletries, a public race scarf he had purchased because Ilya had said cover identities required sacrifice, and a data slate clean enough to insult the profession.

He was released through the public gate into a controlled entrance corridor.

Not directly into the taproom.

Not directly into chaos.

Corridor first.

Then threshold.

Then Hospitality Level 1 opened ahead.

Der Himmelskrug did not reveal itself all at once.

It staged the arrival.

The corridor curved gently, sound gathering before the view arrived: crowd noise softened by acoustic control, a distant announcement, laughter, the clink of service trays, a warm undercurrent of music, the hush of bubble elevators moving somewhere beyond the wall, and beneath it all the station's mechanical breath.

Then the corridor opened.

Hospitality Level 1 spread around the public atrium in warm light and controlled spectacle. Across the atrium, the main taproom occupied its quadrant with the confidence of an institution that had been both warned about and built anyway. Above and below, public levels ringed the void: Observation high and bright, Entertainment busy with event signage, Hospitality 2 louder in food-hall colors, Retail lower down with market movement and festival displays.

The bubble elevators climbed and descended around the atrium perimeter like transparent thoughts.

They did not go below Retail.

Callum noticed.

So did Ilya, when she emerged two minutes later and paused at a public map as if deciding where to buy an ugly pin.

She did not approach him.

Good.

Callum drifted toward an event information pillar and opened the public map.

Himmelskrug Run viewing zones.

Hospitality.

Retail.

Guest house.

Public restrooms.

Press suite.

Festival merchandise.

Crew services did not appear as a tourist destination.

Starship Garage did not appear at all except as a greyed controlled function with a polite warning: AUTHORIZED CREW AND OPERATIONAL ACCESS ONLY.

Services showed as a controlled threshold below public access, visible only as an icon for directed crew and pilot support.

No route.

No casual lift.

No public button.

Bad terrain.

Good to confirm.

Callum looked for *Last Good Haul* in public arrivals.

Nothing.

He had not expected it.

Freighters did not enter through the public corridor like tourists with bad hats. If Andersen had declared properly, the ship would be somewhere lower, controlled, logged, and already harder to touch.

He checked the tracker through a screened glance at his slate.

The mark had stopped moving.

Docked.

Lower vector.

Not public handle.

Callum closed the slate.

Ilya passed him without stopping, close enough to speak under the noise of arriving passengers.

"Lower handle," she said.

"Clean-side?"

"Likely."

"Family release?"

"Pending."

"Then we wait where waiting is normal."

She continued toward a souvenir kiosk displaying course pins.

Callum watched her pick up one shaped like Kaltmond and hold it with the expression of a woman considering whether cover work justified humiliation.

It did.

She bought two.

Callum turned back toward the public flow.

Somewhere below the public warmth, Sven Andersen and his family were being processed by a station that liked forms, doors, and honest problems.

Somewhere below that, *Opportunity Knocks* sat in the dark, no longer just inside a freighter, now inside a system.

That system had accepted the ship before Callum reached the taproom.

Not good.

But not finished.

Systems had thresholds. People crossed them tired. People misunderstood what protection meant before they trusted it. People left family members to find bathrooms, food, forms, crew counters, status boards, and quiet corners. People believed a controlled station made them safe from old debts because safe places felt different from dangerous ones.

Callum had built a career on the distance between feeling safe and being protected.

A public announcement chimed overhead, cheerful and clear.

"Welcome, Himmelskrug Run visitors. Public viewing information is available on your slate and at event pillars. Please remember: if a door, railing, hatch, lift, service corridor, crew gate, or staff member says no, that means no."

Around him, visitors laughed.

Callum smiled with them.

Below the public levels, a ship that had declared its damage waited in a controlled berth.

Above it, two people who had declared nothing walked into the crowd.

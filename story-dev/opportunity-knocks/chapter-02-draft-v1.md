## The Announcement

Der Himmelskrug looked least dignified from the camera angles the public loved best.

This was not the camera crew's fault. It was difficult to make a Bavarian beer stein the size of a city look restrained, especially when the morning feed caught the gold rim of the Crown Level and turned the whole station into something between a royal monument, a docking hub, and a dare issued by a brewer with infrastructure funding.

Heinz Schmitt loved those angles.

He was not allowed to choose them.

"Absolutely not," Feli said.

Heinz lowered the preview slate by three centimeters. "You have not even looked at the third rotation."

"I looked at the first two."

"The third has more dignity."

"The third makes the station look as if it is winking."

Heinz raised the slate again and squinted at the frozen image. Der Himmelskrug's curved handle swept behind the main body in a clean structural arc. Docking lights glittered along the upper approach lanes. The Crown Level caught enough light to suggest grandeur without implying monarchy, which was a line Heinz had been asked not to cross in public messaging since the Lent Crown Napkin Incident.

"It is a confident civic expression," he said.

"It is a wink."

"Can infrastructure not have charisma?"

"Not in the first eight seconds of a safety announcement."

Across the Crown Admin conference table, Amara Reid did not look up from the Flight Control packet. "If the station winks, I am cancelling the race."

Heinz set the slate down.

"Temporarily postponing," he said.

"Cancelling."

The table went quiet in the particular way rooms did when Flight Control used a complete sentence.

The announcement suite had been built for exactly this kind of meeting: large enough for the Governing Board, Communications, Flight Control, Traffic Safety, Security, Port Services, Compliance, media liaison, race operations, emergency routing, staff representation, and one station founder who had promised, in writing, not to improvise.

The wall behind them showed a silent exterior feed of Der Himmelskrug hanging at The Knot, bright and busy and ridiculous in the dark.

Around it moved traffic.

That was the part Heinz loved more than the camera angles, though he tried not to say so too often because people assumed he meant romance when he meant routing. Cargo haulers slipped through assigned lanes with the stubborn grace of working animals. Passenger shuttles crossed above them in timed arcs. Maintenance drones moved where no public camera would linger. Emergency corridors glowed faintly on the operational overlay, not because anyone was using them, but because no festival, however beloved, got to exist unless somebody had protected the routes people would need if the beloved thing caught fire.

Amara tapped one of those glowing corridors on her display.

"The announcement script still says spectators will have a 'front-row view of the most thrilling approach run in settled space.'"

Feli closed her eyes.

Heinz looked at the ceiling.

Anja Reiter, present for the Board's New Bavaria seat and wearing the expression of a woman who had heard both the song and the liability, folded her hands on the table. "That phrase did test well with exactly the sort of people who later ask whether warning railings are legally binding."

"They are," Janelle Okonkwo said.

"I know," Anja said. "That is why I dislike the phrase."

Amara continued, "No one has a front-row view of an approach run. No public viewing area is in an approach run. No sponsor platform, media pod, hospitality balcony, festival gantry, commemorative pretzel stand, or person with a premium wristband is in an approach run."

Mara Venn, Compliance and Customs Integrity, made a note. "Please keep 'commemorative pretzel stand' in the minutes."

"No," Feli said.

"Yes," Mara said.

Heinz leaned toward Feli. "Could there be commemorative pretzels?"

"No."

"Not in the approach run," Amara said.

"Obviously not in the approach run."

"No commemorative pretzels," Feli repeated.

Rolf Petersen, who had attended under protest because Engineering had been asked to sign off on three temporary display pylons and one decorative banner anchor he considered emotionally unstable, lifted one hand. "If a pretzel requires a mounting bracket, I am leaving."

"It would be handheld," Heinz said.

Rolf pointed at him. "That is how it starts."

Greta Mahlberg, Guild Chair and Station Staff Representative, looked up from the labor-impact notes. "If it is handheld, my next question is who cleans mustard off the public lift controls when tourists discover momentum."

Keisha Bell nodded. "Crew Support seconds the mustard concern."

Feli looked at her agenda and inhaled through her nose.

"Returning to the announcement," she said, with the strained calm of a woman pulling a public statement out of a machinery fire. "The phrase has already been replaced. New language: 'public viewing zones will offer safe, authorized sightlines to selected race sectors, with all access controlled by station event staff and Flight Control routing.'"

Amara read it twice.

"Acceptable."

Anja nodded. "Less singable. More survivable."

"That is our festival communications motto now," Mara said.

"It is not," Feli said.

Heinz made a note on his slate.

Feli looked at him.

He stopped writing.

Keisha raised her hand slightly. "Add plain-language wayfinding. Visitors will hear 'safe authorized sightlines' and still ask whether they can get closer."

"Agreed," Feli said. "We'll add: 'If a door, railing, hatch, lift, service corridor, crew gate, or staff member says no, that means no.'"

Janelle nodded. "Please make 'staff member' bold."

"This is spoken announcement copy," Feli said.

"Then speak it bold."

Heinz made another note.

Feli took the slate out of his hand.

The Himmelskrug Run had begun, like many station traditions, as a terrible idea with excellent paperwork.

The first proposal had come from pilots who claimed they only wanted a "small celebratory circuit" around The Knot after the station's first full year of public operations. Flight Control had responded by asking whether the pilots meant a sanctioned race, an unsanctioned hazard, or a confession. The pilots had chosen sanctioned race very quickly. Since then, the Run had become an annual civic festival, route-safety demonstration, media event, repair-season fundraiser, hospitality boom, sponsor negotiation, pilot argument, and proof that Der Himmelskrug could take something deeply silly and make it lawful enough to survive.

Heinz was proud of that.

He was also banned from saying "deeply silly and lawful enough to survive" in the announcement.

Feli had underlined the ban.

Twice.

On the main wall, the exterior feed shifted to a course map.

The Knotward Run appeared in clean lines: launch corridor, opening chicanes, outer arc, Kaltmond slingshot, Kaltmond exit chicane, Twins' Teeth, Teeth-to-Gauntlet transition, The Gauntlet, final acceleration recovery, The Last Pour, finish.

Even silent, the map changed the room.

People sat differently when it appeared. Not dramatically. Not like myth. Like professionals whose work had suddenly acquired consequences with names.

Amara stood.

"Course authority review," she said.

Heinz folded his hands.

Not because he wanted to.

Because Feli had told him his resting excitement looked like he was about to buy something expensive.

Amara enlarged the launch corridor.

"The Run is sanctioned inside temporary Flight Control race authority windows only. Outside those windows, it is traffic space. Not practice space. Not sponsor demonstration space. Not 'just one quick calibration loop' space."

Rolf muttered, "That one should be engraved."

Greta glanced at him. "On what?"

"On whatever the first person ignores."

"Too expensive."

"Then on the incident report."

Amara moved to the opening chicanes.

"Opening chicanes are designed to separate pilots by control discipline before they reach high-speed sections. They are not decoration. They are not a place to prove your childhood instructor was wrong."

Heinz nodded gravely, though his childhood instructor had been wrong about several things.

"Kaltmond," Amara said.

The barren moon appeared on the overlay, small and gray and indifferent.

"Gravity-assisted slingshot corridor is narrow by design and bounded by exclusion markers. Too shallow, you lose the boost and the race line. Too wide, you leave the course. Too deep, you become an incident report with grieving relatives. We will not soften that language for pilots."

Feli glanced at her script. "The public version currently says, 'too deep, you risk catastrophic contact with terrain.'"

"Fine."

"Could we say 'moon' instead of terrain?" Heinz asked.

Amara looked at him.

"For clarity," he said.

Mara murmured, "Terrain is legally broader."

"Terrain it is," Heinz said.

Amara advanced the map.

"The Twins' Teeth require controlled yaw and roll through angled offset gates. They are not a straight squeeze. Media graphics must not imply that they are a straight squeeze."

Ren Calder, primary race commentator, looked personally offended on behalf of graphics. "The graphics team already corrected that."

"They corrected one angle."

"They will correct all angles."

"Before the public feed?"

"Yes."

"Before sponsor preview?"

Ren hesitated.

Amara's eyebrow moved.

"Yes," Ren said.

Maude Kess, retired racer and technical analyst, leaned back in her chair. "Good. If you show the Teeth as a straight gap, half the amateurs watching will think bravery is the missing component."

"It isn't?" Heinz asked.

Maude smiled without kindness. "Bravery is what bad pilots call geometry after they stop understanding it."

Heinz reached for his slate.

Feli moved it farther away.

"That was Maude," Heinz said.

"No borrowing Maude to improvise."

Maude looked pleased.

Amara moved to The Gauntlet.

"The Gauntlet remains under live Flight Control telemetry oversight. Every registered racer must accept remote abort authority. Refusal means no start clearance. Interference with telemetry means disqualification and possible detention, depending on what they broke while being educational."

Janelle nodded again. "Security supports educational detention."

"Security supports lawful detention," Mara said.

"Lawful educational detention."

"Janelle."

"Fine."

The Last Pour appeared on the map: a final kink on approach, visually elegant and operationally mean.

Heinz loved The Last Pour. Everyone loved The Last Pour except pilots who had entered too hot, spectators who did not understand why the prettiest part made the finish less predictable, and one sponsor who had once suggested renaming it the GoldArc Premium Finish Sector. That sponsor had not been invited back to naming conversations.

"The Last Pour is unchanged," Amara said. "Final kink remains inside controlled race corridor. No active weapons. No hyperspace. No deliberate contact. No sensor spoofing. No unregistered boost systems. No sponsor equipment on hull surfaces unless cleared by Engineering and Race Operations. No exceptions for legacy craft, celebrity pilots, Board guests, historic winners, or anyone Heinz met at breakfast."

Heinz looked up. "That last one feels personal."

"It became policy because of you."

"It was one retired shuttle champion."

"It was three retired shuttle champions and a man who claimed to be a shuttle champion because he had once stolen a shuttle."

"In my defense, breakfast was crowded."

Feli put both hands over her face.

Rolf leaned toward Keisha. "If there are commemorative pretzels, I want one shaped like an incident report."

Keisha whispered back, "That is just a rectangle."

"Exactly."

Feli dropped her hands. "All right. Prize purse language."

The room changed again.

Not much. But enough.

Money did that.

The Board-approved purse had required six months of sponsor commitments, event reserve allocation, media licensing, hospitality projections, insurance review, labor-impact review, and one meeting in which Heinz had described the proposed first prize as "life-altering in an attractively irresponsible way" and been asked to leave his own sentence.

Feli read from the final copy.

"'This year's Himmelskrug Run will award twenty-five million IFP Standard Credits for first place, twenty million for second, and fifteen million for third, funded through the Board-approved event reserve, sponsor commitments, and licensed media distribution. Sponsor participation does not alter race authority, safety rules, pilot eligibility, Flight Control authority, or emergency routing.'"

Mara nodded. "Good."

Amara nodded. "Good."

Greta looked at the staff-impact column. "Add that sponsor participation also does not alter worker safety requirements or festival surge staffing limits."

Feli checked the release notes. "Already in the operational bulletin."

"Public copy?"

"In the public FAQ."

Greta waited.

Feli added, "And I'll put it in the spoken briefing for vendors."

Greta nodded. "Good."

Heinz waited.

Feli did not look at him.

Heinz waited harder.

"No," Feli said.

"I have said nothing."

"You are about to ask whether 'life-altering' can go back in."

"It is accurate."

"It is bait."

"It is also accurate."

Keisha tapped her stylus. "For some pilots, it will be life-altering. For most, it will be a reason to do math badly."

"Then we should encourage good math," Heinz said.

"That is not a festival slogan," Feli said.

"It could be."

"It could not."

Ren lifted a hand. "Broadcast can frame it as major purse, major responsibility. We can say the prize is large enough to change lives, but no prize changes physics."

Maude pointed at him. "That one you may keep."

Feli considered. "Fine. Broadcast phrasing, not announcement copy."

Heinz smiled at Ren.

Ren, wisely, looked away.

A junior event coordinator near the end of the table raised her hand. "Question about public excitement level."

Feli's expression suggested she had seen those words kill before.

"Yes?"

"Are we trying to make the race sound dangerous or safe?"

Half the room answered at once.

"Safe," said Mara.

"Dangerous," said Maude.

"Controlled," said Amara.

"Accountable," said Greta.

"Festive," said Heinz.

"Legible," said Keisha.

"Attached to inspected brackets," said Rolf.

Feli held up one hand until the table settled.

"We are making it sound like what it is," she said. "A dangerous race made publicly acceptable by rules, routing, engineering, labor, emergency planning, consent, and consequences."

The room was quiet.

Heinz looked at her.

Feli glanced at him, wary. "What?"

"That was very good."

"I know."

"You should say it."

"I just did."

"In the announcement."

"No."

"But-"

"No."

Amara leaned back. "Put a shorter version in the release notes."

Feli made a note. "Release notes."

Heinz mouthed release notes as if they were a consolation prize he intended to cherish.

The wall clock reached the announcement window.

Outside the conference room, Der Himmelskrug was already changing shape for the day. Public lifts had festival routing loaded but not yet active. Retail had temporary queue markers stacked behind service desks. Hospitality had printed menus with race-sector names and disclaimers so long they required folding. The Starship Garage had posted a polite notice reminding visitors that historic ships, racing ships, unusual ships, emotionally significant ships, and ships with famous paint were still not touchable without permission.

Flight Control had posted no decorative notice.

Flight Control had posted restrictions.

Feli stood and gathered her slate.

"Final sequence," she said. "Heinz opens with welcome, no wink language, no spontaneous metaphors involving yeast, destiny, or orbital courage."

Heinz looked wounded.

"Board confirms civic authorization," she continued. "Amara confirms Flight Control authority and safety limits. Mara confirms compliance language. Ren and Maude take over broadcast expansion after the official announcement. Sponsor acknowledgments remain after safety language. Prize purse after rules, not before."

Anja raised a hand. "Prize after rules remains final?"

"Yes," said Feli, Mara, Amara, and Greta together.

Anja smiled faintly. "Good. Tradition survives better when it does not trip over liability in the first paragraph."

Heinz raised his own hand.

Feli looked at him with deep suspicion. "Yes?"

"May I say welcome to Der Himmelskrug?"

"That is literally your line."

"Excellent."

"And then stop."

Heinz nodded solemnly. "A devastating constraint, but I accept."

They stood.

Chairs shifted. Slates were gathered. The exterior feed held on Der Himmelskrug's best non-winking angle. Through the wall, faintly, the station public chime sounded once, then again, alerting visitors and crews that a formal civic announcement was about to begin.

Heinz looked at the silent image of the station.

A beer stein at The Knot.

A joke with docking authority.

A festival with emergency corridors.

A thing so absurd it had to be run honestly or it would become dangerous in exactly the ways its enemies had once predicted.

He felt, as he often did before public events, a ridiculous swell of affection for all of it: the glowing Crown, the busy public levels, the hidden service corridors, the staff who kept visitors from becoming incidents, the pilots who would complain and comply, the dockworkers who would be blamed if planners got lazy, the families who would come because joy was also infrastructure.

Feli touched his elbow.

"Ready?"

Heinz straightened his jacket.

"No," he said. "But in the civic sense."

Amara opened the door.

The announcement light went green.

Heinz stepped toward the feed, already hearing the first line in his head and the second line Feli had forbidden trying to sneak in behind it.

He smiled.

Not too broadly.

Not like a wink.

"Welcome to Der Himmelskrug," he said.

The public chime rang three times.

Der Himmelskrug knew how to make a noise feel civic.

It began low, somewhere under the public decks, not so much heard as felt through the soles of shoes, chair legs, lift rails, counter edges, and the warm body of the station itself. Then it rose into the visitor levels, clean and bell-like, threading through taproom laughter, retail chatter, cargo notifications, children arguing over which lift bubble was fastest, and the constant soft murmur of a city-sized beer stein pretending to be ordinary infrastructure.

The first chime meant attention.

The second meant official.

The third meant Feli had approved the wording.

In the Observation Level public atrium, tourists turned toward the main display wall. Workers checked the nearest screen while still carrying things. A family from Tannenfall paused under the carved route-map arch with four children, three bags, and one ancestral sapling in a legal transport tube. A pair of long-haul pilots at the coffee kiosk stopped mid-argument over docking fees. Two hospitality trainees straightened on instinct, though neither of them had caused anything yet.

On Hospitality 1, Mateo Alvarez paused beside a table where three guests had attempted to order the "race thing" before the race thing had technically been announced.

"Now we may learn what you have been incorrectly excited about," he said.

The guests looked delighted.

On Retail, shopkeepers stood beside covered displays and pretended the cloths did not have commemorative graphics underneath. One cloth had slipped enough to reveal the words THE LAST POUR in gold letters. A clerk stepped in front of it with the calm speed of someone preventing a small legal event.

In the Starship Garage, a clean-side tech looked up from a diagnostic cart and said, "Chime."

Oskar Teague did not look up from the intake schedule.

"I know what a chime is."

"It's the announcement."

"I know what an announcement is."

The tech waited.

Oskar sighed and turned the nearest wall display volume up by exactly two notches.

"If anyone stops torque checks to clap," he said, "they are clapping with a wrench in their teeth."

Three people put their hands behind their backs.

On the Crown Level, the official feed went live.

The first image was Der Himmelskrug from a restrained exterior angle.

Not winking.

Feli Brandt had won that fight.

The station hung at The Knot in clean profile: Crown Level rim bright but not royal, public body warm with window light, handle arc visible behind it like a structural flourish someone had dared Engineering to justify and Engineering had, to everyone's inconvenience, justified very well. Traffic moved around it in ordered lanes. Route markers glowed in the dark. Emergency corridors remained visible in the overlay, thin and untouchable.

Then the feed cut to Heinz Schmitt.

He stood before the official civic backdrop, which showed the station seal, the Governing Board mark, and a subdued race emblem: a stylized stein outline crossed by a clean orbital line. It was dignified enough that Heinz looked faintly suspicious of it.

He smiled.

Not too broadly.

"Welcome to Der Himmelskrug."

Feli, off-camera, did not visibly relax because visible relaxation was how improvisers found openings.

Heinz continued.

"Today, on behalf of the Der Himmelskrug Governing Board, our station staff, our route partners, and every worker who has spent the last six months turning joy into schedules, barriers, inspections, permits, emergency corridors, public toilets, vendor rules, and traffic windows, I am pleased to announce the opening of this year's Himmelskrug Run."

Across the station, the word landed.

Run.

The atrium cheered first. It always did. Tourists cheered because they liked being present when other people decided an event was important. Pilots cheered because they knew exactly how dangerous it was and had somehow made that part of the appeal. Retail cheered because covered merchandise could finally become uncovered merchandise. Hospitality cheered because menus with disclaimers were still menus.

In Flight Control, no one cheered.

They were busy.

Amara Reid appeared beside Heinz on the feed.

That ended several cheers early.

Amara did not have to look severe. She had the kind of professional calm that made unsafe confidence begin revising itself in private.

"The Himmelskrug Run is a sanctioned race operating under temporary Flight Control race authority," she said. "All normal approach, departure, emergency, and exclusion-zone laws remain in force outside authorized race windows. Registered racers will receive clearance only after inspection, eligibility review, transponder confirmation, telemetry handshake, and acceptance of remote abort authority."

A pilot at the coffee kiosk muttered, "Romantic."

His companion said, "Alive is romantic."

Amara continued.

"The rules are simple because simple rules are harder to misunderstand creatively. No hyperspace. No active weapons. No deliberate contact. No sensor spoofing. No unauthorized boost systems. No interference with course telemetry. No unsanctioned practice in traffic space. No sponsor, guest, media, Board, or founder exemption from safety requirements."

The display cut briefly to Heinz.

He nodded with great dignity, as if founder exemptions had never personally wounded him.

Amara looked directly into the camera.

"If Flight Control tells you to hold, you hold. If Flight Control tells you to abort, you abort. If Flight Control tells you that your race is over, your race is over. You may be disappointed after you stop moving."

In the atrium, one long-haul pilot whispered, "I love her."

"Everyone does," said another pilot.

"No, I mean fearfully."

"Everyone does."

The feed shifted to a course map.

The Knotward Run appeared in bright clean lines around The Knot and its local landmarks. The public version was beautiful, which meant at least seven departments had fought to make it accurate enough not to kill anyone.

Ren Calder's voice took over, warm, clear, practiced, and entirely too happy to be legally allowed near a live feed.

"This year's official course is the Knotward Run, returning with its full sanctioned sequence: opening chicanes, the Kaltmond gravity-assist corridor, the Kaltmond exit chicane, the Twins' Teeth, the Teeth-to-Gauntlet transition, The Gauntlet, final acceleration recovery, The Last Pour, and the finish line under Flight Control timing."

The course map animated.

Ships represented by clean white markers left the launch corridor and threaded the opening chicanes. The public version ran at reduced speed so spectators could understand it without becoming dizzy or developing incorrect confidence.

Maude Kess joined the commentary.

"If this map makes the course look easy," she said, "that is because graphics have manners and physics does not."

The atrium laughed.

Pilots laughed harder.

Maude continued.

"The opening chicanes are not decoration. They punish sloppy rhythm early. Kaltmond rewards pilots who respect gravity and embarrasses pilots who think gravity is impressed by them. The Twins' Teeth require yaw and roll through offset gates, not a straight-line squeeze. The Gauntlet tests correction discipline after your nerves have already spent money. The Last Pour is beautiful, which is how you know it wants something."

On Hospitality 1, Mateo stopped beside a server station and listened with the expression of a man hearing the exact description of several customers he had met.

Ren said, "Public viewing will be available only from authorized zones. Station event staff, Safety, Security, Port Services, and Flight Control routing determine access."

The display showed public zones in green, staff-only routing in amber, and restricted or operational areas in red.

Feli's voice entered the announcement for the practical section, warm and precise.

"If a door, railing, hatch, lift, service corridor, crew gate, or staff member says no, that means no. Visitors who attempt to enter restricted areas will be removed from festival access. Visitors who attempt to enter operational areas may become case studies in why public signage uses verbs."

Janelle Okonkwo's face appeared beside the zone map.

She did not smile.

"Station Security supports this message."

That was all she said.

It was enough.

On Retail, three customers stepped back from a rope line they had not yet crossed.

Keisha Bell appeared next, standing in Port Services with crew boards visible behind her.

"Crew services remain active during festival operations. Visiting crews will have posted routing for rest pods, showers, laundry, food access, family waiting areas, and quiet support. Festival traffic does not override crew safety. If you are too tired to fly, come to Port Services before you make yourself expensive to everyone."

Somewhere in the Starship Garage, a technician murmured, "A rested pilot is cheaper than a memorial plaque."

Oskar pointed at the torque check without looking.

The technician returned to work.

The feed moved through the civic sequence: Board authorization, partner acknowledgments, sponsor names held tastefully after safety language, route-user thanks, labor consultation, emergency planning, and a brief note that all race activity remained subject to inspection and cancellation if conditions required.

Then Feli returned to the center of the feed.

"And now," she said, "the purse."

Across Der Himmelskrug, rooms changed.

Even people who did not care about racing understood money.

Feli's delivery stayed steady, which was how viewers knew the number was large.

"This year's Himmelskrug Run will award twenty-five million IFP Standard Credits for first place, twenty million for second, and fifteen million for third."

The atrium made a sound that was not exactly a cheer.

It was too startled for that.

It began as a collective intake of breath and then broke into voices. Pilots swore. A child asked whether twenty-five million was more than twelve. Someone in Retail dropped a covered display sign. At the coffee kiosk, both long-haul pilots stopped pretending they were too seasoned to be impressed.

On the feed, Feli continued before anyone could fill the silence with bad math.

"The purse is funded through Board-approved event reserve, sponsor commitments, and licensed media distribution. Sponsor participation does not alter race authority, safety rules, pilot eligibility, Flight Control authority, emergency routing, worker safety requirements, or inspection outcomes."

Amara appeared again.

"No prize changes physics."

Maude's voice followed, dry and immediate.

"And physics has never accepted a bribe."

That line traveled faster than the official phrasing.

Within seconds, someone in the atrium repeated it. Then a pilot at the coffee kiosk. Then a child misquoted it as "physics does not eat money," which several adults privately preferred.

Heinz returned for the closing.

He had the expression of a man standing on a festival stage while holding the entire load path of a civic institution in his head.

"The Himmelskrug Run is one of our proudest traditions," he said. "Not because it is loud, though it is loud. Not because it is beautiful, though it is sometimes beautiful if everyone respects the signage. It matters because joy at The Knot has to be built honestly. Pilots, workers, residents, guests, crews, sponsors, families, and officials all share the same station while the Run is underway. So we will celebrate. We will argue about impossible lines in public places. We will buy questionable souvenir items from vendors who have completed their forms. We will feed people. We will route people. We will keep emergency corridors empty. We will listen to Flight Control."

He paused.

The pause was dangerous.

Feli's off-camera stillness became visible in the air.

Heinz smiled, smaller this time.

"Welcome to the Run."

The feed cut to the race emblem.

Then to the course map.

Then to Ren and Maude, who began the expanded broadcast segment with sponsor-safe enthusiasm and technically accurate dread.

Der Himmelskrug erupted.

Not everywhere at once. Stations did not erupt evenly. They had decks and schedules and people on break and people not on break and people pretending to be on break while watching from behind inventory carts.

The Observation Level filled with voices. Hospitality menus were uncovered. Retail displays bloomed open: course maps, sector pins, legal foam fingers, Last Pour shirts, Kaltmond plush moons with warning tags, and one line of mugs that said NO PRIZE CHANGES PHYSICS before anyone admitted who had approved them.

On Services, staff checked event routing.

On Residential, Priya Natarajan's team confirmed quiet-hour notices and child-safe lift priorities.

In the Starship Garage, Oskar turned the volume down.

The tech beside him stared at the frozen prize graphic.

"Twenty-five million," the tech said.

Oskar picked up the diagnostic slate.

"Torque check."

"That is a very large torque check."

"Ours pays less and kills fewer people."

"Yes, Chief."

Oskar glanced once at the course map before the display cycled back to intake schedules.

Opening chicanes. Kaltmond. Teeth. Gauntlet. Last Pour.

He knew the shape of that course in his hands.

He also knew what large purses did to small operators.

"Expect bad decisions," he said.

The technician looked up.

"From racers?"

"From everyone."

On the Crown Level, Heinz stepped off the feed platform and exhaled.

Feli handed him his slate back.

He accepted it carefully. "Was I acceptable?"

"You were almost entirely line-compliant."

"Almost?"

"You said questionable souvenir items."

"That was true."

"It was also bait."

"It was affectionate bait."

"Still bait."

Amara passed them with her Flight Control packet. "No one entered an approach lane during the announcement. I am calling it a success."

"That is a harsh metric," Heinz said.

"That is why it works."

Greta joined them near the side corridor, already reading the first staff-impact messages. "Retail wants clarification on whether 'physics does not eat money' can go on shirts."

"No," Feli said.

"Yes," Heinz said.

Feli looked at him.

Heinz reconsidered.

"Maybe in the release notes?"

"No."

Anja Reiter approached from the Board side of the platform. "The announcement landed well. New Bavaria will be pleased, alarmed, proud, and legally cautious."

"In what order?" Heinz asked.

"All at once. We are efficient."

Rolf emerged behind her carrying a bracket sample no one had asked to see.

"Who approved the temporary banner anchors on Retail?"

Feli closed her eyes. "Not now."

"Temporary is where permanent hides until it kills someone."

Greta pointed at Rolf without looking up from her slate. "That line is going in the labor bulletin."

"It is an engineering warning."

"Most labor bulletins are."

Good.

Somewhere, pilots were seeing it.

Somewhere, small crews were doing dangerous arithmetic.

Somewhere, a family might hear a number and mistake it for rescue.

Now the announcement belonged to everyone who heard it.

Not live.

Nothing aboard *Last Good Haul* received public feeds live unless the universe had made an administrative error. The freighter's comms buffer collected things in pieces, sorted them according to ancient priority rules, lost half the thumbnails, compressed the rest until everyone looked like they had been carved from soap, and delivered civic broadcasts with the confidence of a machine that had technically fulfilled its duty.

FORMAL CIVIC ANNOUNCEMENT: DER HIMMELSKRUG.

FESTIVAL / TRAFFIC / RACE AUTHORITY NOTICE.

He was half-listening to the freighter's power pulse, half-watching the cargo-bay pressure, and half-arguing with a coolant prediction that had invented a new way to be rude.

That was three halves.

He noticed.

Opening chicanes to discipline the field.

Gravity assist if you trusted the line.

Offset gates if you could feel yaw and roll as one motion instead of two.

A final kink that punished pilots who treated the finish as permission to stop thinking.

Not this exact course. Not this sanctioned event. Not the current markers or telemetry rules or Flight Control windows. But the language of it. The grammar.

A race course was an argument with physics conducted at speed.

His father's hands would have twitched at the Kaltmond corridor.

His grandfather would have said something unforgivable about the Twins' Teeth and then been right enough to make it worse.

Fair.

Not into hope. Not that fast.

Into someone who had been standing in a locked room and heard, from very far away, the scrape of a key.

He should have.

Pause.

Tap tap.

Long pause.

Swipe.

Tap.

No prize changes physics.

Good.

Physics had already filed several objections to their family.

"Don't."

Amber. Holding.

Amber. Holding.

Power sag. Recover.

Cargo bay stable.

Route clear.

No immediate disaster.

A very rude time for the universe to introduce a possible future.

"Yes."

There it was.

Not rescue.

Freya would never call it that. Rescue was something outside you. This was a door with teeth.

"I know."

Too long.

Sven's chest tightened.

"Yes?"

Almost.

Possible low-power feedback delay. Full diagnostic required before any powered movement or release from cradle.

Not safe yet.

Not lost yet.

The words stayed anyway.

She stopped.

Because that was the edge.

If she can.

Sven finished it badly.

"If she can, then I humiliate myself in front of every pilot who remembers what my grandfather and father did with her."

"That is less poetic."

"It is more useful."

Ingrid made a small approving sound.

Freya added, "And you do it because the alternative is letting fear make the decision before evidence does."

Evidence.

That word he understood.

Not hope. Not destiny. Not Andersen legend.

Evidence.

Amber. Holding.

Coolant. Watch.

Power. Recover.

Cargo. Stable.

Route. Clear.

Race bulletin. Saved.

Future. Unknown.

He could work with unknown. Unknown had edges. Unknown could be inspected, tested, declared, routed, denied, repaired, or marked unsafe.

Hopelessness had no handles.

This had handles.

Too many, maybe.

But some.

Freya stared at him.

"Sven."

"Secure your harness."

"Are you ending the conversation?"

"No."

"Because if you are-"

Not hope.

Not yet.

But the locked room had a key scrape now, and someone had handed him a maintenance light.

Himmelskrug Run: evaluate only after full garage diagnostic, Flight Control eligibility review, and safety signoff. No commitment before evidence.

Added one more line.

But do not refuse before evidence either.

She did not say anything.

She did not need to.

Sven listened to the ship.

Freya listened to the numbers.

Behind them, in the dark cargo bay, *Opportunity Knocks* waited without promising anyone a thing.

For six minutes after the announcement ended, nobody said the word race.

That was not avoidance.

Not entirely.

Aboard *Last Good Haul*, silence had uses. It let the coolant drift show itself before it became an alarm. It let Nora decide the adults were not currently exploding. It let Ingrid resume the old route song, softer this time, which gave the ship something human to carry besides numbers and fear. It let Freya strap in properly and redo the math without the drama of reaching for the slate under thrust.

Mostly, it let Sven fail to think.

He was very busy failing.

The outbound lane stayed clear. Route Control handed them a long, boring vector with a minor correction window in forty-two minutes. The freighter's power sag kept its twelve-second rhythm. The cargo-bay seal remained amber. The cradle load around *Opportunity Knocks* stayed stable enough to resent being watched.

Every system had the courtesy to be manageable.

Which meant the unmanageable thing had room.

Twenty-five million IFP Standard Credits.

Sven tried not to picture it as money. Money was too blunt. Money became numbers, then debts, then signatures, then Northstar's serif font climbing back into his skull with polished shoes.

So he pictured it as weight.

Medical debt lifted.

Berth fees cleared.

Fuel and food paid.

Legal reserve.

A clean refusal Northstar could not starve into surrender.

Freya had already seen all of it. Of course she had. Freya could look at a number and hear the shape of every door it opened and every trapdoor underneath. She had said first prize could save them, then immediately broken that sentence into categories because hope left uninspected became a hazard.

Sven loved her so much it made him angry.

He adjusted the stabilizer trim a fraction too sharply.

*Last Good Haul* complained.

The cargo-bay seal flickered.

"Sorry," he muttered.

"Ship or wife?" Ingrid asked over the family channel.

Sven closed his eyes for half a second.

"Both."

"I accept on behalf of the ship," Ingrid said. "Your wife may require forms."

Freya's voice came from the rear jump seat. "I require fewer forms than Northstar."

"That is an almost romantic standard."

"Do not test it."

Nora whispered, not very quietly, "Are Mama and Papa doing jokes or terms?"

"Both," Ingrid said.

Freya said, "Terms with jokes nearby."

Nora accepted this.

Sven checked the route again.

Clear.

There it was.

The quiet room.

"I know."

"I can infer."

Almost.

"No. You're arranging the decision so it has fewer hiding places."

"That is a very accurate and unhelpful accusation."

He glanced at the reflection panel. He could see her in miniature, strapped in now, slate held against her lap, hair loose on one side. Nora sat beyond her, half-visible, apparently making the moon-bear nod along to Ingrid's song.

"She's answering late," he said.

"You told me."

"I need to say it again."

"Then say it."

"That means old frame resonance, control-feedback lag, a pilot-interface issue, actuator timing, or something uglier hiding where a standard inspection will call it age. "Yes."

He gripped the control yoke.

Of course.

Of course she would do that. Take the thing he meant as a locked door and label it an entrance with conditions.

"No. Listen to me now."

He listened.

Not because she ordered it.

Because she had earned more than listening.

"We do not race a broken ship," she said. "We do not hide a fault. We do not let Northstar's pressure make us reckless. We do not let money bribe physics. We do not let Andersen pride rename danger as destiny."

His jaw tightened at that last one.

She heard it.

The words were not new.

But now there was less announcement noise around them. Less prize shock. More edge.

That landed.

"No."

Gates.

Most ships were.

Cargo. Stable.

Race. Unknown.

Good.

Sven nodded once, though she could barely see it.

"Then I agree to try to reach a decision."

Freya made a sound that might have been a laugh if there had been more air in it.

"That is the most engineer sentence ever used to admit hope."

"It is not hope."

"No?"

"It is a procedural opening."

"Of course."

"With safety gates."

"Obviously."

"And no emotional overcommitment."

"Never."

He could hear her smiling now.

Not much.

Enough.

Nora's voice cut across the family channel, which apparently had been less closed than either adult thought.

"Are we doing the race?"

Sven froze.

Freya whispered something that was probably not for children.

Ingrid said, with immense calm, "We are doing grown-up planning."

Nora considered this. "Is that like packing?"

"Yes," Ingrid said. "But with more sighing."

"Is Papa scared?"

Sven looked at the route lane.

"Yes," he said.

"Is Mama scared?"

"Yes," Freya said.

"Is Grandma scared?"

Ingrid paused just long enough for the truth to put on shoes.

"Yes."

Nora was quiet.

Then she said, "Moon-bear is also scared, but he says he can be strapped in."

Sven closed his eyes.

Freya made a tiny sound.

Ingrid said, "Moon-bear is wise."

Nora added, "He says no racing if Opportunity is sick."

Freya answered before Sven could.

"That is exactly right."

Sven opened his eyes.

The lane was still there.

The ship was still old.

The money was still impossible.

The risk had not gotten smaller.

But the decision had changed shape.

It was no longer a cliff he had to jump from because panic pushed him.

It was a series of gates.

Most gates could close.

One might open.

Sven routed the saved announcement, Freya's calculation note, and his own safety-gate list into a shared family folder labeled DER HIMMELSKRUG / REVIEW. Then he added a new item at the bottom.

Ask garage intake for independent diagnostic before discussing registration.

He hovered over the final word.

Registration.

There it was.

He saved the file.

Freya's slate chimed.

She read it.

"Good," she said.

The word did not mean victory.

It meant the stack had not fallen in the last minute.

It meant there was a next action.

For the Andersens, that was close enough to grace.

Ahead, Der Himmelskrug remained beyond sensor range, but its announcement sat inside *Last Good Haul* now, bright and dangerous and properly filed.

Sven set his hands lightly on the controls.

The hand that listened.

The mouth that did not brag.

The husband who could at least admit gates existed.

"Freya?"

"Yes?"

"If this becomes stupid, you tell me."

"If this becomes stupid, I will bring documentation."

"That is what I was afraid of."

"Good."

He smiled.

Behind him, Nora began explaining safety gates to the moon-bear.

Ingrid resumed humming.

Freya kept the math open.

And Sven Andersen flew toward Der Himmelskrug without yet saying yes, which was the first honest way he had found not to say no.

Sven waited twenty-three minutes before going back to the cargo bay.

That was discipline.

Or cowardice with a timer.

He told himself it was operationally necessary. Route Control had assigned a stable corridor, but *Last Good Haul* still had three amber systems, one coolant mood, and a cargo-bay seal that seemed to consider honesty a personal challenge. He needed to watch the outbound lane through the next correction window. He needed to make sure the power sag stayed on its ugly twelve-second rhythm. He needed to confirm the cradle load after the last minor thrust adjustment.

All of that was true.

It was also true that the cargo bay contained the ship he had not agreed to race.

Not yet.

Not yes.

Not no.

A procedural opening with safety gates.

Freya had repeated that phrase back to him once, very dryly, and then written it down exactly because she was a woman who believed in preserving evidence of emotional cowardice for future use.

Now the next correction window had passed. The seal remained amber. The coolant remained offended but manageable. Route Control had cleared them for a long quiet interval.

Sven unbuckled.

The harness released with a click that sounded too loud.

Freya looked up from the rear jump seat. The slate rested against her knees, still open to the shared folder. Nora had fallen asleep against her own harness with moon-bear wedged under one arm and its crooked face pressed to the strap like a weary navigator. Ingrid sat beside her, awake, hands folded, eyes closed.

Not sleeping.

Listening.

Freya kept her voice low. "Going aft?"

"Yes."

"Systems?"

"Stable enough."

"That is not a category I love."

"It is a category I am respecting."

Her mouth moved slightly.

A tired echo of earlier.

"Do you want me to come?" she asked.

He did.

Immediately. Completely.

"No," he said.

Freya studied him.

Then she nodded.

"Take the slate."

"I know the list."

"Take the slate."

He reached for it.

She did not let go right away.

"Sven."

He looked at her hand on the slate. Not her face. That was safer.

"We are not asking her for an answer," Freya said.

The cargo bay seemed suddenly very far away.

"I know."

"We are not making a promise to a ship."

"I know."

"We are not letting your father's ghost, your grandfather's ghost, Northstar, Mirador, the prize purse, or an entire network of race fans crowd into that bay and vote."

He looked up then.

Freya's eyes were sharp with fatigue.

"Nora gets a vote," he said softly.

Freya's face bent.

"Yes," she said. "Nora gets a vote."

He took the slate.

The list was open.

DER HIMMELSKRUG / REVIEW

- Arrive clean.
- Declare freighter limitations.
- Declare stored racing craft.
- Declare possible control-response lag before any powered movement.
- Request independent Starship Garage diagnostic.
- No powered release from cradle before safety signoff.
- No race registration before ship safety, pilot eligibility, and Flight Control clearance.
- No promises to Nora.
- No calling prize money rescue.
- No pretending prize money does not matter.
- No pretending prize money matters more than survival.

Freya had added one more line while he was flying.

- No shame decisions.

He stared at it.

"She has been editing," Ingrid said, eyes still closed.

Sven glanced toward her. "I thought you were asleep."

"At my age, sleep is a rumor I inspect occasionally."

Nora made a small sleeping sound.

Moon-bear remained neutral.

Sven held the slate against his side.

"I'll be a few minutes."

Freya nodded. "If the seal moves, I'll call."

"If Route Control pings-"

"I know."

"If the coolant-"

"Sven."

He stopped.

Freya's expression softened into something that hurt worse than impatience.

"Go listen," she said.

So he did.

The corridor aft was narrow enough that he had to turn one shoulder past the emergency kit. *Last Good Haul* hummed around him, every old panel carrying vibration in a slightly different dialect. He knew which wall seam buzzed under low thrust. He knew where the floor dipped near the midship hatch because the support bracket had been replaced with a part meant for a lighter frame. He knew the galley latch would rattle on the next deceleration unless he wedged the folded cleaning cloth back under it.

He knew this ship.

He loved this ship, in the exhausted way people loved things that had kept promises longer than they were built to keep them.

He did not want to owe her more.

The cargo-bay hatch opened with a hydraulic sigh and a smell of coolant, dust, and old freight webbing.

The lights came up low.

*Opportunity Knocks* waited in the cradle.

The racer looked different now.

That was impossible. Nothing had changed. Same folded vanes. Same dull red-gold Andersen stripe. Same old release labels and sealed canopy and cradle restraints and tiedown points. Same posture of sharp, patient purpose.

But the announcement had reached the cargo bay before him.

Not through speakers. Not through mysticism. Through him.

He had carried the race in.

Opening chicanes.

Kaltmond.

Twins' Teeth.

The Gauntlet.

The Last Pour.

Twenty-five million credits.

No prize changes physics.

No shame decisions.

Sven stopped three steps inside the bay.

"You heard none of that," he said.

The ship, sensibly, did not answer.

"Good."

The bay power pulsed.

The lamps dimmed and recovered.

"Not you," he told *Last Good Haul*. "I'm busy."

The freighter ticked overhead, offended by being excluded from a family problem.

Sven crossed to the cradle and set the slate on a crate near the service step. He did not climb into the cockpit this time. That felt important. The cockpit made questions too intimate. It put Lars's note under his hands and Nils's bad label near his face and sixteen-year-old Sven in the seat beside him, furious with instruction and hungry for approval.

He stayed outside.

A man looking at a ship.

An engineer looking at a fault.

A father looking at a risk.

He placed one palm against the hull, flat and still.

Cool metal.

No answer.

He closed his eyes anyway.

Not to feel magic. There was no magic in this. No destiny stored under the clearcoat. No ancestral blessing humming in the actuator lines. The ship did not know his debt. The ship did not care about prize purses. The ship would not become safe because the story needed her to.

That was exactly why he trusted her more than he trusted himself.

"You're answering late," he said.

The words were easier alone.

"Or I think you are. Maybe I'm wrong. Maybe I'm hearing grief in the controls. Maybe I'm turning age into omen because it lets me be scared of a repair instead of everything else."

The bay held the confession without improving it.

"But I felt it."

He opened his eyes.

The red-gold stripe ran beneath his hand. Nils's choice. Lars's maintenance. His inheritance whether he wanted the weight or not.

"I'm going to tell them."

That was the first promise.

Not to race.

Not to win.

Not to save anyone.

To tell.

"We get to Der Himmelskrug, and I tell garage intake you may be late on response. I tell them before powered movement. I tell them before anyone gets romantic near your cockpit. I tell them before Heinz Schmitt or a race official or some commentator or any person with a camera says your name like it means something simple."

His hand tightened once against the hull.

"I tell them even if that means they say no."

That one hurt.

Good.

Pain was not proof, but it was sometimes where the truth had been standing when the rest of him tried to leave.

Behind him, the hatch clicked.

Sven looked back.

Ingrid stood in the doorway.

She had a blanket around her shoulders and moved like someone who had decided age was advisory. Her hair was loose from its travel clip. The faded tattoo on her wrist showed where the blanket slipped.

"You said no company," Sven said.

"You said that to your wife."

"Ah."

"I am your mother. Different jurisdiction."

He accepted this because there was no useful appeal.

Ingrid came down the short ramp, one hand on the rail. Sven moved automatically to help, and she gave him a look so old and sharp he redirected the motion into checking that the ramp edge was clear.

She noticed.

Did not punish him for it.

Progress.

Ingrid stopped beside him and looked at *Opportunity Knocks*.

For a while, neither of them spoke.

The cargo bay hummed. The cradle restraints held. The ship waited between generations as if waiting were the one maneuver everyone eventually learned.

"Your father hated this part," Ingrid said.

Sven glanced at her. "Standing in cargo bays?"

"Being unable to make skill into certainty."

He looked back at the ship.

"Lars always seemed certain."

Ingrid snorted.

The sound was small and magnificently rude.

"Lars performed certainty the way Nils performed charm. Loudly, selectively, and most often when frightened."

Sven let that settle.

It altered the room.

Not much.

Enough.

"He was frightened?" Sven asked.

"Constantly."

"That's not what he showed me."

"No," Ingrid said. "He showed you standards. Standards are easier to pass down than fear."

Sven's jaw tightened.

Ingrid reached out and touched the hull with two fingers. Not possessive. Not ceremonial. Familiar.

"He was frightened before every race that mattered," she said. "Not of losing. Losing annoyed him. He was frightened of becoming careless because people expected him to be brave."

The cargo-bay lights hummed.

Sven looked at the cockpit.

Lars's note sat inside, invisible from here.

LISTEN FIRST. CORRECT SECOND. BRAG NEVER.

"I thought he wrote that for me," Sven said.

"He did."

Ingrid's fingers traced the stripe once.

"He wrote it for himself first."

Sven swallowed.

The bay blurred, then steadied.

Ingrid pretended not to see because she loved him.

"Did he ever want to quit?" Sven asked.

"Yes."

That answer came too easily.

He turned to her.

Ingrid kept looking at the racer. "After Nils died. After the second big win, when everyone wanted him to be Nils with better manners. After you were born. After the freight business started needing race money in ways he did not like. After he got sick and realized people were already turning him into a story while he was still in the room."

Sven's throat closed around the last words.

"He didn't tell me."

"You were his son."

"That should have meant he told me more."

"It often means fathers tell less and hope the rest becomes obvious through maintenance notes."

A laugh broke out of him, brief and painful.

Ingrid looked satisfied.

"Your father was many things," she said. "Subtle was not one of them. But even he understood that a ship is not family because she wins. She is family because people tell the truth around her."

Sven looked at his hand on the hull.

"I told Freya I'm not enough for her."

"Yes."

"You heard that?"

"I was strapped in, not dead."

He huffed a laugh.

Ingrid's voice softened.

"Enough is not a useful word for ships. Safe is useful. Honest is useful. Skilled is useful. Prepared is useful. Cleared is useful. Enough belongs to stories people tell after the difficult part, when they want the ending to look inevitable."

Sven closed his eyes.

"No inevitability," he said.

"No," Ingrid said. "Thank God."

They stood with that.

Then Ingrid reached into the blanket fold and pulled out a small object.

A flight tag.

Old, flexible, worn at the edges. The kind pilots clipped to their wrist rings during sanctioned events before newer transponder practices made them ceremonial. This one had been sealed in clear film so long ago the corners had yellowed slightly.

ANDERSEN, LARS  
OPPORTUNITY KNOCKS

Sven stared.

"I thought you packed the records."

"I did."

"That's not a record."

"No."

"Why do you have it?"

"Because your father gave it to me after his last race."

Sven could not look away from the tag.

Ingrid held it flat in her palm.

"He said, 'Hide this before Nils frames it in the galley.'"

Despite everything, Sven smiled.

"Did Nils try?"

"Three times."

"Of course he did."

"I told Lars I would keep it until he needed to remember that he had been a pilot and not only a man paying bills."

The smile faded.

Ingrid placed the tag on the crate beside Freya's slate.

"He does not need it now."

Sven looked at her.

"No," he said.

Ingrid nodded toward the tag. "You may need it. Not as permission. Not as demand. As context."

"I don't know what to do with that."

"Good. Then you are unlikely to do something theatrical."

He laughed again.

This one hurt less.

Ingrid leaned closer to the ship. "Hello, old girl."

Sven looked sharply at her.

"What? Ships do not hear. I am speaking for myself."

"That's what I told Nora."

"Nora is wise."

"She is five."

"Wisdom has fewer bad habits at five."

The cargo bay hatch clicked again.

This time Freya's voice came through the intercom instead of her body.

"Route Control correction in six minutes."

Sven touched the wall pickup. "On my way."

A pause.

"Everything all right?"

He looked at the ship, the tag, his mother, the slate, the list of gates, and the dark cockpit that no longer felt like it was waiting for a performance.

"No," he said.

Freya said nothing.

He added, "But clearer."

"That I can work with."

The comm clicked off.

Ingrid pulled the blanket tighter around her shoulders.

"She is good for you."

"Yes."

"She is also right."

"She often is."

"Not often. Strategically."

Sven picked up the old flight tag.

It weighed almost nothing.

Rude, really, that something so light could pull so hard.

He did not clip it to anything. He did not put it in his pocket. He set it back beside the slate and took a picture with the maintenance cam, logging it into the family folder under PERSONAL / LARS.

Then, after a moment, he added a note.

Context, not permission.

He saved it.

Ingrid read over his shoulder.

"Good," she said.

"Freya's word."

"Useful family word."

Sven picked up the slate.

The list remained open. Gates, not promises.

He added one more line.

- Tell the truth around the ship.

Ingrid watched him do it.

Then she nodded once, the way she had in the bay before departure, accepting a new problem because it had been properly named.

Sven took his hand off *Opportunity Knocks*.

"I have to fly the freighter."

"Yes," Ingrid said. "Start with the ship currently moving."

He looked at the racer one last time.

"Not yet," he told her.

This time it did not sound like refusal.

It sounded like sequence.

He helped Ingrid back toward the corridor, though both of them pretended he was only walking nearby in case the ramp needed supervision. Behind them, the cargo-bay lights dimmed back to their travel setting.

*Opportunity Knocks* waited in the dark.

Not promised.

Not chosen.

Not refused.

Properly, for the first time since the announcement, not alone.

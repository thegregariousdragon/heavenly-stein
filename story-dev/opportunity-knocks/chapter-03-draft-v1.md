# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 3 Draft v1

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## Predators Adjust Course

Callum Dresk liked creditors best when they were frightened enough to be specific.

Most were not.

Most creditors believed specificity was something other people could later be blamed for. They preferred words polished smooth by lawyers and cowardice: preservation, resolution, exposure, mitigation, cooperation. Words that looked clean on a review screen because all the blood had been assigned to subcontractors.

Callum had been a subcontractor long enough to respect the arrangement.

He also knew when an arrangement had begun to rot.

The room Mirador had rented on Kestrel Minor's outer transit tier was not an office, because offices implied leases, names, waste schedules, and neighbors who remembered shoes. It was a logistics room with two fold-down desks, a flickering wall display, one water dispenser that made a clicking noise like a small animal reconsidering its life, and a door that could be paid to forget how often it opened.

Ilya Voren stood at the display with both arms folded, watching the tracker crawl across the local route grid.

The mark was small.

That annoyed Callum.

Small marks made people underestimate the cost of what they represented.

*Last Good Haul* appeared as a commercial tag now, legal and dull and obedient. Old freighter. Declared departure. Conservative vector. No emergency flag. No pursuit warrant. No public lien alert attached to the vessel identification because Northstar had not yet found a way to make "we may or may not be able to claim the racing ship inside the freighter" sound like a clean traffic instruction.

That was what bothered Callum most.

Not the debt. Debt was weather.

Not the family. Families were leverage with names.

The problem was that Sven Andersen had left clean.

People who fled badly made doors for Mirador. Skipped clearance. False manifests. Disabled beacons. Undeclared cargo. Threatened dockworkers. Bribed clerks. Anything that let Callum point at a rule and say, with some truth, that the target had made intervention necessary.

Sven had not done that.

Sven had filed.

Sven had declared enough problems to become inconveniently sympathetic to anyone who liked procedure more than creditors.

Ilya tapped the tracker position.

"He is still on the expected corridor."

"For now."

"You say that as if corridors have betrayed you personally."

"Corridors are promises made by people who believe nobody else is desperate."

Ilya glanced over. "That should go on a wall."

"In Mirador?"

"In a prison intake room."

Callum smiled without warmth.

The rented display chimed.

Not the tracker.

A route bulletin packet arrived in the corner of the screen, flagged public, high circulation, Der Himmelskrug origin.

Ilya opened it.

The first frame showed the station from a polished exterior angle. Der Himmelskrug gleamed against the dark, crown bright, handle curved, traffic disciplined around it with the obscene confidence of infrastructure that knew people liked looking at it.

Callum disliked Der Himmelskrug on principle.

It looked like a joke and behaved like a jurisdiction.

That combination was bad for people who made their living in the space between paperwork and bruises.

The announcement buffered. The audio stuttered once, corrected, and delivered Heinz Schmitt's welcome with the irritating clarity of a man whose absurdity had funding.

Ilya turned the volume lower.

Callum read the transcript instead.

Himmelskrug Run.

Sanctioned race.

Temporary Flight Control race authority.

No hyperspace.

No active weapons.

Inspection.

Eligibility review.

Telemetry handshake.

Remote abort authority.

Public viewing restrictions.

Crew services.

Emergency corridors.

Sponsor limitations.

Worker safety.

He scrolled faster.

"There," Ilya said.

Callum stopped.

The purse line sat in the transcript like a detonator someone had set gently on a desk.

First place: twenty-five million IFP Standard Credits.

Second: twenty million.

Third: fifteen.

The room became quieter.

The water dispenser clicked.

Callum looked from the transcript to the tracker.

Small mark.

Old freighter.

Andersen racing ship in the bay.

Family debt in the documents.

A prize large enough to turn desperation into a legal strategy.

Ilya spoke first. "That changes his options."

"It changes his wife's options."

"She is the paperwork one?"

"Freya Andersen. Freight accounts, medical billing, creditor communication, refinancing records."

"She sees it, then."

"She saw it before the announcement finished."

Ilya leaned closer to the display. The light flattened the scars along one side of her knuckles. "Does Sven race?"

"Unknown."

"That ship is not ready."

"You inspected it?"

"No."

"Then do not say unknown things in a room that charges by the hour."

Ilya gave him a mild look.

Callum accepted it. Ilya's mild looks usually meant she had noticed him becoming performative and was deciding whether to file it under useful irritation or wasted oxygen.

Callum enlarged the route packet, then pulled up the Andersen file.

Northstar Collateral Services had supplied the summary in layers. The first layer was clean: principal debt, amended preservation claim, disputed collateral classification, urgent review pending. The second layer was less clean: medical debt, palliative care, business collapse, repair arrears, refinancing penalties, legal ambiguity around *Opportunity Knocks*. The third layer was the one Northstar pretended not to have sent: pressure points, family structure, recent unemployment, known grief markers, likely decision-maker, possible shame response.

Mirador was not supposed to call that last layer coercion.

They called it behavioral context.

Callum preferred honest ugly words, but honest ugly words made invoices harder.

He highlighted the prize amount.

"Twenty-five million clears the table," he said.

Ilya did not ask which table.

Debt.

Penalties.

Berth fees.

Legal reserve.

The ability to hire someone with clean sleeves and time.

The ability to move a dispute from hunger to process.

The ability to make Northstar explain itself in daylight.

Ilya said, "Second prize?"

"Still damages leverage. Third complicates it."

"Winning is unlikely."

"So is a creditor hiring Mirador for a case it can win politely."

Ilya accepted that.

The display chimed again.

This time the packet came through a private channel.

NORTHSTAR COLLATERAL SERVICES  
PRIORITY PRESERVATION UPDATE  
ANDERSEN FILE

Callum did not open it immediately.

He looked at Ilya.

Ilya looked back.

Neither of them spoke.

The water dispenser clicked again, bravely filling the moral silence.

Callum opened the packet.

The message had been drafted by someone who had never personally had to block a corridor with their body. The sentences were elegant. The grammar had money. The verbs wore gloves.

Northstar remained concerned that unauthorized movement of disputed preservation assets could degrade collateral integrity.

Northstar noted that Der Himmelskrug's public event environment could create improper third-party access, publicity distortion, operational concealment, asset modification, registration interference, or value impairment.

Northstar expected Mirador Asset Protection Group to maintain active situational awareness, encourage voluntary resolution, preserve all lawful remedies, prevent dissipation of collateral value, and avoid unnecessary public escalation.

Northstar did not authorize unlawful conduct.

Northstar did not request confrontation.

Northstar did not instruct Mirador personnel to misrepresent legal authority.

Northstar also required hourly updates once the Andersen party entered Der Himmelskrug approach jurisdiction.

Callum read the message twice.

Then he leaned back.

"They are scared."

Ilya's mouth moved slightly. Not a smile. The rumor of one.

"They wrote 'voluntary resolution' four times."

"Five if you count the attachment."

"I don't count attachments before bad coffee."

"There is no coffee."

"That is part of the problem."

Callum opened the attachment.

The title was worse.

PRESERVATION PRIORITY: COLLATERAL ACCESS BEFORE PUBLIC STATUS CHANGE

"There it is," he said.

Ilya read over his shoulder from a professional distance that left room for neither trust games nor sentiment.

The attachment never said race registration. It did not need to. It referred to public status change, third-party process capture, external inspection interference, and creditor access deterioration.

Callum translated as he read.

Do not let the ship become a famous sympathetic race entrant under station inspection.

Do not let Sven become a protected pilot with garage witnesses, Flight Control logs, crew support, public attention, and a wife holding copies.

Do not let Der Himmelskrug turn the disputed lien into a process.

Get the signature first.

Get surrender first.

Get anything that lets Northstar say, later, that the family cooperated.

Ilya said, "No Northstar representative travelling?"

Callum checked the routing field.

"No."

"Of course not."

"They dislike contamination."

"They like distance."

"Distance is a kind of soap."

Ilya made a quiet sound. "That one should also go on a prison wall."

Callum closed the attachment and opened the Der Himmelskrug public access map.

The station unfolded across the screen in layers: Crown, Observation, Entertainment, Hospitality, Retail, Residential, Services, Starship Garage, Restricted Works. Public levels glowed in friendly colors. Controlled areas wore amber. Restricted areas wore red with the sort of calm that meant argument had already been anticipated and priced.

Callum disliked maps that spoke in advance.

Ilya leaned closer.

"Public atrium only through Retail."

"Yes."

"Residential controlled."

"Yes."

"Services controlled staff, crew, pilot, operational."

"Yes."

"Starship Garage clean-side controlled."

"Yes."

"Restricted Works dirty-side restricted."

"Yes."

"Bubble elevators public levels only."

"Yes."

Ilya crossed her arms again.

"Bad terrain."

"Very."

Callum turned on the overlay for ports and legal interfaces.

Arrival Control.

Flight Control.

Station Security.

Compliance and Customs Integrity.

Crew Support.

Port Services.

Starship Garage intake.

Emergency routing.

Public viewing zones.

Staff-only corridors.

No casual circulation from public decks to controlled ship interfaces. No public access to Restricted Works. No unauthorized entry to Starship Garage. No private seizure rights inside docking control. No creditor override of Flight Control release windows. No lien language that could move a ship through a pressure door.

Der Himmelskrug looked ridiculous to people who liked architecture to be embarrassed by joy.

It was not ridiculous in its bones.

Its bones were process, labor, doors, logs, and people whose job descriptions said no in different accents.

Ilya pointed at Starship Garage.

"If *Opportunity Knocks* enters there, we do not touch it."

"No."

"If Flight Control has arrival logs, release windows, craft declaration, transponder connection-"

"They will."

"-then moving against the ship looks like traffic interference."

"Or asset interference."

"Or coercion."

"Depends who writes it first."

Ilya pointed at Services. "What about here?"

Callum studied the layer.

Services sat below the public levels and above the ship-facing systems that made Der Himmelskrug useful. Controlled, but not sealed like the garage. Crew support routes. Pilot waiting areas. Port services. Staff transitions. People tired enough to want quiet. People stressed enough to believe a conversation was private because it was not happening in a food court.

"Better," Callum said.

"Still cameras."

"Everywhere has cameras."

"Staff."

"Everywhere useful has staff."

"Security response?"

"Fast if they understand what they are seeing."

"So we make sure they do not understand quickly."

Ilya looked at him.

Callum did not look away.

That was the work, when it was done well. Not violence. Violence was loud, expensive, and frequently performed by people who confused fear with skill. The better work was compression. Narrow time. Narrow options. Narrow language. Make the target believe the door had closed before any witness realized there had been a door.

No one needed to drag Sven Andersen anywhere.

They needed him tired, ashamed, legally frightened, and convinced that signing something was the last adult act left.

Ilya said, "Freya complicates that."

"Yes."

"Mother?"

"Witness risk. Emotional complication."

"Child?"

Callum gave her a cold look.

Ilya held up one hand. "I am assessing."

"Do not use the child."

"I did not suggest using the child."

"Good."

Ilya's face did not change, but something in the room settled. They had worked together long enough to know where each person's lines were, and long enough to know that lines were not goodness. Lines were professional maintenance. People without them became sloppy and made cases expensive.

Callum returned to the map.

"Sven is the hinge."

"Because the lien names the ship."

"Because shame makes him reachable."

"Freya sees traps."

"Yes."

"So isolate him from Freya."

"Briefly. Quietly. Not forcefully if possible."

Ilya nodded toward Services again. "Pilot registration?"

"Maybe. Garage intake. Crew support. Public meal. Family cannot afford hotel. They may try to return to the freighter."

"Station might move them to temporary lodging."

"After someone notices."

"So before that."

Callum closed the map and opened commercial transport schedules.

Kestrel Minor to Der Himmelskrug was not hard. Not direct at all hours, but the Himmelskrug Run announcement had changed the route economy within minutes. Extra passenger capacity had already appeared like mold in warm bread. Pilots, tourists, mechanics, media freelancers, people with opinions, people with hats, people who believed festivals improved them. Commercial carriers loved civic joy because civic joy bought tickets and complained less than funerals.

Ilya watched him search.

"No Mirador credentials," she said.

"No."

"No enforcement declaration."

"No."

"No asset-protection purpose at arrival."

"No."

"Tourists?"

"Too visible."

"Vendors?"

"Requires station vendor review."

"Media?"

"Higher scrutiny during race."

"Route consultants?"

"Boring. Plausible."

Callum paused.

"Maintenance supply auditors," Ilya said.

Callum shook his head. "Too close to controlled access. They will ask why we are not on a list."

"Private insurance observers?"

"Maybe."

"For a sponsor?"

"Risky."

"Race fans?"

Callum looked pained.

Ilya's almost-smile returned. "It is the cleanest lie."

"I dislike lies that require enthusiasm."

"You dislike enthusiasm."

"Correct."

Ilya opened a second search. "Two passenger seats. Ordinary arrival. No restricted access request. No business declaration beyond tourism. We enter public, watch, find Andersen, press before support forms around him."

"Race fans," Callum repeated.

"As cover, not identity."

Callum considered the station map again.

Public arrival did one useful thing: it gave them legitimacy until they wanted too much. Arrival Control would log them, yes. But logged public presence was not suspicious by itself. Der Himmelskrug expected thousands of people to arrive because someone had said twenty-five million credits near a camera.

A person entering a station to watch a famous race was ordinary.

A person entering a station to locate a financially cornered engineer and coerce a disputed collateral transfer was not.

Therefore, be the first person until the second one had what they needed.

Callum searched identity stock.

Mirador maintained files the way old ships maintained spare parts: some legal, some obsolete, some obtained from places that preferred not to discuss procurement. Der Himmelskrug would catch bad identities if the identities asked for important doors. They did not need important doors.

They needed public corridors, ordinary purchases, and time.

Callum selected two.

Marek Sova.

Tilen Voss.

Boring names. Mid-tier route histories. No violent flags. No outstanding conflicts. No station exclusions. No reason to remember them unless they did something memorable.

He assigned Marek to himself.

Ilya got Tilen.

Ilya read the profile. "Tilen collects race pins?"

"You do now."

"I object."

"Noted."

"She has public posts about vintage heat exchangers."

"That may be useful."

"She says 'thrilling' too often."

"Then speak less."

Ilya accepted this with more grace than the profile deserved.

Callum booked passage through a carrier named BrightSpan Local, which sounded cheerful enough to have cut maintenance margins but not enough to be illegal. Two seats. Standard luggage. No declared tools beyond personal electronics. No weapons. No restricted materials. No special docking request. No crew transfer. No professional credential.

Clean arrival.

Public purpose.

Ordinary passengers.

The booking confirmed.

Callum saved the tickets into the false identity wallets and forwarded a sanitized itinerary through Mirador's internal channel.

Northstar's private packet remained open in the corner of the display.

Ilya looked at it.

"Do we answer?"

Callum typed.

ACTIVE TRACKING CONTINUES. SUBJECT VESSEL EN ROUTE TO DER HIMMELSKRUG. LOCAL ENTRY PLAN ADJUSTED FOR EVENT CONDITIONS. PRIORITY REMAINS VOLUNTARY RESOLUTION BEFORE PUBLIC STATUS CHANGE OR THIRD-PARTY PROCESS CAPTURE. PUBLIC ESCALATION NOT RECOMMENDED.

He paused over the final sentence.

Then added:

NO NORTHSTAR PRESENCE REQUESTED OR REQUIRED.

Ilya read it. "Petty."

"Accurate."

"Also petty."

"Good."

He sent it.

The water dispenser clicked again, then released one bubble into its tank with the solemnity of a creature making peace with disappointment.

Ilya began clearing the room.

She wiped the display cache, packed the identity slates, removed the local relay chip, and took the cheap paper cups even though neither of them had used one. Ilya was thorough with rooms. It was one of the reasons Callum worked with her. A person who remembered cups generally remembered cameras.

Callum watched the tracker.

*Last Good Haul* moved another fraction toward Der Himmelskrug.

Somewhere on that old ship, Sven Andersen had heard twenty-five million credits and tried to pretend the number had not touched him.

Somewhere on that old ship, Freya Andersen had already done the math.

Somewhere in that old ship's bay, *Opportunity Knocks* waited in the dark, famous enough to be dangerous before she ever powered up.

Callum closed the tracker.

"Problem?" Ilya asked.

"No."

"You looked like you had one."

"I was thinking about the ship."

"The racing ship?"

"Yes."

"What about it?"

Callum picked up his coat.

"People keep calling it collateral."

"It is collateral."

"No," Callum said. "It is leverage. Collateral sits still."

Ilya considered that.

Then she nodded once.

They left the logistics room separately, two ordinary people stepping into the outer transit tier's evening flow. Around them, Kestrel Minor continued its routine of selling fuel, hot noodles, replacement seals, debt extensions, and small departures to people who had not yet learned what was following them.

At the BrightSpan Local gate, a crowd had already gathered under a fresh temporary sign.

DER HIMMELSKRUG RUN CONNECTIONS  
ADDITIONAL PASSENGER CAPACITY ADDED  
EXPECT FESTIVAL SCREENING DELAYS

Ilya looked at the sign.

"Festival screening delays," she said.

Callum adjusted the collar of Marek Sova's jacket. "Joy has paperwork."

"That sounds like Der Himmelskrug."

"That is why we arrive as joy."

They joined the line with three passengers between them.

That was habit.

Partners who stood too close became a pattern. Partners who stood too far apart lost timing. Three people between them made them strangers with shared destination pressure, which was what half the line already was.

Ahead of Callum, a pair of young mechanics argued about Kaltmond. Behind Ilya, an older woman in a race scarf told a child that no, physics could not be bribed, even with very good cake. A vendor dragged three sealed crates marked novelty legal foam toward oversized baggage. Someone had already printed a shirt with THE LAST POUR WILL GET YOU IF YOU GET COCKY, which Callum considered both ugly and probably true.

The crowd moved slowly.

Arrival would be logged.

Faces would be scanned.

Bags would be screened.

Names would be checked against lists.

None of that worried him.

Not yet.

Systems were built to catch people who looked like threats before they entered.

Callum and Ilya had spent years learning how to look like paperwork until the room was too small for anyone else to breathe.

At the gate, the attendant scanned Callum's ticket.

"Marek Sova?"

"Yes."

"Purpose of travel?"

Callum looked past her toward the route display, where Der Himmelskrug spun in promotional miniature beside the words WELCOME TO THE RUN.

He let himself smile the way ordinary people smiled when they wanted to be mistaken for harmless.

"The race," he said.

The attendant waved him through.

Three passengers later, Ilya became Tilen Voss and said something forgettable about vintage heat exchangers.

The tracker kept moving toward Der Himmelskrug.

So did they.

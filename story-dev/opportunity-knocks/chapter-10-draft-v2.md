# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 10 Draft v2

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## Systems With Teeth

Security did not arrive like heroes.

That helped.

Heroes wanted a room to know they had entered it. They ran when walking would do, shouted when procedure could carry, and made frightened people watch them become important.

Der Himmelskrug Security arrived like a door deciding to be locked.

The first two officers stopped at the far end of Crew Support corridor eight and took the space apart with their eyes. One stayed near the Medical/Emergency Core entrance. One moved two steps toward the Garage/Service Core door and no farther. Neither drew a weapon. Neither touched Marek Sova. Neither touched Tilen Voss.

The corridor did not need touching yet.

It needed sorting.

“Everyone stay where you are,” the lead officer said again.

Her nameplate read REYES. Her voice was calm in the way calm became when it had been trained, inspected, and used in worse rooms.

“Andersen family party, remain by the blue line. Crew Support personnel, hold witness positions. Public visitors, hands visible. No slate use. No bag contact. No route changes.”

Marek Sova lifted both hands slightly.

Not surrender.

Compliance.

Temporary, calculated, insultingly neat.

“This is a misunderstanding,” he said.

Officer Reyes looked at him for less than a second, which seemed to annoy him more than argument would have.

“Noted,” she said.

Beside him, Tilen Voss—no, Ilya, because he had given that name away and the corridor had heard it—kept her hands away from her jacket and her eyes on exits she no longer owned.

Pia Stern stood with her tool case beside the maintenance transition, one boot angled to keep her sightline open.

Calder Yim stood with his Crew Support slate held low, thumb no longer moving. He looked less frightened than Sven expected.

Or not less frightened.

More practiced at using frightened correctly.

Freya stood beside Sven on the blue line, hand still in his.

The line glowed gently under their boots.

Sven had never been given a floor instruction before. Not like this. Not one that knew his name without saying it, that said stand here and meant we know where the danger is not because you are imagining it.

Behind the frosted privacy glass, Ingrid had drawn Nora back from the door.

Nora was no longer visible.

Sven’s chest hurt with gratitude so sharp it felt like another kind of threat.

Marek said, “Officer, my colleague and I are public visitors. We have not entered a restricted zone.”

Reyes looked at the floor line.

Then at the route light.

Then at the visitor pins.

“No one said restricted,” she said. “The current issue is controlled-route interference.”

“This is a private legal communication.”

“It was a private legal communication,” Pia said, “until you did it in a crew-family corridor with your body blocking half the room.”

Reyes did not look at Pia.

“Witness statement held,” she said.

Pia nodded once.

Marek’s jaw shifted.

Not enough to be anger.

Enough to stop pretending he was only disappointed.

A second station voice came from the ceiling, lower and drier.

“Reyes, this is Okonkwo. Corridor command is mine.”

Officer Reyes touched two fingers to her comm. “Confirmed, Chief.”

Sven had never met Janelle Okonkwo.

He had seen her name once on a safety notice in the public arrival packet. Chief of Station Security. Public-area enforcement. Weapons checks. Crew-service corridor access. Festival safety.

The notice had made her sound like a function.

The voice made her sound like a person who had once watched someone try the wrong door and remembered how badly that had gone for them.

“Crew Support,” Janelle said, “status of child and elder?”

Calder answered before Sven could.

“Protected inside family waiting room. Visual shield in place. Ingrid Andersen with Nora Andersen. No corridor exposure.”

“Good. Keisha?”

Keisha Bell’s voice entered the channel. “I have family transfer route ready. Temporary lodging privacy lock is live. Nora does not need this hallway as a memory.”

Sven’s hand tightened around Freya’s.

Janelle said, “Correct. Reyes, move the Andersen adults when Crew Support is ready. Take statements in protected space, not corridor theatre.”

Marek’s eyes flicked toward Sven.

Corridor theatre.

The phrase had found him and named the thing he had tried to make.

Good.

Janelle continued. “Public visitors Marek Sova and Tilen Voss remain in place pending identity, access, and safety review.”

Marek said, “Chief Okonkwo, I am willing to cooperate, but I need to understand whether we are being detained.”

“You are.”

“On what grounds?”

“Station safety authority.”

“We have made no threat.”

“You are in a controlled-adjacent crew-support corridor using creditor language toward a crew-family party connected to a protected ship in Garage Ops custody. Movement was blocked. A private financial detail was spoken in a support corridor. A witness report was initiated before Security arrival. That is enough for station safety detention while we sort identity, authorization, and evidence.”

Marek looked toward the ceiling.

There was no face there.

Only the station answering.

“That is a broad interpretation,” he said.

“Yes,” Janelle said.

The corridor became quiet enough for Sven to hear the laundry desk vent cycle.

Marek blinked.

Janelle added, “Broad is useful before narrow facts arrive.”

Freya breathed out through her nose.

It was not quite a laugh.

It was the sound she made when a spreadsheet proved someone else had been lying first.

Reyes stepped half a pace closer to Sven and Freya, still leaving them space.

“Shipmaster Andersen. Mrs. Andersen. Crew Support will move you now.”

Sven looked at Marek.

He hated that he did.

Marek was not looking at him anymore.

That should have felt better.

It did not.

“*Opportunity Knocks*,” Sven said.

Reyes answered immediately. “Garage Ops confirms no powered movement, no creditor access, no public status, no release authorization. Your ship remains under Garage Ops custody.”

Sven closed his eyes.

Just for one second.

Freya’s thumb moved once against his hand.

Not a rescue.

A count.

One second, then back.

He opened his eyes.

“Thank you.”

Reyes gave him a small nod.

“Thank Garage Ops when they allow gratitude near tools.”

Freya said, “They don’t?”

“Rarely unsupervised.”

Under different circumstances, Sven might have smiled.

Keisha appeared from the Crew Support side door with two staff members and a privacy screen cart that looked like a folded wall on quiet wheels. The cart unfolded in two panels, not blocking Security sightlines but cutting off the family room from the corridor.

“Nora is with Ingrid,” Keisha said. “She has been told there is boring adult hallway sorting. Moon-bear has accepted jurisdiction.”

Freya’s mouth trembled once.

Then held.

“Can I go to her?”

“Yes,” Keisha said. “That is not retreat. That is parenting in the right room.”

Freya looked as if she might argue.

Keisha looked back as if she had already planned where the argument would be safely stored.

Freya nodded.

Sven started to follow.

Then stopped.

Marek was still there.

Ilya was still there.

The corridor was still shaped around them.

Shame tried to find its old place in his chest and sit down.

Reyes saw him hesitate.

“Shipmaster,” she said quietly, “statements happen after safety.”

“I know.”

“Knowing is not always doing.”

“No.”

“Do safety first.”

Freya’s hand tightened. “Sven.”

He went with her.

The blue line guided them three meters to the family waiting-room door, then bent toward a side route lit in the same soft blue. A Crew Support staffer opened the privacy screen just enough.

Inside, Ingrid stood with Nora against her hip.

Nora was not crying.

That was worse in a way.

Her eyes were too large, and moon-bear was tucked under her chin like a shield with ears.

“Papa?” she asked.

Sven crouched before he knew he had decided to.

“We’re moving rooms,” he said.

“Because of hallway sorting?”

“Yes.”

“Did you do a towel crime?”

Freya made a sound that almost broke.

Sven looked at Ingrid.

Ingrid looked back as if to say: I chose the available lie and it was excellent.

“Yes,” Sven said solemnly. “But I am cooperating.”

Nora considered this.

“Good.”

Keisha said, “The new room has a door, a table, four beds, a washroom, and no public listing.”

Nora looked at her. “Does moon-bear have to sign?”

“No,” Keisha said. “Moon-bear is covered under family privacy.”

Nora nodded. “He likes that.”

“So do we.”

The family moved.

Not far.

Far enough.

The route did not use the public atrium. It did not pass the corridor where Marek and Ilya stood. It turned through a Crew Support side passage, past laundry carts, a staff hydration point, a wall of clean folded blankets, and a Medical direction sign that smelled faintly of antiseptic when the door seal breathed.

Sven looked back once.

Not because he wanted to.

Because fear had not yet learned the new route.

Pia was still visible through the narrowing gap, tool case in one hand, face turned toward Security.

Calder was speaking to Reyes now, calm and precise.

Marek stood with his hands visible.

Ilya watched the route lights.

Then the door closed.

The corridor disappeared.

Nora breathed better when it did.

That was the only evidence Sven needed that Keisha had been right.

The temporary lodging room was plain, clean, and small in a way that felt expensive because it did not ask them to admire it. Four narrow bunks folded from one wall. A table locked to the floor. A washroom door. A soft panel that could become a privacy divider. A kettle already steaming. A station slate on the table showing:

CREW SUPPORT HOLDOVER  
PRIVATE / FAMILY ACCESS ONLY  
NO PUBLIC REGISTRY  
NO MEDIA ACCESS  
NO VISITOR ACCESS WITHOUT CONSENT AND STAFF APPROVAL

Freya read it before sitting.

Of course she did.

Ingrid read her face.

Then read the slate.

Then nodded once.

“This room has good bones,” Ingrid said.

Nora climbed onto the lowest bunk with moon-bear, then looked at Sven.

“Are we in trouble?”

Sven opened his mouth.

Too many answers arrived.

Yes.

No.

Not the way you think.

Not because of the towel.

Not because of the ship.

Not because of you.

Freya sat beside Nora.

“No,” she said. “Some adults tried to make their problem into our problem in a hallway. The station noticed. Now we are in the room where our family waits while the right people sort it.”

Nora frowned.

“Were they bad adults?”

Ingrid said, “They were adults behaving badly enough that other adults had to become very organized.”

Nora accepted this with the gravity of a person who had been organized against her will many times.

“Do we still get noodles?”

Keisha, at the door, said, “Yes.”

Nora nodded.

“Then the room is okay.”

Keisha looked at Sven and Freya. “A statement team will come here. Not a crowd. One Security, one Crew Support, and, if needed, Compliance. Nora does not need details. Medical is available if anyone’s body decides to be dramatic after being brave.”

Ingrid said, “Bodies often do that.”

“They do,” Keisha said. “Rude, but common.”

Sven looked at the door.

“What happens to them?”

Keisha’s expression did not change much.

Enough to become official.

“Security is sorting identity and safety status. Compliance is being notified. If the facts go where they appear to be going, station evidence will move to the IFP liaison for handoff review.”

Freya said, “IFP?”

“Likely,” Keisha said. “Station Security can detain. It does not run a prison and it does not try major cross-jurisdiction matters alone.”

Sven heard the sentence as a kind of mercy.

Not for Marek.

Not for Ilya.

For everyone else.

A station that knew where its own authority ended was more frightening than one that pretended not to.

Back in corridor eight, Marek Sova attempted to become paper again.

“I am not refusing cooperation,” he said.

Officer Reyes stood three paces away.

Another Security officer, older and broader, had taken position near Ilya. His nameplate read TAN. He had the patient face of a person who could wait all day and would bill the time to civic order.

Marek continued, “I am simply asserting that any review of our travel purpose should account for the fact that we entered through public Arrival Control, submitted to screening, declared no weapons, and remained outside restricted thresholds.”

Reyes looked at him.

Then at the route log on her slate.

“You declared public festival attendance.”

“Yes.”

“You did not declare creditor-contact purpose, contractor purpose, asset-protection purpose, legal-service purpose, private enforcement interest, or intent to contact a crew-family party under station support.”

Marek smiled faintly. “Visitors have conversations.”

“Visitors ask where the taproom is.”

“Tilen” spoke for the first time since Security arrived.

“The taproom is visible from public arrival.”

Tan looked at her.

“So is the exit,” he said.

She did not answer.

Reyes touched her comm. “Chief, public identities confirm Arrival Control release. No weapons, no restricted tools, no contractor declaration.”

Janelle said, “Route history?”

Reyes checked the overlay.

“Public H1 release. Event pillar. Retail pass. H2 food hall. Repeated proximity to controlled-route signage. Two failed public-map searches for pilot support. One staff question logged as ‘lost visitor, asking whether crew messages can be delivered.’ Staff refused and directed them to public information. Later presence near Crew Support corridor eight edge.”

Marek said, “Asking a question is not a violation.”

“No,” Janelle said through the comm. “Patterns get to have friends.”

Pia made a small sound.

Reyes looked at her.

Pia said, “Cough.”

“No, it wasn’t,” Tan said.

Pia shrugged.

Calder stepped forward when Reyes indicated.

He gave his report without embellishment, which made it worse for Marek.

“Two public visitors were positioned near the Services threshold without visible escort. Male visitor engaged Shipmaster Andersen while Mrs. Andersen was at the Crew Support counter. Female visitor used the public information panel as a standing cover and narrowed the line between Shipmaster Andersen and the counter. Shipmaster Andersen appeared reluctant to create a scene. Conversation included preservation-interest language, collateral, Northstar, voluntary resolution, and a request for signature. Mrs. Andersen challenged authority. Both visitors failed to present station authorization. Male visitor used private financial detail in the corridor.”

Reyes nodded.

“Your alert timestamp?”

Calder gave it.

“Your reason for alert?”

“Geometry first,” Calder said. “Language second.”

Janelle’s voice came through. “Explain.”

Calder looked at Marek, then at Ilya, then at the corridor.

“People having a private conversation make exits available because they want privacy. People applying pressure make exits feel expensive.”

No one spoke for a second.

Then Janelle said, “Useful. Keep that sentence.”

Calder nodded.

Pia gave her statement next.

She did not know the legal machinery.

She said so.

“I heard creditor words. I saw visitor pins. I saw one person talking and one person placing herself where the family route narrowed. I saw Mrs. Andersen ask them to stop positioning. I saw the woman move anyway. I saw the man say private legal matter after two staff had line of sight. That is not a private matter. That is a controlled-route interference pattern.”

Reyes logged it.

Marek said, “That is interpretation.”

Pia looked at him.

“Yes,” she said. “I maintain machines for a living. Interpretation is what keeps pressure from becoming shrapnel.”

Tan’s mouth moved once.

Not a smile.

A maintenance of face.

Janelle said, “Compliance is joining.”

Mara Venn’s voice entered a moment later, clipped and clean.

“Compliance listening.”

Marek looked upward again.

Sven had looked that way too, earlier.

Reyes wondered if Der Himmelskrug made people discover ceilings because they were not used to buildings that answered back.

Mara said, “Public visitors under false or incomplete business posture, possible undisclosed asset-protection contact, creditor-pressure language, protected crew-family party, operationally private craft in Garage Ops custody. Confirm no station authorization for creditor contact.”

Reyes checked.

“None found.”

“Confirm no registered Northstar or Mirador station-side appointment, filing, inquiry, Compliance notice, or legal-service check-in associated with Marek Sova or Tilen Voss.”

A pause.

“Confirmed.”

Marek said, “You have not established that we are Mirador.”

Mara answered before Reyes could.

“Correct. We have established that your declared visitor purpose does not cover your conduct, that you used creditor-aligned language near a controlled Services route, and that you approached a protected party outside station process. Identity depth comes next.”

That was when Ilya looked at Marek.

Not much.

Enough.

Reyes saw it.

Tan saw it.

The corridor cameras saw it.

Janelle likely saw the shape of it in the silence.

“Route and identity review continues,” Janelle said. “Garage Ops?”

Oskar Teague’s voice arrived with the displeasure of a man who had been asked to speak to Security while useful work existed.

“Garage Ops.”

“Status of *Opportunity Knocks*.”

“Powered down. Garage custody. Privacy lock. No movement authorization. No creditor access. No public status. No external data access beyond approved maintenance archive. Also, if anyone asks whether a visitor pin grants berth access, I will retire by violence of paperwork.”

“Thank you, Oskar.”

“I was not joking.”

“I know.”

Marek watched the exchange with the expression of a man discovering that departments with boundaries could still agree quickly.

Janelle said, “Flight Control?”

Amara Reid answered, warm until the steel showed under it.

“Flight Control confirms no ship-release, race-practice, flight-window, traffic, docking, towing, transponder, or course authorization exists for any creditor, contractor, public visitor, or alleged asset-protection party connected to *Opportunity Knocks*. Joy does not modify traffic law. Debt does not either.”

“Thank you,” Janelle said.

Amara added, “If someone tries to turn a lien into a release window, I want their name.”

“You may have several.”

“Excellent.”

Marek said, “No one requested a release window.”

Janelle said, “Noted. We are ruling out doors before someone claims not to have touched them.”

The phrase seemed to bother him.

Good.

The station was no longer arguing inside his chosen frame.

It was building its own.

Then Calder said, “Chief?”

“Go.”

“They knew too much.”

Reyes looked at him.

Calder continued, “They knew Shipmaster Andersen was separated from Garage Ops but not public. They knew the family’s support status. They knew enough to time the conversation near lodging review. That may be observation, but the pattern started before corridor eight.”

Marek became very still.

Ilya did not.

That was the difference.

Stillness could be defense.

Looking unchanged could be training.

Janelle said, “Security analysis, pull visitor proximity history against Andersen support movements. Compliance, check public feed exposure. Garage Ops, confirm privacy-lock leak status. Port Services, check for non-station tracking signals attached to lower-handle arrivals or cargo route.”

Oskar made a sound like someone had just put a filthy boot on his floor.

“Tracker?” he said.

“Checking,” Janelle said.

Sven was not in the corridor to hear the word.

Freya was.

Not directly.

The protected Crew Support room had a wall slate with incident status reduced to family-safe language. Keisha had set it that way on purpose.

But Freya saw Keisha glance once at her own slate.

She knew that glance.

It was the look of a new column appearing.

“What?” Freya asked.

Keisha looked at her.

Decided not to soften into fog.

“They are checking how the visitors found the timing.”

Freya sat straighter.

“A tracker?”

“Possibly.”

Sven looked from Keisha to Freya.

The room seemed to lose a degree of warmth.

Ingrid put one hand on Nora’s blanket where the child sat drawing moon-bear in what appeared to be a legal robe.

Nora did not look up.

Good.

Freya said, “The freighter.”

Sven closed his eyes.

He saw Kestrel Minor’s tired docking yard.

The old cargo bay.

The patched panels.

The moment he had told himself the ship was old, tired, and lying only in the ways he could hear.

He had listened for engine trouble.

For seals.

For late answers.

He had not listened for being followed.

“Not confirmed,” Keisha said.

Sven opened his eyes.

“Do not spend shame before evidence arrives,” she added.

Freya looked at him.

“Listen to her.”

“I hate that everyone here has good sentences,” Sven said.

Ingrid said, “It is suspicious.”

Nora looked up. “What is suspicious?”

“Adults being useful,” Ingrid said.

Nora nodded and returned to moon-bear’s legal robes.

In Garage Ops, Oskar had already moved.

He did not like trackers for several reasons.

First, because they were rude.

Second, because they implied someone had thought his floor was part of their private hunting map.

Third, because if the tracker had ridden in on *Last Good Haul* or near *Opportunity Knocks*, then something had been talking inside his clean-side process without permission, and Oskar Teague disliked unauthorized speech from objects.

Pavel and Kiri joined the sweep. Meera pulled lower-handle intake telemetry. Garage Ops did not open cargo systems, move the ship, or touch anything not already authorized. They used intake records, passive scans, dockside sensor history, and the freighter’s declared access map.

The tracker announced itself by being almost boring.

Not active enough to shout.

Not silent enough to be innocent.

A little relay seed tucked near an exterior cargo-frame repair on *Last Good Haul*, placed where a hurried inspection would call it another ugly patch in a family of ugly patches.

Pavel found the signal history first.

Kiri found the physical anomaly second.

Oskar stared at the display.

“Well,” he said.

Meera waited.

Oskar said, “I am choosing a professional word.”

Pavel said, “How long does that take?”

“Longer if interrupted.”

Kiri highlighted the relay’s last ping pattern. “It tracked lower vector arrival. Low-power burst after docking. No penetration of station systems. It did not access Garage Ops. It did not find *Opportunity Knocks* directly after intake lock. But it could confirm the freighter reached lower-handle custody.”

Oskar’s expression flattened.

“Security gets this.”

“Already packaging,” Meera said.

“And Compliance.”

“Already copied.”

“And I want the object isolated without making it interesting.”

Pavel nodded. “Evidence bag, passive containment, no drama.”

“Correct. If anyone calls it cute, they clean filters.”

“No one thinks trackers are cute, Chief.”

“You would be surprised what junior mechanics romanticize near famous ships.”

Security received the tracker packet three minutes later.

Janelle read the summary once.

Then she went quiet.

Officer Reyes knew that quiet from training.

It meant the incident had grown teeth.

“Public visitors Marek Sova and Tilen Voss,” Janelle said over corridor command, “you are being detained under station safety authority pending transfer review with the IFP liaison. Grounds include controlled-route interference, suspected false-purpose entry, coercive contact with a protected crew-family party, and evidence of pre-arrival tracking connected to the same party’s vessel.”

Marek said, “That is an extraordinary escalation.”

“No,” Janelle said. “It is an ordinary escalation with good documentation.”

“You have no basis to connect us to any alleged tracker.”

“Then preserve that version for the IFP interview.”

For the first time, Ilya spoke with something like irritation.

“Are we under arrest?”

Janelle’s voice remained even.

“You are detained by Station Security. The IFP liaison receives the full evidence package. Station Security does not run a prison.”

Marek’s mouth moved.

Janelle finished before he could.

“We run a station. That is why you are leaving in hand restraints.”

Officer Tan stepped forward.

Not fast.

Not cruel.

Just there.

Reyes addressed both visitors. “Turn slowly. Hands visible.”

Marek looked at Ilya.

Ilya gave him nothing.

No reassurance.

No blame.

No plan in public.

They turned.

Security applied restraints designed for station corridors: visible, nonviolent, hard to use as leverage, and humiliating only if a person had arrived believing they were untouchable.

Pia watched.

Calder watched.

The pilot near the shower queue watched and then looked away when the looking stopped being useful.

Marek said, “This will be reviewed.”

Janelle answered, “Yes.”

That seemed to displease him most of all.

Review was less useful when the people detaining you had invited it before you could threaten them with it.

In the protected Crew Support room, Keisha received the detention notice and read it silently.

Freya watched her face.

Sven watched Freya.

Ingrid watched all of them and Nora’s drawing at once, because mothers and grandmothers were apparently issued extra channels by whatever cruel engineer had designed families.

Keisha set the slate down.

“They are detained,” she said. “Station Security is preserving evidence for IFP handoff. A tracker was found associated with *Last Good Haul*. It did not breach Garage Ops systems or reach *Opportunity Knocks* after intake lock.”

Sven sat down.

He had not meant to.

The chair arrived under him because station furniture, unlike his pride, had excellent timing.

Freya’s hand went to her mouth.

Then down.

She did not cry.

Not yet.

Ingrid said something in German under her breath that Sven had last heard when a freight broker tried to charge Lars for air he had already paid to breathe.

Nora looked up.

“Grandma?”

Ingrid smiled at her.

It was not soft.

It was safe.

“Adult word,” Ingrid said.

“Bad one?”

“Useful one.”

Nora considered this and returned to moon-bear, who now appeared to be holding a tiny sign that said NO.

Keisha crouched near Sven’s chair, not too close.

“Shipmaster Andersen.”

He looked at her.

“That tracker is evidence of something done to you. Not by you.”

His throat closed.

Freya came to him then.

Not fast.

Not dramatic.

She sat beside him and put her shoulder against his.

“You hear her?” she asked.

He nodded.

“Say it.”

“I hate that.”

“I know.”

He looked at the floor.

“The tracker is evidence of something done to us,” he said.

Keisha waited.

Sven exhaled.

“Not by us.”

“Good,” Freya said.

Ingrid said, “Again later.”

Sven almost laughed.

It hurt.

But not as much as it should have.

Keisha stood. “Statements next. We can do them one at a time, with breaks. Nora does not need the hallway version. Ingrid, if you want to sit with her while Freya and Sven speak—”

“I will,” Ingrid said.

Nora looked up again. “Are Mama and Papa going to boring adult hallway sorting?”

“Boring adult room sorting,” Freya said.

“Is it about the towel?”

Sven looked at the towel in the corner of the room.

Someone had brought it.

Evidence of towel crimes.

Maybe Crew Support had a protocol for ridiculous objects that kept families attached to themselves.

“Yes,” he said. “A little.”

Nora nodded gravely. “Tell the truth.”

“I will.”

The first statement team arrived five minutes later: Officer Reyes, Calder as the Crew Support witness liaison, and Mara Venn on a secure slate from Compliance because apparently some people could make a screen feel like a clipboard with judgment.

They did not crowd the room.

They did not ask Nora questions.

They did not ask about Lars’s medical details, except to mark that private medical debt existed and was not public content.

They did not ask Sven to prove he had been frightened.

They asked what happened.

They asked who spoke first.

They asked where Ilya stood.

They asked when Freya arrived.

They asked what words Marek used.

They asked whether anyone touched him.

They asked whether anyone blocked movement.

They asked whether anyone mentioned the debt number.

They asked whether he understood the document.

He said no.

Then corrected himself.

“I understood what he wanted me to feel.”

Mara looked up from the slate.

“That is a useful distinction.”

Sven nodded.

“He wanted me to feel like station process would hurt my family unless I signed first.”

Mara typed.

Freya’s statement was sharper.

She named the questions Marek had refused to answer. She named Mirador. She named Northstar’s language. She named the difference between lawful notice and hallway pressure. She named every place where he had tried to make fear sound like adult responsibility.

Mara listened with the focused stillness of a woman who appreciated a clean file.

At the end, Mara said, “Mrs. Andersen, I am going to say this carefully. A creditor may have remedies. A contractor may have paperwork. Neither of those is station authorization. Neither permits coercive contact in controlled support space. Neither moves a ship in Garage Ops custody. Neither overrides Flight Control. Neither makes your child part of a leverage strategy.”

Freya held her gaze.

“Thank you.”

“That was not comfort,” Mara said. “That was jurisdiction.”

Freya’s face changed.

“Still. Thank you.”

Mara accepted this with a tiny nod.

Pia’s statement came from the corridor first, then from a side room. She refused a chair until someone told her refusing chairs was not a labor principle. Calder’s report attached cleanly. The laundry-desk staffer added the corridor audio timestamp. The pilot near the shower queue gave a short statement beginning with I was trying not to listen and ending with but then he said the number.

That mattered.

Private harm had entered a shared space because Marek had put it there.

Der Himmelskrug did not make it content.

It made it evidence.

By the time Janelle Okonkwo came to the protected room, Marek Sova and Tilen Voss had been moved to Security holding under escort.

Janelle did not arrive with drama either.

She entered after Keisha, waited for Nora to be behind the privacy divider with Ingrid and a fresh noodle bowl, and then spoke to Sven and Freya at the table.

She was tall, composed, and tired in a way that did not reduce her. Her hair was pulled back. Her uniform had no theatrical shine. Her eyes missed very little and did not brag about it.

“Shipmaster Andersen. Mrs. Andersen.”

Sven started to stand.

Janelle shook her head once.

“Stay seated. Standing is not cooperation. It is just vertical anxiety.”

Freya’s mouth twitched.

Sven sat.

Janelle placed a slate on the table.

“I want to be clear about what has happened and what has not happened.”

Freya leaned forward.

Sven tried to.

Freya put one hand flat on the table between them.

Not holding him down.

Reminding him the table existed.

Janelle said, “The two public visitors are detained under station authority. Their declared identities are under review. Their declared purpose does not match their conduct. Their contact with you is being treated as a station safety incident, controlled-route interference, and potential coercive asset-pressure attempt. The tracker found on *Last Good Haul* is evidence. It is being preserved. It did not give them access to *Opportunity Knocks*.”

Sven breathed.

Janelle saw that.

“Your ship remains in Garage Ops custody. No creditor access. No public status. No movement. No release window. No practice window. No one has changed that.”

Freya said, “And Northstar?”

“Compliance is preparing notice that any lawful claim must come through proper channels. The IFP liaison will receive the evidence package. If Northstar wants to explain what it did or did not authorize, there will be a formal place to do that.”

Freya looked down.

Sven realized she was smiling.

Not happily.

Legally.

Janelle continued. “Your family’s temporary lodging is active. Privacy lock remains. No media contact. No public guest registry. Security will post quiet route protection outside this room and coordinate with Crew Support for any movement.”

Sven said, “I’m sorry.”

Freya turned toward him.

Keisha, at the door, closed her eyes.

Janelle looked at him for one long second.

“No,” she said.

The word did not rise.

It did not need to.

Sven swallowed.

Janelle said, “You may be frightened. You may be angry. You may be tired. You may later remember something useful and tell us. You may need help explaining some of this to your daughter. You may not apologize for being targeted in my station.”

His eyes burned.

He hated that too.

Freya’s hand found his under the table.

Janelle looked at both of them.

“Do you understand?”

Freya said, “Yes.”

Sven said, “I am trying to.”

“Acceptable.”

From behind the privacy divider, Nora’s voice said, “Grandma, does moon-bear need route protection?”

Ingrid answered, “Moon-bear is considered emotionally strategic cargo.”

Janelle’s eyes shifted toward Keisha.

Keisha said, “I did not teach that exact phrase.”

“You will now,” Janelle said.

The room breathed.

Not easier.

Not safe in the storybook way.

Safe in the way of doors, names, logs, witnesses, food, route lights, people who knew their jobs, and a station that did not mistake kindness for looking away.

Janelle stood.

“The IFP liaison will be notified. Security holding will retain the visitors until transfer review. Compliance will package the creditor-contact and tracker evidence. Crew Support remains your contact for family needs. Garage Ops remains your contact for the ship. Flight Control remains the answer to any question involving movement, practice, release, traffic, or someone trying to staple a lien to physics.”

Sven almost smiled.

“Does that happen often?”

Janelle’s face did not change.

“People try many crafts.”

Freya did smile then.

Just a little.

Janelle picked up her slate.

“One more thing.”

Sven looked at her.

“If anyone contacts you directly about the ship, the debt, the race, your lodging, your daughter, your mother, your father’s medical history, your family finances, or anything that feels like a private room forming around you, you call Crew Support or Security. You do not decide whether it is important first. That is our job.”

Freya said, “He will.”

Janelle looked at Sven.

“He will say it.”

Sven looked at the table.

Then at Freya.

Then toward the privacy divider where Nora was apparently arguing that moon-bear’s legal sign needed glitter.

“I will call Crew Support or Security,” he said. “I will not decide alone first.”

“Good.”

Janelle left.

The door sealed softly behind her.

For a moment, no one at the table moved.

Then Keisha set a fresh pot of tea down.

“Tea,” she said.

Freya looked at it.

“Is this required?”

“No. But people answer questions better when their hands remember warmth.”

Sven took a cup.

The warmth was real.

That did not fix anything.

It did not pay the debt. It did not repair the ship. It did not erase the tracker, or the corridor, or the way Marek had placed seven and a half million credits between them like a blade.

But it gave his hands something to do other than shake.

Below them, in the clean-side Starship Garage, *Opportunity Knocks* remained on handling pads under lights that did not care about creditors.

In Security holding, two public visitors with borrowed names waited for IFP transfer review.

In Compliance, Mara Venn began turning a hallway into a case file.

In Crew Support, Nora drew moon-bear’s route-protection badge too large and insisted that glitter was a safety color.

And in the middle of all of it, Der Himmelskrug kept doing what it had been built to do.

Not rescue people from consequences.

Not forgive debts by being kind.

Not turn fear into entertainment.

It made process around harm.

It made witnesses out of workers.

It made doors mean what they said.

It made private pressure answer to public rules.

Sven held the tea in both hands and listened to the station breathe.

For the first time since the corridor, the sound did not feel like machinery.

It felt like teeth closing in the right direction.

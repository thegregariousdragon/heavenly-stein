# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 19 Draft v1

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## Second Breakfast

Freya Andersen looked at the sale packet for *Last Good Haul* and said, “This may be too honest to sell.”

Sven glanced up from the maintenance appendix.

“That sounds like a feature.”

“It is twelve pages of reasons to be careful.”

“Good.”

“It has a fuel-efficiency warning table.”

“It deserves one.”

“It says the cargo-bay door is repaired and tested, but still has an acoustic profile that may cause concern in unfamiliar operators.”

Sven winced. “That is generous.”

“It says the power instability has been corrected within safe operating tolerances but should be monitored before long-haul use.”

“That is true.”

“It says not recommended for high-margin luxury contracts, time-critical medical freight, aggressive speculative hauling, or owners who confuse sentiment with preventive maintenance.”

Sven looked across the buyer review room at Oskar Teague.

Oskar did not look up from his slate.

“I improved that sentence,” Oskar said.

Freya looked back at the packet.

“You improved it from what?”

“No.”

“Oskar.”

“No.”

Sven leaned closer to Freya and whispered, “I think we should accept the gift of not knowing.”

The buyer review room sat beside the Starship Garage’s clean-side commercial-transfer office. It had a table, four chairs, a wall display, a privacy indicator, and a view through reinforced interior glass toward the controlled berth where *Last Good Haul* waited under working lights.

The freighter looked better than it had any right to.

Not young.

Not pretty.

Not transformed.

Better.

Panels sealed. Cargo door seated. Registry lights repaired. Utility scarring cleaned but not hidden. The old hull still wore its work history plainly, which Freya preferred. Polished lies had nearly cost them everything.

The sale packet did not polish either.

Repaired systems.

Remaining limitations.

Maintenance schedule.

Safe-use envelope.

Known sounds.

Known smells.

Known refusal to be hurried in low-temperature startup.

Known habit of making new pilots believe the port vent was muttering, which Sven insisted was not technically a fault.

Freya read the price page again.

The number still looked strange.

They could have asked for more.

Not enormously more. *Last Good Haul* was still an old freighter with a list of cautions and a personality like a tired dockworker arguing with weather. But post-race attention had inflated interest in anything connected to the Andersen family. Some collector with more money than ethics would have paid for the story and ignored the ship.

Freya had refused three such inquiries before breakfast.

One had used the phrase authentic hardship provenance.

Feli had answered that one personally.

Freya did not know exactly what Feli had said.

She only knew the inquiry had not returned.

Oskar moved to the next page.

“The disclosure packet is clear.”

Freya looked at him.

“That is not the same as good.”

“Clear is a kind of good that survives paperwork.”

Sven murmured, “That sounds like something Freya would put on a mug.”

Freya said, “No mugs.”

Oskar’s slate chimed.

He checked it.

“Prospective buyers are here.”

Freya’s hand tightened on the packet.

Sven saw it.

“You can still say no.”

“I know.”

“Do you?”

Freya looked through the glass at the freighter.

For so long, *Last Good Haul* had been the thing they could not afford to keep and could not survive losing. It had been business, shelter, shame, transport, memory, and emergency plan. It had carried the ship that saved them, which made the freighter important in ways money could not sort cleanly.

But it was not their future anymore.

That did not make letting go simple.

The door opened.

Keisha Bell entered first, because Crew Support had opinions about transitions involving families, large feelings, and old ships.

Behind her came Tamsin Orr and Keo Orr.

Tamsin was tall, spare, and careful in the way people became when every credit had been taught to report for duty. Their jacket was clean but mended at both cuffs. Their boots were practical. Their eyes went first to the room, then the exits, then the packet on the table, then the freighter through the glass.

Keo, who looked maybe fifteen or sixteen, tried very hard not to look immediately at the ship.

They failed.

Sven liked them for it.

Keo wore a Brindlehook Junction patch on one sleeve and had a coil of diagnostic tags clipped to their belt, not because they needed them in a buyer review room, but because teenagers with mechanical curiosity liked to keep proof of identity close.

Keisha made introductions.

“Tamsin Orr. Keo Orr. Sven Andersen, Freya Andersen, Ingrid Andersen, Nora Andersen. Oskar Teague representing Garage Ops inspection status.”

Nora stood beside Ingrid with moon-bear under one arm. She had insisted on attending because “the old ship should know the family came,” and Freya had not found a reason good enough to say no.

Tamsin nodded to each of them.

“Thank you for seeing us.”

Freya gestured to the chairs.

“Thank you for reading the packet before asking to see the ship.”

Tamsin’s mouth twitched.

“That obvious?”

“Rare.”

Keo sat carefully, then blurted, “The port vent note was very helpful.”

Sven looked at them.

Keo flushed.

“I mean, I would have thought it was a pressure flutter.”

“It sounds like one,” Sven said. “It is actually three old brackets and a vent lip that resents authority. The pressure flutter is lower.”

Keo’s eyes lit.

Tamsin gave them a look.

Keo pressed their lips together.

Sven said, “That was a good question.”

Keo relaxed by one millimeter.

Freya watched Tamsin watch the room.

Not greedy.

Not dazzled.

Not trying to make the freighter into a legend because it had once carried a famous racing craft.

Cautious.

Good.

Tamsin opened their copy of the packet.

“We are not looking for beautiful,” they said. “We are looking for a ship that can move clean freight between Brindlehook, Kestrel Minor, and the smaller repair stops without eating us alive.”

Freya nodded.

“Then the packet is the beginning of the conversation.”

“Not the sale?”

“No.”

Tamsin studied her.

Then nodded once.

“Good.”

Oskar stood.

“Walkaround.”

The berth smelled of clean sealant, old metal, and the faint warm bitterness of repaired power systems.

*Last Good Haul* waited with its cargo ramp lowered under Garage Ops supervision. The ramp no longer shuddered halfway down. Sven had not realized how used he had become to that shudder until it was gone.

Nora noticed too.

“It sounds less angry,” she whispered.

Ingrid said, “It has fewer reasons.”

Sven stepped to the yellow owner-seller mark.

Oskar pointed at him.

“Describe. Do not apologize to the hull.”

“I was not going to.”

Oskar looked at him.

“Much,” Sven amended.

Freya took the disclosure slate. Tamsin opened theirs. Keo moved to the edge of the permitted line and leaned forward like gravity had become optional.

Sven began with the cargo-bay door.

“Primary actuator rebuilt. Secondary sensor replaced. Manual lock inspected. Door seats properly now. It still makes a low groan in final closure because the frame is old and the acoustic channel carries it badly. That sound is logged here. A higher clicking sound is not normal. If you hear that, stop and inspect.”

Keo nodded rapidly.

Tamsin made a note.

Sven moved to the power trunk.

“The instability that gave us trouble leaving Kestrel Minor is corrected. Not masked. Corrected. The bad relay and two heat-stressed couplings are out. Power distribution tests clean under the safe-use envelope listed. If you overload auxiliary draw while running cold start plus cargo-lift assist, it will complain before it fails. Let it complain. Do not bypass the warning because a client is impatient.”

Tamsin glanced at Freya.

Freya said, “Impatient clients are not cheaper than emergency repairs.”

Tamsin’s expression changed.

Respect, maybe.

Or recognition.

They walked the hull.

Fuel margins.

Life-support checks.

Docking collar wear.

The port vent.

The galley heater that was reliable if cleaned and dramatic if not.

The flight chair adjustment that stuck at one point and should not be forced unless the buyer wanted to meet Oskar professionally.

Keo asked questions.

Sharp ones.

“Why not replace the port vent lip?”

“Because the sound is harmless and replacement would require opening a panel that is currently sealed and stable,” Sven said. “If you open it later for another reason, replace it then. Do not create work just because a ship sounds old.”

Oskar said, “Acceptable.”

Keo looked delighted.

At the engine access, Tamsin asked, “How far would you take it?”

Sven did not answer quickly.

Freya watched him choose not to love the ship at them.

“Short and medium clean-freight runs first,” he said. “Predictable routes. No speculative rush contracts until you know its habits. Keep the first six months boring. Let the maintenance schedule become history before you ask it for heroics.”

Tamsin looked at the old freighter.

“We do not need heroics.”

“Good,” Freya said.

Tamsin looked back.

“Why this price?”

There it was.

Freya had expected the question in the review room.

She was almost glad it had arrived in front of the ship.

She handed Tamsin the price page.

“The price reflects the repairs completed, remaining age limits, known maintenance needs, and the fact that we are not selling a story.”

Tamsin read the page again.

“It is below what you could get this week.”

“Yes.”

“Because of the race.”

“Yes.”

“Because someone would buy the association.”

“Yes.”

Tamsin’s eyes lifted.

“We do not want pity.”

Freya liked them better for saying it plainly.

“This is not pity.”

“It is not market extraction either.”

“No.”

“Then what is it?”

Freya looked at *Last Good Haul*.

Then at Sven.

Then Ingrid.

Then Nora, who was whispering something to moon-bear about cargo ramps.

Freya said, “It is the amount we can accept without lying to ourselves about what the ship is or what selling it does to someone else.”

Tamsin did not speak.

Freya continued.

“We know what it is to inherit a number that makes every choice smaller. We are not putting our relief inside your loan papers. The ship is old. It is repaired. It is useful. It deserves a buyer who knows all three things. You deserve a price that does not require pretending one of them is false.”

Keo looked down.

Tamsin kept their face steady, but their hand tightened around the slate.

“That is ethics,” they said.

Freya said, “It is also math.”

“That helps.”

“It usually does.”

Oskar made a small approving sound.

Freya chose to ignore how much that pleased her.

Tamsin looked at Keo.

Keo looked at the freighter.

Then at Tamsin.

“We read the packet,” Keo said softly.

“We did.”

“We can do the first six months boring.”

Tamsin’s mouth twisted.

“Can you?”

Keo thought about lying.

Did not.

“With supervision.”

Sven said, “Best answer.”

Tamsin took one breath.

“Pending final inspection confirmation and title transfer, we accept.”

Nora gasped.

Everyone looked at her.

She clapped one hand over her mouth.

Freya said, “It is all right.”

Nora lowered her hand.

“Does it know?”

Tamsin looked at her.

“The ship?”

Nora nodded.

Tamsin considered the question with more seriousness than most adults would have given it.

“Not yet,” they said. “We have to introduce ourselves properly.”

Nora nodded. “Good.”

The title transfer took place at a Garage Ops administrative desk because Freya had refused to sign ship papers in any room with decorative lighting.

Tamsin respected this.

The desk had scuffs, a dent in one corner, and a label that read:

SALE / TRANSFER / DISCLOSURE  
IF YOU DID NOT READ IT, DO NOT SIGN IT

Freya admired the label.

Oskar pretended not to notice.

The transfer packet included the full disclosure appendix, inspection status, safe-use envelope, repair summary, maintenance schedule, known limitation list, price rationale, no-hidden-warranty language, and a buyer acknowledgment that did not insult anyone’s intelligence.

Tamsin read every line.

Keo read most of them until Tamsin quietly redirected them from the maintenance appendix to the buyer acknowledgment, which Keo accepted with the long-suffering dignity of a teenager being parented near machinery.

Sven signed seller acknowledgment.

Freya signed financial receipt and disclosure confirmation.

Tamsin signed buyer acceptance.

Oskar signed Garage Ops inspection status witness, then added a note to the maintenance system that simply said:

TRANSFER CONDUCTED WITHOUT OBVIOUS FOOLISHNESS.

Freya looked at it.

“Is that official?”

“It is now in the log.”

“That did not answer my question.”

“It answered a better one.”

Keisha, who had come to observe the handoff, smiled into her tea.

When the final title light changed, *Last Good Haul* stopped being theirs.

Freya had expected the moment to hurt sharply.

Instead, it opened quietly.

Like a hand uncurling after carrying something too long.

Sven stared at the title display.

Nora asked, “Is it gone?”

“Not yet,” Sven said.

“But not ours?”

Freya knelt beside her.

“Not ours.”

Nora looked through the glass toward the old freighter.

“Can we still say goodbye?”

“Yes.”

Keo stepped closer, shy now that the ship had become real.

“We were thinking of a name.”

Nora turned immediately.

“A good one?”

Keo looked at Tamsin.

Tamsin lifted both hands. “This is Keo’s jurisdiction, apparently.”

Keo straightened.

“Second Breakfast.”

Sven blinked.

Freya stared.

Ingrid’s eyes brightened.

Nora gasped with reverence.

“Second Breakfast,” Nora repeated.

Keo nodded. “Because it already had one life. And because if a ship is going to work hard, it should sound fed.”

Ingrid put one hand to her chest.

“That,” she said, “is an excellent name.”

Sven started laughing.

Not loudly.

Not mockingly.

The kind of laugh that released something he had not known was still fastened.

Freya tried not to.

Failed.

Tamsin looked relieved.

Keo looked proud enough to lift off the deck.

Oskar looked at the ship.

“Name change request will require registry update, external marking plan, and transponder revision.”

Keo deflated slightly.

Oskar continued.

“Good name.”

Keo brightened again.

Nora ran to Ingrid and whispered, “He likes breakfast.”

Ingrid whispered back, “Most wise people do.”

The departure was scheduled for the next morning.

Not immediately.

Ships did not leave because feelings had reached a tidy shape. They left when Flight Control approved a window, Garage Ops completed release checks, paperwork updated, fuel and supplies were correct, and no one had tried to bring farewell pastries into an active diagnostic lane.

Sven was accused of the last one despite being innocent.

He accepted this as part of his future working environment.

The name change was temporary-display only for departure. Full exterior marking would come later. For now, the race-status public noise had moved on enough that only a few nearby workers and one extremely invested child watched as the berth display changed from:

LAST GOOD HAUL  
TRANSFERRED / RELEASE PENDING

to:

SECOND BREAKFAST  
TRANSFERRED / RELEASE PENDING

Nora applauded.

Tamsin looked at the sign for a long time.

Keo took a picture for private use after asking permission from everyone within the privacy boundary, including moon-bear.

The next morning, *Second Breakfast* powered up under Garage Ops supervision.

It did not sound young.

It sounded ready enough.

The port vent muttered.

Keo grinned.

Tamsin did not.

“Normal,” Keo said quickly.

“Logged normal,” Sven said.

“Logged normal,” Tamsin repeated, and some tension left their shoulders.

Flight Control cleared the departure window.

“Second Breakfast, this is Lichtbrunn Departure. Clean-side release authorized. Maintain assigned vector. No unscheduled cargo-cycle tests. Confirm flight plan to Brindlehook Junction with Kestrel Minor service stop.”

Tamsin’s voice came through the berth audio, steady and careful.

“Lichtbrunn Departure, Second Breakfast confirms assigned vector, no unscheduled cargo-cycle tests, flight plan Brindlehook Junction with Kestrel Minor service stop.”

Keo’s voice, farther from the pickup, whispered, “They said it.”

Tamsin whispered back, “I heard.”

Sven stood with Freya, Ingrid, and Nora at the approved viewing line.

No chase.

No creditor alert.

No hurried departure.

No hidden racing craft strapped in the bay.

No family pretending an old ship was more reliable than fear.

Just a repaired freighter, an honest packet, and new owners who knew what they were taking into the dark.

Nora held moon-bear up.

“Goodbye now,” she said.

Sven looked at her.

“Now?”

Nora nodded.

“It is leaving.”

“Yes,” he said.

She waved moon-bear’s paw.

“Goodbye, Last Good Haul. Goodbye, Second Breakfast. Be good and eat snacks.”

Freya put one hand over her mouth.

Ingrid said, “Sound instruction.”

Sven watched the ship move.

The docking clamps released.

The old freighter eased out under station handling, then transitioned cleanly into its assigned vector. Its repaired lights held steady. Its transponder read **Second Breakfast** without embarrassment.

The ship that had carried them away from Kestrel Minor did not vanish.

It got smaller.

That was different.

Freya slipped her hand into Sven’s.

He held it.

Ingrid stood on Nora’s other side, one hand resting lightly on the child’s shoulder.

Oskar, behind them, checked the departure trace.

“Clean release,” he said.

Freya looked back.

“Is that goodbye in Garage Ops?”

“No. It is clean release.”

Nora asked, “Did it pass inspection for its next life?”

Oskar looked at her.

Then at the departing freighter.

“Yes,” he said. “It passed inspection for its next life.”

Nora nodded, satisfied.

Sven watched until *Second Breakfast* became one more moving light in Der Himmelskrug’s lawful traffic, carrying someone else’s hope without carrying the Andersen family’s burden hidden inside it.

The old ship was gone.

The next life had begun.

And this time, no one had needed to run.

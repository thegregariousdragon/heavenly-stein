# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 6 Draft v2

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## Heinz Is Being Very Normal

Feli Brandt knew three things before she opened Oskar Teague's notification.

First, Oskar did not escalate garage matters because something was interesting. Oskar distrusted interesting. He treated interesting as the little cousin of catastrophic, invited to family gatherings only because someone had forgotten to change the lock code.

Second, a message marked **operational need-to-know** from the Starship Garage during Himmelskrug Run intake week was either about a ship, a person, or a person who had made a bad decision near a ship.

Third, Heinz Schmitt was standing six meters away with festival sponsor copy in one hand, a half-corrected routing speech in the other, and the exact expression of a man about to become a communications problem.

So Feli opened the notification quietly.

She read the subject line.

Clean-side intake: high-recognition racing craft / suspected control-response delay / privacy hold.

High-recognition racing craft was the kind of phrase systems used when they were trying not to say a name loudly.

Feli read the body.

Then she read it again.

Then, very carefully, she did not look at Heinz.

That was her mistake.

Heinz noticed.

Heinz always noticed when people did not look at him too deliberately. It was one of his more dangerous civic skills.

"What?" he asked.

"Nothing for public release."

"That is not the same as nothing."

"It is exactly the amount of nothing you are authorized to become excited about."

Heinz lowered both slates. "Feli."

"No."

"I have not asked."

"You are about to."

"I might have been about to ask whether this concerns a structural issue, route safety, festival access, public communications, or something that requires founder-level awareness."

Feli stared at him.

Heinz looked proud of himself.

"You practiced that," she said.

"I am growing."

"You are hunting."

"I contain multitudes."

"You contain a pending statement about sponsor seating that still uses the phrase 'a thrilling intimacy with race velocity.'"

Heinz winced. "That was not my final phrasing."

"It had better not be anyone's final phrasing."

Across the Crown Admin workroom, three communications assistants pretended not to listen with the intensity of professionals who planned to quote everything later in private.

Feli closed Oskar's notification and tapped the privacy lock again, not because the system needed it, but because her hands needed to do something with the instinct to swat Heinz away from the entire subject.

The workroom had been loud all morning. Run week turned Crown Admin into a controlled storm: vendor clarifications, route bulletins, media credential disputes, public safety copy, sponsor language, Board inquiries, staff notices, festival signage, and one ongoing argument about whether a foam finger shaped like The Last Pour violated good taste or merely documented its absence.

But Oskar's message had made the room quieter around Feli.

High public-recognition craft.

Operational privacy.

Family under creditor pressure.

Suspected control fault with possible racecourse relevance.

No race registration action requested or authorized.

Good.

That last line mattered.

Feli did look at Heinz then.

His face had changed.

The playful hunt was still there, but under it was the part of him that understood when the station had put a hand on something fragile.

"This is not a fan item," she said.

His posture straightened.

"No."

"A privately owned craft arrived under operational privacy. There is a declared safety concern. The family is under pressure. The garage is handling it correctly."

Heinz nodded once.

"And," Feli said, "if the name reaches public rumor before the family consents and before the garage knows what it is handling, we may turn someone's crisis into station entertainment."

Heinz's jaw tightened.

"No."

"Good."

"Do I need to know the name?"

"No," Feli said.

Heinz accepted that for nearly two seconds.

Then Oskar's notification chimed again because Feli had routing authority and the system had decided the founder was included at Communications discretion after privacy framing.

Feli closed her eyes.

"Oskar," she said to the ceiling, "you precise old menace."

Heinz did not move.

To his credit.

Feli forwarded the controlled summary to Heinz's slate with public sharing disabled, screenshot disabled, quote extraction disabled, and a note at the top that said:

READ LIKE AN ADULT.

Heinz looked at the note first.

"That feels personal."

"It is."

Then he read the message.

Feli watched him reach the ship name.

There were moments in Der Himmelskrug's civic life when Heinz Schmitt, founder, independent captain, strategic operations authority, aerospace engineer, and crisis responder, looked exactly like the station's impossible center of gravity.

This was not one of them.

This was a man being hit in the face by childhood and immediately trying to convert the impact into a risk matrix.

His eyes widened.

His mouth opened.

No sound came out.

Good start.

He looked at Feli.

Feli pointed one finger at him.

"No."

He closed his mouth.

He looked back at the slate.

His eyebrows climbed once at the ship name, then lowered at the fault note.

That was the difference.

Feli saw the fan arrive.

Then she saw the engineer push him aside.

"Control-response delay," Heinz said quietly.

"Yes."

"Owner-observed, not instrument-confirmed."

"Yes."

"Declared before powered movement."

"Yes."

Heinz exhaled.

"That is either nothing, a sensor-path ghost, or exactly the kind of thing that becomes fatal when a pilot needs clean feedback through combined attitude correction."

Feli held his gaze.

"There he is," she said.

Heinz looked down at the slate again.

"It is *Opportunity Knocks*."

Three assistants failed to type.

Feli turned her head.

All three began typing again.

Heinz caught himself. He looked at the nearest assistant, then at the privacy lock glowing on Feli's slate, then at the floor.

"Apologies," he said.

Feli watched the apology land properly.

"All right," she said.

Heinz took a breath through his nose.

Then another.

He held the slate in both hands as if it had become breakable.

"What does Oskar need?"

That was why Feli had forwarded it.

Not because Heinz loved ships.

Because beneath the disaster of that love, Heinz knew what dangerous craft deserved: the correct authority, good data, and no theatrical handling.

"Rolf first," Feli said. "Engineering risk. Amara awareness because of race implications. Keisha because the family is in crew/passenger clearance and may need support. Mara may need a quiet note if creditor pressure becomes station-side conduct. Janelle only if there is cause, not because the name is famous."

"And me?"

"You are informed because if you learn from a hallway whisper, you will become weather."

"I object to the accuracy."

"Object later."

Heinz looked again at the slate.

"No race registration action," he read.

"Correct."

"Good."

Feli blinked.

He looked up.

"What? I can be normal."

"You just whispered the ship name like it was a saint's relic."

"I did not."

"You did."

"I whispered it like a historically significant high-performance craft with an undefeated Andersen record in class operations."

Feli held up a hand.

He stopped.

"Normal," she said.

Heinz nodded, solemn as a treaty.

"Normal."

Rolf Petersen arrived seven minutes later carrying a structural tablet, two styluses, and the expression of a man who had been interrupted by a bracket.

Amara Reid arrived two minutes after him by secure call rather than in person, because Flight Control did not abandon active Run-week traffic to watch Heinz attempt self-control near a famous ship. Her face appeared on the conference wall, calm and sharp and already unconvinced by everyone.

Keisha Bell joined from Port Services with a crew-support board open beside her.

Mara Venn declined the first invitation, accepted the second after Feli changed the subject line from "privacy awareness" to "possible creditor-pressure boundary," and joined with her camera off, which somehow made the call feel more legally awake.

Feli opened the small conference nook, locked the door, and placed the notification in the middle of the shared display.

Rolf read it once.

"Port-forward historical asymmetry," he said.

Not *Opportunity Knocks*.

Not Andersen.

Not the Run.

Port-forward historical asymmetry.

Feli could have kissed him professionally.

Heinz looked at Rolf with betrayed admiration.

"That is your first response?"

Rolf did not look up. "It is the first station problem."

"The first station problem?"

"Yes. Oskar owns the craft on the floor. Amara owns traffic and race clearance. You can do aerodynamics in your head all you like; if the frame sends bad loads into a station handling system, that becomes my problem."

Heinz considered this, then nodded. "Fair."

Rolf enlarged the diagnostic excerpt.

"Initial cold resonance baseline shows port-forward historical asymmetry. Not factory symmetric. Possible frame memory. Oskar is right to keep it passive."

Heinz leaned closer without touching the display. "If the feedback path is smoothing old vibration, then under a gentle hand it could look like nothing."

"Or feel like nothing to anyone expecting an old ship to be old," Rolf said.

"But in a high-load correction-"

"The structure may tell the controls a slightly different story than the pilot thinks they are asking for."

Amara said from the wall, "That is the part I care about."

Heinz nodded toward the screen. "Especially through the Teeth. Coordinated pitch, yaw, and roll with narrow presentation. If feedback returns late, the pilot starts flying the echo instead of the craft."

The room went quiet.

Not because Heinz had surprised them by understanding.

Because he had said the dangerous part cleanly.

Amara's eyes sharpened.

"Yes," she said. "And Flight Control will not put an echo in that corridor."

"No one has requested race registration," Feli said.

"Good," Amara said.

Keisha enlarged the family routing note. "Four aboard. Family crew/passengers. Child present. Creditor-pressure context. No medical emergency. Do we know their current passenger clearance status?"

Feli checked. "Controlled clearance complete for Freya, Ingrid, and Nora. Sven remains owner representative at Garage Ops boundary. Crew Support has offered quiet waiting, food, wash access, and forms in that order."

Keisha nodded. "Good. Whoever set that order deserves a pastry."

"Can pastries be operational?" Heinz asked.

"No," Feli said.

"Yes," Keisha said at the same time.

The room paused.

Keisha looked directly into her camera.

"You have never seen an exhausted freighter family become human again after soup and a pastry?"

Feli conceded with a tilt of her head. "Operational pastries, under Crew Support authority."

Heinz made the tiniest note.

Feli saw it.

"Heinz."

"It says 'Keisha pastry doctrine.'"

"That had better not become public."

"It is doctrine."

"It is lunch."

"Many doctrines begin as lunch."

Mara's audio clicked on. "This is why I attend with camera off."

Rolf was still reading. "If this craft ever moves toward eligibility, Engineering needs load modeling before active handling simulation. Not because I outrank Oskar on craft diagnostics. Because the station hardware, tow interfaces, handling pads, and any test envelope we host are my responsibility."

"Oskar will agree," Heinz said.

"Yes. Which is why Oskar is tolerable."

Feli made a note.

Rolf looked at her.

"That is not a quote."

"It is now an internal morale fragment."

"No."

"Release notes," Heinz whispered.

Feli pointed at him without looking.

Heinz stopped.

Amara said, "Flight Control will not accept an eligibility packet without Garage Ops and Engineering signoff. And if we reach that point, I want the control-feedback issue translated into pilot-handling risk, not nostalgia."

Heinz nodded. "I can help with translation if asked."

Feli looked at him.

He caught it.

"If asked by the people whose jobs it is," he added. "And not near the owner unless useful."

"Good," Feli said.

Mara's voice came through cool and dry. "Creditor pressure plus famous ship plus large prize purse plus family debt is a litigation swamp wearing tap shoes. No public framing without consent, legal review, and investigation awareness if Mirador or Northstar step onto station conduct."

Heinz looked at the blank square that represented Mara.

"I appreciate the image."

"I do not provide imagery. I provide warnings people remember."

"Efficient."

Feli leaned forward. "Rules for this room. One: the ship's presence stays operational need-to-know. Two: the family is not content. Three: the garage owns the ship problem until it becomes an Engineering, Flight Control, Compliance, Security, or Communications problem through proper channels. Four: Heinz does not visit the garage unless Oskar authorizes it and Rolf or I am physically present."

Heinz put a hand to his chest.

"Physically present?"

"Yes."

"As supervision?"

"As role ballast."

"I can follow the problem."

"I know," Feli said. "That is why you need ballast. If you were technically useless, this would be easier."

Rolf grunted. "Correct."

Heinz looked from Feli to Rolf. "This is a very strange form of trust."

"It is the only kind you reliably respect," Feli said.

Amara said, "I vote no visit."

Everyone looked at the wall.

Amara shrugged slightly. "It was worth trying."

In the end, Oskar approved a limited observation visit with conditions.

His reply arrived nine minutes later.

FOUNDER / LEADERSHIP OBSERVATION PERMITTED FOR PRIVACY-RISK, STRUCTURAL-AWARENESS, AND FLIGHT-HANDLING CONTEXT ONLY. NO FLOOR ENTRY WITHOUT GARAGE OPS CLEARANCE. NO OWNER INTERRUPTION DURING ACTIVE DIAGNOSTIC STEP. NO TOUCHING. NO PUBLIC COMMENT. NO HISTORICALLY UNDIGNIFIED NOISES.

Feli read the last line aloud.

Heinz said nothing.

Rolf looked at him.

Heinz said nothing harder.

Feli forwarded the restriction to his slate.

Heinz read it and nodded.

"I accept Oskar's cruel but necessary framework."

"He wrote it for you," Rolf said.

"Yes, Rolf."

"Specifically."

"Yes, Rolf."

"By name, spiritually."

"Thank you, Rolf."

Feli stood. "We go, we observe, we do not disrupt."

Heinz stood too quickly.

Feli sat back down.

Heinz froze.

"Too excited?" he asked.

"Yes."

He sat.

Waited three seconds.

Stood again, slower, with the dignity of a man approaching both a legendary racing craft and a trap made by his own personality.

Feli allowed it.

The Starship Garage smelled like metal, sealant, clean coolant, warm composite, and people telling machinery the truth.

Heinz loved it.

Not in the way he loved the station's public levels, where joy had architecture and tourists could be gently bullied into understanding railings. He loved the garage the way he loved a ship's engine room after a hard burn: because here, charm had to survive contact with torque.

Feli walked on his left.

Rolf walked on his right.

This was not subtle.

Oskar had placed the leadership observation boundary behind a clear safety rail and two meters from the owner-representative area. It gave them a clean view of Berth C-Gamma-12 without putting them in anyone's way.

Heinz reached the rail.

Then stopped.

*Opportunity Knocks* rested on handling pads under working light.

Powered down.

Vanes folded.

Red-gold stripe visible along the hull.

Smaller than the broadcasts had made her. Sharper than memory had softened her. Old enough to have history in every seam and still shaped like an answer to a question most ships had never been brave enough to ask.

Heinz made no sound.

Feli relaxed by one millimeter.

Then Heinz whispered, "Oh, you beautiful unreasonable knife."

Feli closed her eyes.

Rolf, traitorously, whispered, "Accurate."

Oskar's voice came from below without him looking up. "Historically undignified noise logged."

Heinz leaned forward. "That was language."

"Same category."

"I said it quietly."

"The ship did not need to hear it."

"The ship is powered down."

"So is your permission to improvise."

Feli put one hand on the rail and breathed through her nose.

Heinz straightened.

"Apologies, Oskar."

"Accepted conditionally."

"Conditionally?"

"Remain useful."

Heinz nodded, solemn again. "Useful."

Rolf pointed at the diagnostic map. "Show me the asymmetry."

Oskar looked up then.

"Director Petersen," he said.

"Oskar."

The two men regarded each other with the mutual respect of people who had both said no to expensive stupidity for decades.

Oskar routed the diagnostic overlay to the observation display.

Rolf's entire body changed.

Not dramatically. Rolf did not do dramatic unless a bracket had lied. But the moment the structural map appeared, he became less a person standing in a garage and more a load path wearing boots.

"Port-forward," Rolf said.

"Yes."

"Old seam."

"Yes."

"Good work."

"Yes."

"Not symmetric."

"No."

"Frame memory?"

"Possible."

"Control-feedback interface?"

"Owner-observed delay. Tiny. Repeatable in his hand motion. Not confirmed in systems yet."

Heinz studied the timing overlay, not the ship.

Feli noticed.

Good.

"May I ask a handling question?" Heinz said.

Oskar looked at him for one full second.

Then said, "One."

"In the owner's replay, is the delay in sensed return only, or do we have any actuator-side evidence yet?"

"No actuator-side evidence yet," Oskar said. "That is why no one gets to name the fault."

"Good," Heinz said. "If the actuator path is clean and the feedback path is late, that changes pilot correction risk without necessarily showing as command failure."

Oskar nodded once.

Not indulgently.

Professionally.

"Yes."

Rolf glanced at Heinz. "And if the frame asymmetry is exciting the feedback path under load, my people become involved before yours start imagining course lines."

Heinz smiled faintly. "I am imagining course lines responsibly."

"You are imagining the Teeth."

"Yes."

"Stop doing it near the ship."

"Fair."

Below, Sven Andersen stood at the owner console with his hands behind the boundary line. He had not turned when Heinz arrived. Either he had not noticed, or he had noticed and chosen not to become more frightened than necessary.

Oskar followed Heinz's glance.

"Do not startle him," Oskar said.

Heinz looked offended for half a second, then thought better of it.

"I was not going to."

"You were considering being meaningful."

Feli said, "He was."

Heinz looked at her.

"You were," she said.

He accepted the conviction.

Sven replayed the manual trim motion again on the owner console. His hand moved in the air with the remembered correction.

Late.

Tiny.

Heinz saw it.

Rolf saw it and leaned closer.

Oskar saw both of them see it.

"That," Oskar said.

Rolf grunted.

Heinz, who had wanted to meet *Opportunity Knocks*, found himself instead watching Sven Andersen's hand.

That was better.

The ship had a legend.

The young man had a problem he was trying not to decorate.

After the replay, Oskar spoke quietly to Sven. Sven nodded, saved the overlay, and stepped back from the console.

Only then did Oskar look toward the observation boundary.

"Shipmaster Andersen," he said. "Leadership privacy and engineering observers are present. You may ignore them."

Sven turned.

He saw Rolf first, probably because Rolf looked like engineering authority even when buying soup.

Then Feli.

Then Heinz.

Sven went still in the particular way people did when the room suddenly contained someone they had only seen in public feeds.

Heinz lifted both hands slightly, palms visible.

Not a wave.

A non-threat.

"Shipmaster Andersen," he said. "Heinz Schmitt. I am here under Oskar's rules, Feli's supervision, Rolf's structural suspicion, and Flight Control's continuing willingness not to care how excited I am."

Sven blinked.

Feli stared at Heinz.

Oskar said, "Borderline."

Heinz nodded. "Understood."

Sven's mouth moved like he had misplaced several replies.

Finally he said, "Sir."

"Heinz is fine if you are comfortable. Sir is acceptable if you are not. No answer is also acceptable while people are pointing diagnostics at your ship."

Sven looked helplessly at Feli.

Feli said, "That was actually him trying not to pressure you."

"It was," Heinz said.

"It was too many options," Feli added.

"Yes," Sven said, then flushed. "Sorry. I mean-yes. There were many options."

Rolf's mouth twitched.

Heinz stepped no closer.

That mattered.

"I will keep this short," Heinz said. "Your ship is under garage protocol. Your privacy request is active. No public announcement is being made. No one here is treating her as a trophy."

Sven's eyes flicked toward *Opportunity Knocks*.

Then back.

"Thank you."

"Oskar would hit me with a diagnostic pad if I tried."

"No," Oskar said. "Pads are calibrated."

"With something uncalibrated, then."

"Possibly."

Heinz continued, more quietly. "I know what she is."

Sven's shoulders tightened.

Heinz saw it and corrected course.

"I do not know what she is to you," he said.

That was the better sentence.

Feli's expression shifted. Approval, probably. Or reduced fear of disaster.

Sven looked down at his slate.

"She is private," he said.

"Yes."

"She is not ready."

"Yes."

"She may not be raceable."

"Yes."

"I may not be raceable."

Heinz did not answer immediately.

Good.

The garage hummed around them: scan arms, low voices, cooling systems, pad locks, diagnostic pings. A machine room that did not care about family shame except where shame interfered with safety.

Heinz rested both hands lightly on the observation rail.

"That," he said, "is not something I can declare for you."

Sven looked up.

"Nor can Oskar declare it today. Nor can Rolf. Nor can Flight Control before their process begins. What the station can do is keep the questions in the right order."

Sven's throat moved.

"Ship first," he said.

"Safety first," Heinz said. "Ship, pilot, route, rules, consent, evidence."

"Ship before story," Oskar said.

Heinz pointed gently toward Oskar without looking away from Sven. "That one. Better and shorter."

Sven looked at the ship again.

"I don't want her turned into a story before I know whether I failed her."

Feli answered this time.

"Then she will not be, from this station."

Sven looked at her.

Feli's voice was not warm in the way public feeds made it warm. It was clearer than that. Warmer things could blur. Feli did not blur.

"If public attention becomes unavoidable later, we will discuss consent, boundaries, family privacy, investigation limits, and what is not for public consumption. Nora is not content. Medical details are not content. Debt pressure is not entertainment. Your private conversations are not station property."

Sven stared at her.

Heinz watched the words reach him one by one.

People often needed a moment when Feli described protection as a list instead of a feeling.

Lists had handles.

"Thank you," Sven said.

It came out rough.

Feli nodded once.

Rolf, apparently done allowing emotions to stand around unsupervised, pointed at Sven's slate.

"Your maintenance archive authorization. Maintenance only?"

"Yes."

"Good. Keep it that way. If someone asks for more without explaining why, say no and find Feli, Oskar, Mara, or Keisha."

Sven nodded quickly.

Heinz said, "Or me."

Feli looked at him.

Heinz sighed. "If the situation has escalated to founder-level judgment."

"Better," Feli said.

Sven's mouth almost smiled.

Almost.

That seemed to be his current maximum.

Heinz accepted it as a triumph.

"Shipmaster Andersen," Oskar said.

Sven turned back.

"Garage question. Are you asking us to open a preliminary race eligibility file?"

The garage seemed to pause.

Not actually.

Good crews kept working.

But attention shifted, tiny and sharp.

Sven looked at Heinz.

Heinz shook his head once.

Not no.

Not yes.

Not mine.

Sven looked at Feli.

Feli gave him nothing but the dignity of not choosing for him.

He looked at Rolf.

Rolf said, "It is a file. Not clearance."

Oskar said, "Not registration."

Amara's voice entered from the observation comm, making everyone except Oskar look up.

"Flight Control confirms. Preliminary eligibility file may be opened as inactive and conditional. It does not grant race status, practice status, start priority, public entrant status, or permission to move the craft under power."

Heinz looked toward the ceiling.

"Amara, were you listening the whole time?"

"Yes."

"Comforting."

"No."

Feli whispered, "Normal."

Heinz whispered back, "I am experiencing civic restraint."

Amara continued, "Shipmaster Andersen, preliminary eligibility review would record your interest in pursuing the Himmelskrug Run if, and only if, Garage Ops, Engineering, Flight Control, Race Ops, and all required safety reviews clear the craft and pilot. You may withdraw at any time before active registration. The station may deny the file at any gate. Do you understand?"

Sven did not answer quickly.

Good, Heinz thought.

Fast answers were often fear wearing a hat.

Sven looked at *Opportunity Knocks*.

The ship waited on handling pads.

Not worshipped.

Not dismissed.

Sven looked at the diagnostic overlay.

Then at Oskar.

"Would opening the file pressure the garage?"

"No," Oskar said.

"Would it pressure Flight Control?"

Amara answered, "No."

"Would it make the ship public?"

Feli answered, "No."

"Would it make my family think I promised?"

Heinz felt that one.

Keisha Bell's voice joined from a side channel, gentle and practical. "Only if you tell them badly. Crew Support can help you tell them accurately."

Sven closed his eyes.

Opened them.

"What would the file say?"

Amara answered. "Owner expresses conditional interest in eligibility review. Craft remains inactive pending diagnostics. No registration. No powered movement. No race commitment. No public status. Outstanding blockers: ship diagnostic, Engineering review, telemetry compatibility, pilot eligibility, Race Ops review, Flight Control clearance."

Sven breathed once.

"That sounds like gates."

"Yes," Amara said.

He nodded slowly.

"I can work with gates."

No one cheered.

That was good.

Cheering would have ruined it.

Oskar turned to the console and opened a form with the least celebratory interface Heinz had ever seen. It looked like taxes had learned to dock ships.

PRELIMINARY ELIGIBILITY REVIEW - INACTIVE / CONDITIONAL

Sven read every line.

No one hurried him.

Once, he looked as if he might apologize for taking too long. Oskar saw it and said, "Forms dislike rushing. It makes them breed errors."

Sven kept reading.

He signed only after Oskar highlighted the withdrawal clause, Amara confirmed the inactive status, and Feli confirmed the privacy lock.

The file accepted his acknowledgment.

No trumpet sounded.

No public feed updated.

No race board changed.

No one outside the operational chain learned that *Opportunity Knocks* had taken one paper step toward the Himmelskrug Run.

That was exactly right.

Sven stepped back from the console.

He looked as if he had expected the act to feel like a leap and found instead that it felt like placing one foot on a marked line.

Heinz understood.

Marked lines saved lives.

Oskar closed the form.

"Back to diagnostics," he said.

The garage obeyed.

Rolf turned toward the lower display. "I want a structural review window before any simulation beyond passive modeling."

"You will get one," Oskar said.

"Before," Rolf repeated.

"I heard the word the first time."

"I am repeating it for Heinz."

Heinz raised both hands. "I know what before means."

"You also know what tempting looks like," Rolf said.

"Unfortunately, yes."

Feli did not believe the dignity Heinz attempted afterward, but she allowed it to stand.

Sven watched them for a moment, confused by the shape of the room: authority arguing, but safely; people caring, but with boundaries; a famous ship present, but not devouring him.

Heinz stepped back from the rail.

"Shipmaster Andersen," he said.

Sven turned.

"I will now say one historically undignified thing, and then we will return to paperwork."

Feli made a sound that was almost a warning.

Oskar said, "One."

Rolf said, "Quietly."

Amara said, "I am logging this."

Heinz nodded to all of them.

Then he looked at Sven, not the ship.

"When I was thirteen," Heinz said, "I watched a recording of your grandfather taking *Opportunity Knocks* through the old Helix Narrows with one thruster fluttering and a commentator audibly reconsidering his career. I decided then that any ship that could make a professional broadcaster whisper 'oh no' in three languages deserved respect."

Sven's face changed.

Not pride.

Not exactly.

Something older and sadder.

Heinz continued, softer.

"That is the undignified part. Here is the paperwork part: respect means she gets inspected properly. So do you."

Sven looked down.

Then nodded.

"Thank you."

Heinz inclined his head and stepped back.

No more.

He had spent the whole room's patience and knew it.

Feli touched his elbow once as they turned to leave.

Not correction.

Not quite praise.

A signal that he had not ruined the day.

Heinz treasured it privately and did not say so, which proved growth.

Behind them, Oskar was already making Sven replay the manual trim motion again.

Rolf had opened a structural note.

Amara had returned her attention fully to Flight Control.

Keisha was arranging family support language.

Feli was drafting a privacy memo in her head.

And *Opportunity Knocks* waited on the handling pads while Der Himmelskrug did what it did best: turned an impossible feeling into accountable process.

For the first time, the Himmelskrug Run was not a miracle in the distance.

It was a file with gates.

Sven could work with gates.

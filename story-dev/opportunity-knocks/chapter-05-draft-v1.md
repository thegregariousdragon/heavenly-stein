# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 5 Draft v1

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## The Legend on Handling Pads

The Starship Garage did not gasp.

It logged.

That was one of the reasons Oskar Teague had not quit, despite twenty-three years of ships arriving with leaking seals, dramatic owners, contradictory manifests, suspiciously optimistic repair histories, and at least four pilots who had described catastrophic drive noise as "a personality thing."

Ships did not need gasps.

Ships needed clearance, scan lanes, cradle locks, torque confirmation, power isolation, clean/dirty declarations, and people who understood that admiration was not a maintenance procedure.

On Berth C-Gamma-12, *Last Good Haul* sat clamped to Der Himmelskrug's lower-handle clean-side intake collar and looked embarrassed to be indoors.

That was unfair.

Old ships did not look embarrassed.

People assigned embarrassment to machines when the machines had survived long enough to make younger infrastructure appear rude.

Oskar had seen worse freighters.

He had also seen better freighters used as cautionary diagrams.

*Last Good Haul* belonged to the category he privately called honest disasters: patched, tired, limited, but not lying. The difference mattered. Dishonest disasters killed people while insisting they had merely surprised them.

The freighter's intake packet sat open on Oskar's slate.

Cargo-bay seal wear.

Docking-bay power instability on twelve-second sag cycle.

Coolant drift under trim correction.

Outdated aft utility maintenance records.

Rough handling cradle.

Stored racing craft.

No active weapons.

No ordnance.

No hazardous cargo attached.

Clean-side Starship Garage intake requested.

Operational privacy requested.

Stored craft handling restriction: no powered release, no self-movement, no standard tow under own systems, suspected low-power control-response delay through pilot-feedback interface or related control path.

Oskar read the last line twice.

Then a third time, because the third time was when owners sometimes became less ridiculous in memory.

Felt late response on manual trim during maintenance check. Not confirmed by instrumentation. Declared before movement.

He looked through the berth glass toward the freighter's cargo bay.

The bay doors remained closed.

Good.

Nothing opened because a pilot was nervous. Nothing opened because a family had suffered. Nothing opened because a craft might be famous. Nothing opened because a creditor somewhere had learned to spell urgency with expensive punctuation.

Garage Ops opened things when the process was ready.

"Passive tow cradle?"

"Positioned," said Meera from Pad Control.

"Scan lane?"

"Clean-side lane three active, no restricted flags."

"Power isolation?"

"Station side isolated. Freighter power remains internal. Cargo-bay cycle locked pending command."

"Family movement?"

"Shipmaster remains at owner-representative mark. Other family members routed to controlled crew/passenger clearance. Crew Support has a quiet waiting option flagged if needed."

Oskar glanced through the side window.

Sven Andersen stood exactly where he had been told to stand.

That was already unusual.

Most anxious owners drifted. They took two steps forward, one step sideways, and then tried to become invisible behind a tool rack while asking whether invisible people could ask questions. Sven stood on the yellow owner-representative mark with both hands folded around a data slate, looking at the sealed cargo bay as if he were afraid of what would happen if he wanted too much near it.

He was young.

Not child-young. Worse. Old-enough-to-have-been-crushed young.

Beside him, a station garage liaison explained the next step. Sven listened. Actually listened. His eyes moved to every point the liaison named: no-power handling, cradle lock, scan lane, inspection authority, owner observation boundary, no public release of craft identity, no race registration action from garage intake.

He nodded at the end.

Did not argue.

Did not say Do you know what ship that is?

Interesting.

Oskar distrusted interesting on principle.

"Cargo-bay cycle," he said.

The garage lights shifted from standby amber to working white.

A warning tone sounded inside the berth, lower and more serious than public station chimes. It meant moving mass, controlled access, nobody gets poetic near a pressure boundary.

The freighter's cargo-bay locks released under Garage Ops authority.

Slowly.

Everything important opened slowly when Oskar had enough authority to make it so.

The outer bay seam widened.

Dust stirred in the intake light.

The first thing Oskar saw was not the racing craft.

It was the straps.

Old but properly doubled. Cross-load secured. Wear points padded. Secondary catch lines placed by someone who understood that "probably stable" was how cargo learned to become legal testimony.

Then the cradle.

Ugly. Improvised. Stronger than it looked. Too rough for long-term use, but good enough for frightened transit if nobody tried to impress physics.

Then the craft inside it.

Oskar Teague had been a professional for too long to make a noise.

Someone behind him failed.

Not loudly.

A half breath. A tiny involuntary scrape of air from a technician who had just watched childhood step out of a cargo bay on handling pads.

Oskar did not turn.

"Name," he said.

The intake clerk hesitated.

Oskar looked at her.

The clerk checked the privacy flag, then routed the sealed manifest to authorized garage personnel only.

Her eyes widened.

She swallowed.

"Stored craft identified as *Opportunity Knocks*."

The garage did not gasp.

It did several smaller, more embarrassing things.

A tool cart stopped moving because the tech pushing it forgot carts did not stop themselves politely forever.

Someone in scan lane three whispered, "No."

Someone else whispered, "Andersen stripe."

Meera at Pad Control sat very straight and said nothing, which told Oskar she knew exactly what she was looking at.

A junior mechanic near the port-side guide rail lifted one hand halfway toward the glass and then, to his credit, put it behind his back like a person choosing employment.

Oskar waited one second.

Two.

He allowed two because people were not machines and because *Opportunity Knocks* was, inconveniently, *Opportunity Knocks*.

Then he said, "If anyone drools on my diagnostic pad, they clean the coolant trench."

The garage returned to life.

Too quickly in some places, not quickly enough in others, but enough.

The tool cart moved again.

Scan lane three remembered its job.

The junior mechanic developed intense professional interest in a wheel chock.

Oskar walked out onto the berth floor.

Not fast.

Never fast toward a newly opened cargo bay with an anxious owner, an old cradle, a famous craft, and a declared possible control fault.

*Opportunity Knocks* emerged under passive tow.

No power.

No lights.

No cockpit wake.

No little dramatic flutter of systems that would have made half the garage believe in fate and the other half file a complaint.

Just station tow pads taking the cradle load, slow and clean, inch by inch.

The ship was smaller than people remembered.

That happened with legends. Memory added mass. Broadcast angles added threat. Stories added wings.

The actual craft sat low and sharp in the cradle, vanes folded, hull lines compact, old red-gold Andersen stripe worn but not ruined. She looked built for precision rather than intimidation. She looked like a machine that would not forgive a pilot for thinking love was a substitute for timing.

Good, Oskar thought.

He disliked forgiving ships.

The passive cradle settled onto handling pads.

Locks engaged.

Scan arms moved into first position.

The garage status board updated.

STORED CRAFT: POWERED DOWN  
HANDLING: PASSIVE  
PUBLIC STATUS: PRIVATE / OPERATIONAL PRIVACY  
GARAGE STATUS: INITIAL CLEAN-SIDE INTAKE  
OWNER ACCESS: OBSERVATION ONLY  
FAULT DECLARATION: SUSPECTED CONTROL-RESPONSE DELAY

Oskar crossed to Sven.

The liaison stepped back.

Sven looked from Oskar's badge to Oskar's face, then very deliberately did not look past him at the ship.

Good again.

"Shipmaster Andersen," Oskar said. "Oskar Teague. Senior intake and diagnostic lead for this berth."

"Sven Andersen."

"I know. You put it on the form."

Sven blinked.

Then nodded once. "Yes."

Oskar studied him.

Not cruelly. Cruelty was what lazy people used when they wanted fear to do the work skill should have done.

Sven looked exhausted, underfed, and too awake around the eyes. His hands held the slate as if it were both shield and confession. He had the posture of a man waiting for someone to punish him for telling the truth.

Oskar knew that posture.

He hated how often he knew that posture.

"You declared a suspected control-response delay," Oskar said.

"Yes."

"Not confirmed by instrumentation."

"No."

"You felt it."

"Yes."

"Where?"

Sven looked briefly toward the ship.

Then back.

"Low-power maintenance check. Manual trim. It was not the main response. Not obvious. More like the control-feedback path was arriving late enough that my hand corrected before the system agreed it needed correction."

Oskar did not move.

Sven rushed on.

"It could be me. I know that. The ship was powered low. The cradle load was uneven. Freighter power is unstable. I am tired. I may be hearing old grief in a control path and calling it mechanics."

"Do not diagnose your feelings for me," Oskar said. "Describe the ship."

Sven stopped.

Behind Oskar, someone made the smallest approving sound and then pretended to cough.

Sven breathed.

"Manual trim input felt clean at contact. Actuator response looked normal on the local indicator. But the pilot-feedback return felt delayed. Not enough for a standard dock maneuver to flag. Enough that, under high-load attitude changes, I would not trust her without diagnostics."

Oskar waited.

Sven added, quieter, "Enough that if I ignored it and someone got hurt, I would know when I chose to be stupid."

That was the sentence.

Oskar had listened through enough owner panic to know when a person had finally stopped decorating the problem.

He looked back at *Opportunity Knocks*.

Scan arms moved over the hull.

No-power inspection path held.

Garage staff worked with the exaggerated care of people trying very hard not to look like they were handling a legend. Which, because station humor had teeth, made them look exactly like people handling a legend.

"Show me," Oskar said.

Sven looked at him.

"What?"

Oskar pointed to the owner observation console beside the berth. "Your note says manual trim. Show me the motion path you tested. No power. No control activation. Walk me through your hand, your indicator, your timing."

Sven's grip shifted on the slate.

"You believe me?"

"No."

The word landed clean.

Sven flinched.

Oskar continued, "I also do not disbelieve you. Belief is for songs and people with spare cargo capacity. You declared a possible fault before movement. That earns diagnostics. Evidence earns the next thing."

Sven stared at him.

Then something in his face changed.

Not relief.

Better.

A man recognizing a door with a proper label.

"Yes," Sven said. "I can show you."

"Good."

Oskar turned toward the console. "Meera, set owner console to passive replay mark. No write access. No command link. Visual overlay only."

"Set."

"Jahn, scan cradle load history from freighter logs. Compare tiedown stress during departure and approach. I want any frame flex that could mimic feedback delay."

"On it."

"Pavel, hull resonance baseline. Cold. No drama."

"No drama," Pavel said, with the face of a man suppressing five dramas.

"Kiri, intake camera privacy stays locked. If anyone routes this to public feeds, I will make them inventory washer sizes until language loses meaning."

"Already locked."

Oskar looked toward the junior mechanic at the guide rail.

The young man straightened.

"And you."

"Yes, Chief."

"Hands."

The mechanic displayed both hands immediately, palms out, nowhere near the hull.

Oskar pointed at the floor line. "That stripe is not a religious object. It is paint over material. You will not touch it without assignment."

"Yes, Chief."

"Say the whole thing."

"The stripe is not a religious object."

"And?"

"It is paint over material."

"And?"

"I will not touch it without assignment."

"Excellent. There may be hope."

A few people smiled.

Not much.

Enough to let the pressure vent without becoming foolish.

Sven looked at the exchange with an expression Oskar could not quite place.

Embarrassment, maybe.

Pain, maybe.

Maybe the particular confusion of seeing strangers love something your family had nearly lost.

Oskar softened his voice by half a degree. "They know the ship. They do not know your problem yet. My floor runs on problems."

Sven nodded.

"I understand."

"No," Oskar said. "You are beginning to."

They moved to the owner observation console.

The console sat behind a marked boundary: close enough for shipmasters to see what mattered, far enough to prevent hands from becoming incidents. Sven stayed behind the line without being reminded.

Oskar appreciated not reminding adults about lines.

Sven opened his maintenance note and projected a simple hand-motion record onto the passive overlay. No system link. No command path. Just his own recorded sequence and timing marks.

"This was before departure," he said. "Low-power cockpit check. I had no reason to move the ship. I was checking whether the control path would wake cleanly after storage."

Oskar watched the overlay.

Sven's recorded hand moved.

Slow.

Careful.

Not a show-off motion. Not nostalgia. A mechanic's touch, which was rarer in pilots than pilots liked to believe.

"Here," Sven said.

The mark paused.

"Contact feels clean. Indicator accepts the input. But the return through the hand is late. Just a hair. I corrected automatically, then realized I had corrected before the feedback stabilized."

Oskar zoomed the timing.

"Again."

Sven replayed it.

Oskar watched Sven this time, not the overlay.

Sven's hand twitched in the air at the same point.

Memory in muscle.

Not proof.

But interesting.

Oskar hated interesting less when it arrived with a timestamp.

"Did your father record similar behavior?"

Sven's face closed.

Then reopened by force.

"I don't know. Records are incomplete. He died before he could finish transferring the maintenance archive."

"Grandfather?"

"My grandfather mostly recorded victories, insults, and fuel arguments."

Meera made a sound from Pad Control.

Oskar did not turn.

"Nils Andersen was famous for all three," he said.

Sven looked startled.

"You know the records?"

"I know enough not to trust them as maintenance documentation."

For the first time since Oskar had approached him, Sven almost smiled.

Almost.

Oskar looked back at the overlay.

"You said high-load attitude changes."

"Yes."

"Why?"

"The lag is tiny. It might not matter in ordinary dock movement. But if the frame is flexing under load or the feedback interface is smoothing old vibration, then at speed, with coordinated pitch, yaw, and roll, especially through something like the Twins' Teeth-"

He stopped.

Oskar's eyes narrowed.

Sven looked down. "Sorry. I know I am nowhere near race clearance. I am not asking for that."

"What are you asking for?"

Sven answered too quickly to be lying.

"A diagnosis before anyone moves her under power."

Oskar nodded.

Good sentence.

Useful sentence.

He turned toward the berth.

"Meera, add high-load attitude simulation to later diagnostic plan. Passive model only until Engineering signs off. Flag possible frame-feedback resonance. Low confidence, owner-observed, worth chasing."

Meera's hands moved over controls.

"Flagged."

Across the berth, Pavel looked up from the hull baseline. "Chief."

Oskar heard the tone.

Everyone on a garage floor learned to hear tone.

"What?"

"Cold resonance baseline is mostly clean, but port-forward frame memory has a wrinkle."

"Define wrinkle."

Pavel glanced at *Opportunity Knocks*, then at Sven, then back at the data.

"Old repair seam. Beautiful work. Not wrong. But not factory symmetric anymore."

Sven's face went pale.

Oskar held up one hand before guilt could sprint across the room and break something.

"Old ships are records of surviving. Asymmetry is not fault by itself."

"I know," Sven said.

"You looked like you forgot."

"I did."

"Remember faster."

"Yes."

Oskar walked to Pavel's station.

The baseline displayed in thin blue lines. Most of *Opportunity Knocks* mapped clean for her age. Better than clean, in places. Somebody had loved this ship with tools, not just stories.

Port-forward showed what Pavel had called a wrinkle: a tiny historical difference in stiffness, buried where ordinary scans might call it acceptable repair drift.

Not proof.

But interesting.

Behind him, the garage had gone quiet in the working way.

No whispers now.

No race stats.

No awe.

The legend had become a machine with a possible problem.

That was better for everyone.

Oskar enlarged the seam data.

"Pull repair-history search. Local records, Andersen archive if owner permits, public race incident records, manufacturer advisories if any survived the century without being eaten by corporate optimism."

Sven stepped closer to the boundary but not over it.

"You can access my family archive," he said.

Oskar looked at him.

"Do you understand what you just offered?"

Sven swallowed. "Maintenance files only. Ship systems, repair logs, race telemetry if it helps. Not private family messages."

"Good. Boundaries make better evidence."

Freya Andersen, Oskar decided, had either trained him well or married him because he could eventually be trained.

Possibly both.

"Send maintenance-only authorization to this console," Oskar said. "Read access. Revocable. Logged."

Sven did.

His hands shook once.

Only once.

The console accepted the permission.

Oskar watched the data begin to populate: partial logs, old tags, Lars Andersen's maintenance notes, Nils Andersen's more chaotic entries, Sven's recent preservation work. Messy. Human. Incomplete.

Useful.

Kiri at privacy control said, "Chief."

"If this is another person asking whether they can tell their cousin-"

"No. Leadership notification threshold."

Oskar looked at her.

Kiri pointed to the screen.

Operational privacy flag. High public-recognition craft. Race event active. Suspected control fault with possible racecourse relevance. Family under creditor pressure per sealed arrival note. Garage staff containment active. Public exposure risk moderate and rising.

The station's systems were polite.

They were not subtle.

Oskar exhaled through his nose.

"Right."

Sven looked at him. "What happens now?"

"Now your ship remains here. Powered down. Locked to garage authority. We diagnose."

"And my family?"

"Crew Support has them in controlled passenger clearance. If they are not fools, they will offer food, quiet seating, and forms in the least cruel order."

Sven nodded.

"You?"

"I notify people who need to know before someone who does not need to know learns loudly."

Sven's expression tightened. "Because of the ship's name."

"Because of the ship's name, the fault, the race, the privacy request, and the fact that your freighter arrived with creditor language attached to its shadow."

Sven went still.

Oskar did not ask whether that was true.

It was in the notes, carefully worded and ugly around the edges.

"Is this going to become public?" Sven asked.

"Not from my floor."

Sven looked at the garage staff.

They were all suddenly very interested in work.

Good.

Oskar raised his voice.

"Reminder. Operational privacy is not decorative. Anyone turning this ship into a story before authorization gets reassigned to filter inventory, and I will choose the filters emotionally."

The garage accepted this as law.

Oskar lowered his voice again.

"Your ship is safe here as long as everyone keeps behaving like adults with badges."

Sven's face did something painful.

"I don't know what to do with that sentence."

"Try believing it slowly."

Sven nodded once.

Oskar opened an internal notification.

TO: Rolf Petersen, Engineering and Structural Systems  
CC: Flight Control Watch Supervisor; Garage Ops Duty Lead; Keisha Bell, Port Services and Crew Support; Feli Brandt, Communications and Civic Interface, privacy risk only  
VISIBILITY: Operational need-to-know  
SUBJECT: Clean-side intake: high-recognition racing craft / suspected control-response delay / privacy hold

He paused before adding Heinz.

Oskar stared at the recipient line.

Then removed Heinz.

Then added a note for Feli:

Founder notification at Communications discretion after privacy framing. Do not allow informal garage-floor rumor path.

That was not because Heinz could not be trusted.

It was because Heinz could be trusted to care very loudly if surprised.

Feli existed to make caring survive contact with public consequences.

Oskar typed the body.

Clean-side intake received a privately owned stored racing craft under operational privacy flag. Craft identified in sealed manifest as *Opportunity Knocks*. Owner/shipmaster Sven Andersen declared suspected low-power control-response delay prior to any powered movement. Garage Ops has accepted no-power handling only. Initial cold resonance baseline shows port-forward historical asymmetry requiring deeper review. No race registration action requested or authorized through intake. Family routed through controlled passenger/crew clearance. Creditor-pressure context noted in arrival packet; no station-side incident at this time. Request Engineering awareness and privacy coordination.

He reviewed every sentence.

Removed one adjective.

Sent it.

Above them, somewhere in the station's administrative and operational nervous system, the name *Opportunity Knocks* began to move.

Not through public feeds.

Not through fan channels.

Not through a garage tech's cousin.

Through the proper route.

Oskar looked back at the ship.

The red-gold stripe caught the working light.

A junior mechanic stared at it for exactly one second too long, felt Oskar's attention without seeing him, and returned to the scan readout.

Progress.

Sven stood behind the owner line, watching the diagnostic map build itself around his family's legend.

He looked frightened.

He also looked less alone.

Oskar pointed at the console.

"Again," he said.

Sven blinked. "The manual trim replay?"

"Yes."

"We already-"

"Again. This time, stop apologizing before the data has a chance to be useful."

Sven looked at the overlay.

Then at *Opportunity Knocks*.

Then back at the console.

He replayed the motion.

His hand moved in the air at the same point.

Late.

Tiny.

Maybe nothing.

Maybe the difference between a legend and a funeral in the wrong gate.

Oskar watched.

The garage watched.

The ship waited on handling pads, not worshipped, not dismissed, finally being treated like the dangerous, beloved, repairable machine she was.

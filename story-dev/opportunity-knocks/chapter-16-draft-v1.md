# Opportunity Knocks — Chapter 16 Draft v1

Status: draft-only prose.

This chapter draft does not create active canon or active chronology. It is working prose for the **Opportunity Knocks** story-development packet.

## Legal Freedom

Three weeks after Sven Andersen won the Himmelskrug Run, Freya Andersen sat in a private administrative office on Der Himmelskrug and refused to let anyone call it solved.

This made three people visibly uncomfortable and one person visibly proud.

The uncomfortable people were a station financial officer named Pell Marwick, an IFP clearing representative whose face had been trained by committees, and Sven.

The proud person was Mara Venn, Director of Compliance and Customs Integrity, who had discovered in Freya Andersen a civilian with the rare and useful gift of reading every word that wanted to become a knife.

The room had no windows.

Freya approved of this.

Windows encouraged metaphor. Metaphor encouraged people to say things like “new horizon” when what they meant was “conditional release subject to settlement verification.” Freya had survived too much paperwork to permit scenic language near her family’s future.

On the table in front of her were seven separate document stacks.

Race prize clearance.

Official race result certification.

Prize-fee deductions.

Temporary support ledger.

Northstar payoff statement.

Lien-release packet.

Title boundary memorandum for *Opportunity Knocks*.

Sven sat to her left with both hands folded and the expression of a man who had learned that winning a race was, in some ways, easier than sitting quietly while his wife did arithmetic at institutions.

Freya did not feel sorry for him.

Much.

Pell Marwick cleared his throat. “As you can see, the first-place purse has completed standard processing. The twenty-five million credit prize was certified after post-race review, held through the ordinary contest-clearing interval, and released into protected settlement routing.”

Freya looked at the first stack.

“Released to whom?”

“Settlement account under race-prize processing authority.”

“Not to Northstar.”

“No.”

“Not to Andersen Freight.”

“No.”

“Not to Sven personally.”

“No.”

“Not to me personally.”

“No.”

“Good.”

Pell blinked.

Freya made a note.

Sven leaned closer. “Was that a good good, or a continue-before-I-remove-your-bones good?”

Freya did not look at him. “This is an office.”

“I whispered.”

Mara said, “Not effectively.”

Sven sat back.

The IFP clearing representative, whose nameplate read Talan Or, folded their hands.

“The prize settlement has sufficient cleared balance to satisfy the Northstar statement, station support reimbursement where applicable, outstanding clean-garage and race processing fees, and retained family reserve per your chosen routing.”

Freya looked up.

“Per my chosen routing?”

Talan Or nodded. “Your filed preference: debt satisfaction first, contested lien extinguishment or withdrawal documented before discretionary release, family reserve protected from creditor sweep, and no admission language.”

Sven looked at her.

Freya felt the look and refused to take it yet.

Admiration was dangerous during document review. It made people skip lines.

“Show me the no-admission language,” she said.

Pell slid the relevant page forward.

Freya read.

Then read again.

Then read aloud, because lies hated air.

“Payment issued from race-prize proceeds shall satisfy Andersen Freight obligation number NF-KM-8821 in full. Payment does not constitute admission that Northstar Collateral Services possessed valid claim, lien, preservation authority, or collateral access right against the privately titled racing craft *Opportunity Knocks*.”

She stopped.

Read the next sentence.

Her eyes narrowed.

Sven saw it and became very still.

Mara saw it and smiled faintly.

Pell Marwick saw it and looked like a man hearing a hull tick in the wall.

Freya tapped the page.

“No.”

Pell looked down. “Which line?”

“This one.”

She read it aloud.

“Northstar reserves all lawful claims not expressly resolved herein.”

Pell said carefully, “That is standard reservation language.”

“I am not opposed to standard language. I am opposed to useful fog.”

Talan Or’s committee face shifted by half a millimeter.

Mara leaned back in her chair.

Freya continued. “This packet resolves the debt, the claimed preservation interest, and any Northstar claim against *Opportunity Knocks* arising from the Andersen Freight obligation, historical business support, storage aboard *Last Good Haul*, or disputed bundled-asset theory. If they reserve unrelated lawful claims, say unrelated. If they reserve claims against the ship, the debt is not resolved. If they reserve claims against us from this transaction, they are leaving a hook.”

Pell inhaled.

Freya held up one hand.

“Do not tell me it is standard again unless standard means harmless.”

Sven coughed once into his fist.

Not laughing.

Trying not to.

Mara said, “Mrs. Andersen is correct.”

Pell looked wounded.

Freya did not care.

Talan Or pulled the page closer. “The reservation can be narrowed. Northstar may resist the wording.”

“Northstar has resisted food, sleep, clean title, and human decency,” Freya said. “It can resist in writing.”

Sven looked at the ceiling.

Mara’s smile became dangerous.

Pell made a note.

Talan Or said, “Proposed replacement: Northstar reserves only unrelated lawful claims, if any, not arising from Andersen Freight obligation NF-KM-8821, disputed preservation notices, alleged collateral reach into *Opportunity Knocks*, or events under active station/IFP review.”

Freya considered it.

“Add no claim against the racing craft’s title, possession, repair history, prize eligibility, future race earnings, or storage berth.”

Talan Or typed.

Pell whispered, “That is a lot.”

Freya looked at him.

He stopped being audible.

Sven leaned closer again, carefully. “Freya.”

“What?”

“You are terrifying.”

“Read paragraph four.”

He read paragraph four.

Then he stopped smiling.

“That says family asset.”

“Yes.”

“It should say privately titled family property.”

“Yes.”

“And not business asset.”

“Correct.”

He looked at Pell.

Freya nearly patted his hand.

He was learning.

Pell said, “We can correct that.”

“You will correct that before I authorize settlement release,” Freya said.

“Of course.”

“And before the payoff transmission leaves protected routing.”

“Yes.”

“And before anyone sends Northstar a receipt it can misread professionally.”

Pell looked at Mara.

Mara gave him nothing.

He looked back at Freya. “Yes.”

The office went quiet except for the soft clicking of Talan Or’s slate.

Outside the room, Der Himmelskrug continued being a station. Ships docked. Visitors got lost. Someone somewhere tried to bring festival food into a diagnostic area and encountered doctrine. The public feed still replayed the final kink of The Last Pour twice an hour on race-week retrospectives, each time with Ren Calder’s voice cracking in a slightly different way depending on the edit.

Inside the office, freedom became clauses.

Freya preferred that.

Clauses could be checked.

The revised Northstar packet arrived twenty-two minutes later through the protected creditor channel.

Freya read every word.

Then she made Sven read every word.

Then she made Pell read one paragraph aloud because his voice had become too relaxed and she disliked the trend.

Pell read:

“Northstar Collateral Services acknowledges full satisfaction of Andersen Freight obligation NF-KM-8821 upon receipt of settlement funds. Northstar withdraws all preservation notices, collateral-access demands, and disputed bundled-asset assertions relating to the racing craft *Opportunity Knocks*. Northstar asserts no continuing lien, claim, access right, title interest, possession right, repair-control interest, race-earning claim, storage claim, or preservation authority over *Opportunity Knocks* arising from Andersen Freight, historical business support, storage aboard *Last Good Haul*, or any related disputed theory.”

He stopped.

Freya looked at him.

Pell continued quickly.

“This release does not constitute admission by Sven Andersen, Freya Andersen, Ingrid Andersen, Nora Andersen, Andersen Freight, or the Andersen family that Northstar’s prior asserted claim was valid. Any unrelated lawful claim, if any, must be asserted through ordinary legal process and may not rely on the withdrawn preservation theory.”

Freya looked at Mara.

Mara said, “That says what it must say.”

Freya looked at Talan Or.

Talan Or said, “IFP clearing accepts the revision for protected settlement release.”

Freya looked at Sven.

Sven was staring at the page.

His face had gone too still.

“Sven.”

He swallowed.

“They can’t call her collateral from the freight debt.”

“No.”

“They can’t demand access because she was stored aboard *Last Good Haul*.”

“No.”

“They can’t claim prize earnings from her future races.”

“No.”

“They can’t say paying them means we agreed they were right.”

“No.”

Freya let herself answer the last one slowly.

“No.”

He put one hand over his mouth.

Freya waited.

Mara waited.

Even Pell had enough sense to wait.

Sven lowered his hand.

“I didn’t know how much of me was still standing in the cargo bay,” he said.

Freya did not touch him yet.

If she touched him, she might stop being able to read.

“Stay in the office,” she said.

He laughed once, broken and quiet.

“Yes.”

Freya signed only after the title memorandum changed *family asset* to *privately titled Andersen family property* in every place it mattered and once in a place it probably did not, because she was not in a generous mood.

The settlement transmission left protected routing at 14:07 station time.

Northstar acknowledged receipt at 14:09.

At 14:10, the debt line changed status.

SATISFIED IN FULL.

Freya stared at it.

Not because she did not understand it.

Because understanding took longer than reading.

Sven whispered, “Is that real?”

Freya looked at the status, the release packet, the title memo, the IFP clearing confirmation, Mara’s countersignature, and the station timestamp.

“Yes,” she said.

Then she corrected herself, because truth mattered.

“It is documented.”

Sven looked at her.

Freya’s eyes burned.

“Documented is better than real,” she said. “Real can be argued with. Documented has copies.”

Mara Venn nodded solemnly.

“That may be the finest thing anyone has said in this office.”

Freya stood before anyone could applaud paperwork and ruin it.

“I want copies in station archive, IFP clearing archive, our family secure file, and the ship’s title file.”

Pell nodded. “Already routed.”

“I want the creditor-contact restriction to remain active for any investigation-related matter.”

Mara answered that one. “Active.”

“I want no public notification of debt satisfaction unless we approve language.”

Feli Brandt’s voice came from the room speaker, startling Sven enough that he nearly knocked over a water bulb.

“Already waiting outside your door with Communications restraint and two teas.”

Freya closed her eyes.

“Feli.”

“Yes?”

“How long have you been there?”

“Long enough to admire your work. Not long enough to become evidence.”

Mara said, “Debatable.”

Feli replied, “I brought tea.”

“Mitigating.”

Freya looked at Sven.

For the first time in the meeting, she let herself take his hand.

His fingers closed around hers hard enough to hurt.

She allowed it for three seconds.

Then she squeezed back harder.

“We still have work,” she said.

“I know.”

But for once, the work was not falling through a hole underneath them.

It sat on a table.

It had names.

It had signatures.

It could be filed.

Crew Support had given the Andersens a room with a table large enough for documents and small enough that Nora could not use it as a runway.

This had been wise.

Freya returned with the confirmed packet in a secure sleeve, Sven beside her, and the strange silence that followed people who had just left a room carrying proof that a nightmare had become administratively inconvenient.

Ingrid sat with Nora on the floor.

Moon-bear was wearing a paper crown that said FIRST PLACE PRIVATE SUPPORT OBJECT, because Keisha Bell had read Feli’s message aloud and immediately regretted nothing.

Nora looked up.

“Are the papers done?”

Freya stopped.

Sven stopped with her.

Ingrid saw their faces and set down the cup she was holding.

“The important papers,” Freya said, “are done for today.”

Nora frowned. “That is grown-up for no.”

“It is grown-up for yes, but carefully.”

Sven sat down on the floor because the chair required too much dignity.

Nora climbed into his lap as if he had not recently become a race winner and legal adult with settlement documents. To her, he remained a father-shaped structure whose knees were under-regulated.

“Can the papers take Opportunity?” she asked.

Freya looked at Sven.

Sven looked at Freya.

Then Freya crouched so she was eye level with Nora.

“No,” Freya said.

Nora’s mouth opened.

Freya held up a finger.

“The papers we fixed today say Northstar cannot take *Opportunity Knocks* because of Grandpa Lars’s freight debt, because she was stored on *Last Good Haul*, because Papa raced her, or because they once used confusing words.”

Nora listened with serious effort.

“Northstar is the mean papers?”

“Northstar is the company that sent the mean papers.”

“Are they still mean?”

“Yes,” Freya said.

Ingrid made a small approving sound.

Sven looked at Freya, surprised.

Freya did not look away from Nora.

“They may still be mean. But these papers say they cannot take the ship that way.”

Nora looked at moon-bear.

Moon-bear, crowned and seated against a cushion, appeared unmoved by creditor law.

“Can they take moon-bear?”

“No,” Sven said immediately.

Freya nodded. “Moon-bear has excellent title protection.”

Nora hugged the bear.

“Good.”

Ingrid stood slowly.

She crossed to Freya and held out one hand for the packet.

Freya gave it to her.

Ingrid did not pretend to understand every line at a glance. That was not her way. She sat at the table, put on the reading lenses she hated, and read the first page.

Then the second.

Then the title memo.

Freya waited.

Sven waited.

Nora whispered to moon-bear that Grandma was doing law.

At last, Ingrid removed the lenses.

“She is ours,” Ingrid said.

Sven closed his eyes.

Freya’s face twisted once and steadied.

“Yes,” she said.

Ingrid put one palm flat on the packet.

“Not won.”

“No.”

“Not rescued.”

“No.”

“Confirmed.”

Freya nodded.

“Confirmed.”

Ingrid’s hand remained there a moment longer.

Then she said, “Good. I am too old to haunt a finance office.”

Nora gasped. “Grandma!”

“What? I would have been tasteful.”

Sven laughed.

It came out wrong.

Too close to crying.

Nora patted his cheek.

“Papa, you are leaking.”

“I am not.”

“You are.”

“Ingrid, please tell my daughter I am not leaking.”

Ingrid looked at him.

“You are leaking.”

Freya sat beside him on the floor.

For a few minutes, no one asked what came next.

That was new.

The next thing had been chasing them for so long that its absence felt like another presence in the room.

Eventually, the station wall panel chimed softly.

Crew Support did not knock with urgency unless urgency had signed for itself.

Keisha Bell’s voice came through.

“Freya, Sven, Mara asked whether you want the IFP custody-transfer notice now or after dinner.”

Freya looked at Sven.

Sven looked down at Nora.

Nora was adjusting moon-bear’s crown.

“After dinner,” Sven said.

Freya’s eyes moved to him.

He added, “Unless it changes safety.”

Keisha answered, “It does not change immediate family safety. It is confirmation only.”

Freya said, “After dinner.”

“Good,” Keisha said. “Dinner exists in twelve minutes. Nora, moon-bear’s crown has been ruled compliant.”

Nora beamed.

Dinner existed.

That was also new.

The custody transfer happened without the Andersens watching.

That was Janelle Okonkwo’s recommendation, Mara’s agreement, and Freya’s decision after reading the notification twice.

Sven had wanted to see them leave.

Not because he wanted revenge.

He told himself that, and mostly believed it.

He wanted to see Callum Dresk and Ilya Voren removed from the station because part of him was still in the corridor with Freya behind him, Nora somewhere above, and two false names trying to turn shame into surrender.

Freya had listened.

Then she had said, “I do not want them in another family memory.”

So they did not go.

Instead, they received the notice afterward in a private Compliance room with Mara, Janelle, and an IFP liaison whose name was Deputy Ral Omari and whose voice sounded like a locked cabinet.

Janelle stood with her hands behind her back.

“Callum Dresk and Ilya Voren have been transferred from station detention to IFP custody for cross-jurisdiction processing,” she said. “Station-side evidence package transferred with custody. Copies remain preserved in station evidence archive.”

Sven sat beside Freya.

Freya had the secure sleeve in her lap.

“What evidence?” she asked.

Janelle nodded to Mara, who brought up a list.

“False identity use to enter Der Himmelskrug under concealed business purpose. Unauthorized tracker continuity connected to *Last Good Haul*’s departure from Kestrel Minor. Attempted coercive contact in a controlled crew-support-adjacent route. Obstruction of movement. Intimidation tied to disputed lien language. Misrepresentation of practical authority. Access-pattern irregularities. Witness statements from Calder Yim and Pia Stern. Security logs. Arrival Control entries. Corridor footage under privacy restrictions. Northstar communications routed through discovery hold where law allows.”

Freya read the list.

“Corridor footage under privacy restrictions,” she said.

Mara answered, “Not public. Not feed-accessible. Not released outside legal process without review.”

“Good.”

Deputy Omari added, “IFP custody does not mean conviction. It means the matter has crossed the threshold for formal route-security and cross-jurisdiction review.”

Sven nodded.

He appreciated the precision.

He hated needing it.

Janelle said, “They left in hand restraints.”

Mara looked at her.

Janelle looked back.

“What? We do not run a prison. We run a station.”

Sven almost smiled.

Freya did not.

“Do they know the debt is paid?” Freya asked.

Mara’s expression sharpened.

“They may learn through counsel. They do not receive operational family updates.”

“Good.”

Deputy Omari continued. “Northstar Collateral Services is also under preliminary investigation for lien-abuse patterning, predatory refinancing concerns, disputed collateral expansion, and possible coordination with coercive seizure tactics.”

“Preliminary,” Freya repeated.

“Yes.”

“Not solved.”

“No.”

“Not punishment yet.”

“No.”

“Not nothing.”

“No,” Omari said. “Not nothing.”

Freya looked at Sven.

Then at Mara.

“And Mirador?”

Mara did not soften the answer.

“The two field agents are in IFP custody. Mirador Asset Protection Group’s wider conduct remains under review. The station has issued access restrictions and evidence-preservation notices. We are also auditing any prior station-adjacent contacts or attempted credential use. Wider accountability will take longer.”

Sven felt the old anger move.

Not hot.

Tired.

“Longer,” he said.

Janelle’s voice was dry. “People who write crimes in administrative language often require administrative patience before they meet faster consequences.”

Freya looked at her.

“That is terrible.”

“Yes.”

“Also useful.”

“Yes.”

Mara slid another document forward.

“This is the protective notice. It preserves family-contact restrictions, creditor-channel limits, and investigation-related communications boundaries. It also confirms *Opportunity Knocks* is not subject to any station-recognized Northstar access request.”

Freya read it.

Sven watched her read it.

He had watched her read death estimates, credit notices, ship repair quotes, school intake forms, freight cancellations, and race rules.

This was the first document he had ever seen make her shoulders lower.

Only a little.

Enough.

She signed receipt.

Not agreement.

Receipt.

Freya had opinions about verbs now.

When the meeting ended, Janelle walked them to the controlled Crew Support corridor herself.

Halfway there, Sven said, “Thank you.”

Janelle did not slow.

“For what part?”

“All of it.”

“That is too broad to survive audit.”

“For the corridor.”

She glanced at him.

“For saying no.”

Janelle stopped then.

The corridor around them was quiet, controlled, plain. Doors that meant things. Lights that did not blink for drama. A station built from ridiculous premise and serious boundaries.

“You said no first,” Janelle said.

Sven looked at her.

“You held the line until we arrived. Do not surrender that to Security because we had better uniforms.”

Freya’s fingers tightened around the secure sleeve.

Sven nodded.

Janelle resumed walking.

“Also, your towel use was irregular.”

Freya closed her eyes.

Sven said, “I was under stress.”

“I wrote that in the report.”

“You wrote about the towel?”

“I preserve evidence.”

Mara, walking behind them, murmured, “The towel is not public.”

“Good,” Freya said.

“No,” Sven said. “Unjust.”

Janelle’s mouth almost moved.

Almost.

That evening, Feli Brandt came to Crew Support with tea, a slate, and the expression of a communications director prepared to be disliked for the correct reasons.

Keisha came with her.

Mara did not, which Freya noticed.

“Mara?” Freya asked.

“Available if needed,” Feli said. “Absent by design. This is not a compliance decision unless you want it to be. This is a story decision.”

Sven sat beside Freya at the table.

Ingrid took the chair near the wall.

Nora was in the sleeping alcove, not asleep, because children recognized important adult voices and treated bedtime as an opening argument.

Keisha had placed a quiet sound buffer at the alcove entrance and told Nora it was a moon-bear privacy tent.

Nora had accepted this and immediately begun whispering legal advice to the bear.

Feli placed the slate on the table.

“The public wants the story,” she said.

Sven winced.

Freya did not.

Feli continued. “The station can continue giving limited race statements and no personal comment. That remains available. You owe no one a human-interest package.”

Ingrid’s eyes narrowed at the phrase.

Feli noticed.

“Yes. That is the ugly term. I use it here because ugly terms should be made to stand in the light.”

Freya nodded once.

“What do they know?” Sven asked.

“Public facts: you won the Himmelskrug Run in *Opportunity Knocks*. The craft is privately owned. The family required privacy. Flight Control confirmed a legal finish. Race Ops certified the result. No public statement has been made about private debt, Lars’s illness, Northstar, Mirador, the corridor incident, family lodging, or Nora.”

Freya said, “Good.”

“Speculation exists,” Feli said. “Speculation always exists. Some of it is sentimental. Some of it is predatory. Some of it has excellent graphics and terrible ethics.”

Sven looked toward the sleeping alcove.

“No Nora.”

“Already locked,” Feli said. “No images. No name beyond what is already unavoidable in controlled family context. No child reaction. No school, lodging, route, or support detail.”

Freya said, “No debt number.”

“Correct.”

“No Lars medical history.”

“Correct.”

“No corridor footage.”

“Correct.”

“No interview where someone asks Sven when he knew he was flying for his family.”

Feli’s face changed by one degree.

“That question has already been submitted by three outlets. I rejected all three so hard one filed a complaint about tone.”

Ingrid said, “Did you enjoy that?”

“Yes,” Feli said. “Professionally.”

Keisha placed tea in front of Ingrid.

Ingrid accepted it like tribute.

Sven rubbed both hands over his face.

“I don’t know what to say.”

“That is why I brought a blank slate and not a camera,” Feli said.

Freya looked at the blank slate.

For weeks, every document had told her what someone else wanted the truth to become.

This one waited.

That made it more frightening.

“What happens if we say nothing?” she asked.

“Nothing official from you. The race remains public. The legal matters remain private or process-bound. The station keeps saying privacy required. Commentators keep replaying legal race footage. People invent what they invent.”

“And if we say something?”

“Then you shape a small true thing before others build a large false one.”

Freya looked at Sven.

Sven looked at Ingrid.

Ingrid looked toward the sleeping alcove.

The sound buffer hummed softly. Behind it, Nora whispered, “Moon-bear objects,” to no one who had standing.

Ingrid smiled faintly.

“Not tonight,” she said.

Feli waited.

Ingrid turned back.

“I have something I may say about Lars. Later. Not because they ask. Because he should not be remembered only by debt and absence.”

Sven’s throat closed.

Freya reached for his hand under the table.

Feli nodded.

“Then not tonight.”

Freya looked at her. “You can do that? Put a story in stages?”

Feli’s smile was small and fierce.

“My entire profession is keeping people from pouring the whole barrel because someone asked for a cup.”

Keisha said, “Sometimes she lets them smell the barrel.”

“Only under supervision.”

Sven laughed softly despite himself.

Freya looked at the blank slate again.

“What do we want to say now?”

No one answered quickly.

That was better.

Fast answers were how families became slogans.

Finally, Sven said, “Thank Flight Control.”

Feli typed.

Freya said, “Garage Ops. Oskar’s team. Engineering. Crew Support. Security. Compliance.”

Keisha lifted her tea. “Accepted without making it weird.”

Mara was not in the room, but Freya added, “And Mara by title, not by personal detail.”

Feli typed.

Ingrid said, “The ship is private family property.”

Freya nodded. “Yes.”

Sven said, “Not a station trophy.”

Feli typed that in different words.

Freya said, “No debt number. No medical details. No statement about Northstar or Mirador beyond respecting investigation.”

Feli typed.

Ingrid held her cup with both hands.

“We can say Lars taught Sven to listen later,” she said. “When it is ours to say properly.”

Sven looked down.

Freya squeezed his hand.

Feli did not type that line.

Not yet.

She only made a small note at the bottom:

FAMILY-APPROVED LARS QUOTE — HOLD FOR LATER STATEMENT

They worked for nearly an hour and produced six sentences.

Feli called that a triumph.

Ren Calder would have called it starvation.

Maude Kess, Feli suspected, would understand.

The draft read:

Sven Andersen and his family are grateful to Der Himmelskrug Flight Control, Race Ops, Starship Garage, Engineering, Crew Support, Security, Compliance, race staff, and the workers who made the Himmelskrug Run possible. *Opportunity Knocks* is privately owned Andersen family property. The family is proud of the race and grateful for the support that allowed safety, process, and privacy to be preserved. Private family, medical, financial, support, and investigation matters will not be discussed publicly. The family asks that any ongoing legal process be allowed to continue through proper channels. Further comment, if any, will come from the family in its own time.

Freya read it.

Sven read it.

Ingrid read it.

Keisha read it and nodded.

Feli waited.

“It is not warm,” Sven said.

Freya looked at him.

He added quickly, “I am not saying that is bad.”

“It is warm enough,” Ingrid said. “People can bring their own blankets.”

Feli looked delighted.

Freya read the statement again.

“It does not say why.”

“No,” Feli said.

“It does not say what they did.”

“No.”

“It does not say what we owe or owed.”

“No.”

“It does not say Lars.”

“No.”

“It says we choose later.”

“Yes.”

Freya set the slate down.

For the first time since Kestrel Minor, choice did not feel like a trap disguised as a door.

She looked at Sven.

Then Ingrid.

Then toward the sleeping alcove, where Nora had stopped whispering and might actually have fallen asleep under moon-bear’s legal counsel.

Freya said, “We will tell the story.”

Feli’s fingers hovered over the slate.

Freya continued.

“But we will tell it in our order.”

Sven nodded.

Ingrid lifted her tea.

Keisha smiled.

Feli saved the draft under family approval pending final morning review.

Outside the protected room, Der Himmelskrug remained loud, curious, grateful, ridiculous, and hungry for a story.

Inside, the family kept the first real version for themselves.

The debt was paid.

The ship was theirs.

The investigation had begun.

The public could wait.

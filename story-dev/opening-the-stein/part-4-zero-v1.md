# Opening the Stein — Part 4: Zero

Status: draft-only story prose. Not active canon until completed, audited, and promoted.

At twelve minutes to public opening, Feli Brandt stood at the Hospitality Level 1 event console and watched the station become a list that could breathe.

**Arrival Control:** ready.

**Flight Control:** green enough.

**Hospitality:** ready.

**Taproom:** ready.

**H2 food hall:** corrected and verified.

**Residential:** contained and quiet.

**Entertainment:** program adjusted.

**Medical:** no public note needed.

**Media:** positioned where allowed.

**Founder:** present.

Feli looked at the last line longer than the others.

Heinz stood near the public speaking mark, not under the chandelier line, because the angle could still enjoy disappointment. He held his slate in both hands. He was not reading from it anymore. That was either excellent or catastrophic. Feli decided she would find out with everyone else.

Above Arrival Control, the official countdown moved through its final minutes in gold numerals. Beneath it, the release doors remained closed. Beyond them, on the other side of screening and declarations and the public release gate, the first cleared visitor group waited in the Transit Reception Center under Arrival Control’s care.

They had not appeared in the taproom.

They had not been spirited dramatically into the atrium.

They had arrived like people entering a real station: through clearance, identity confirmation, customs, clean-route declarations, baggage handling, weapons declarations, allergen and access needs, and the quiet procedural work that kept welcome from becoming carelessness.

Feli watched the Arrival Control liaison on her slate.

“Final visitor group status?” she asked.

“First public wave cleared and holding at release,” came the answer. “Families, route crew, two workers in transit, partner-colony delegation, four curious tourists, one skeptical repeat traveler, three media-cleared public guests, and one child who has asked whether the bubble elevators are alive.”

Feli smiled. “Are they?”

“Arrival Control declines to establish theology.”

“Good. Release remains on countdown.”

“Confirmed. Release on zero.”

The exterior feed shifted across the H1 display wall.

Der Himmelskrug hung against the black: ridiculous, warm-lit, impossible not to understand as a stein once someone said the word, impossible to reduce to one once the approach lanes came into view. Ships held in disciplined arcs around the upper handle. A service vehicle glided where it belonged and nowhere else. The first public-arrival transport sat docked and quiet, already part of the station’s body.

Amara’s voice entered the coordination channel.

“Flight Control to opening coordination. Final approach order locked. First wave docked cleanly. One late private vessel has discovered chronology. It is coping in the hold.”

Feli touched her earpiece. “Any danger?”

“No.”

“Any drama?”

“Not operationally.”

“Are we green?”

“Green enough to proceed. If anyone asks, say that. If anyone important asks, say exactly that.”

“I always do.”

“No, you improve me for public digestion.”

“I remove shrapnel.”

“You leave useful bruising.”

“Thank you.”

“That was not praise.”

“It worked like praise.”

Amara made a sound very close to a laugh and cut the channel.

On another display, Starlight Tonight’s exterior commentator leaned into the shot with visible effort not to grin too hard.

“There it is. Der Himmelskrug, the station everyone has been joking about for months. From a distance, yes, you can see why. But around it—look at the traffic pattern. Look at the lane discipline. Whatever else this is, it is not a prop.”

Feli let that play for two seconds, then muted it before anyone could ruin the moment by being pleased too loudly.

The opening program shifted into its final track.

Mirelle Voss’s rehearsal recording entered the H1 atrium not as spectacle, but as atmosphere: a warm instrumental line with her voice folded into the back of it, wordless and steady. No camera cut to the performer. No hungry backstage angle. No explanation that turned privacy into a smaller kind of exposure.

Tariq sent one message:

**ENTERTAINMENT:**  
Room accepts the adjustment. Celebration holding.

Feli replied:

**COMMUNICATIONS:**  
Good room.

Tariq sent back:

**ENTERTAINMENT:**  
Finally, someone says it.

Janna stood beside Feli, slate tucked against her chest.

“Press pool B is asking whether the performer will appear later.”

“Answer?”

“Opening program adjusted to center first public arrival and station reveal. Performer not available for pre-show media. No further personal detail.”

“Good.”

“They hate it.”

“They may bond with disappointment.”

Janna nodded. “Galaxy News wants the traffic priority link again.”

“Send it. Also the labor overview and the published access map.”

“Civic Meridian wants a quote on governance.”

“Give them the prepared language after Heinz speaks.”

“Starlight wants a child reaction to the bubble elevators.”

“No faces without guardian consent.”

“Already said it.”

Feli looked at her.

Janna shrugged. “I learn.”

The countdown reached five minutes.

H2 reported safe first service staged. Naya was on the corrected line by request. Feli saved the note and did not turn it into a story. Some victories belonged to training logs.

Priya reported Residential quiet. Not silent. Quiet. Feli knew enough now to understand the difference.

Mateo reported the first-pour glass was clean, chilled correctly, and not to be touched by anyone with a title.

Lukas reported: **Foam will be acceptable if people stop emotionally warming the room.**

Mateo followed: **He means ready.**

Heinz looked across the atrium and found Feli.

He did not ask her to read the speech.

He did not mouth a question.

He simply lifted the slate a little, as if to say he still had it, and then lowered it.

Feli touched two fingers to her own slate.

Not a salute.

Not a rescue.

A hand at the far end of a bridge.

The countdown reached one minute.

The H1 atrium changed.

It did not grow louder. If anything, the sound became more precise. Public music softened beneath the readiness tones. The public lift banks glowed active for Observation, Entertainment, Hospitality Level 1, Hospitality Level 2, and Retail. Their signs named where they went and where they did not. Staff at controlled routes adjusted their stances. Press cameras settled onto assigned marks. Taproom workers stopped pretending not to watch the release doors.

Feli opened the all-staff opening channel.

“Opening coordination to all departments. Final minute. Public release remains through Arrival Control only. Hold your lanes.”

One by one, the answers came back.

“Flight Control holding law,” Amara said.

“Arrival Control holding release,” the liaison said.

“Hospitality holding warmth,” Mateo said.

“Taproom holding quality,” Lukas said.

“Food hall holding labels,” H2 said.

“Residential holding quiet,” Priya said.

“Entertainment holding the room,” Tariq said.

“Medical holding privacy,” came the quiet advisory.

There was a brief pause.

Then Heinz said, “Founder holding speech.”

Feli looked at him.

He looked terrified.

Good, she thought.

Not because fear was pleasant. Because it meant he understood he was not opening a monument to himself.

The countdown reached ten.

The atrium display stopped rotating through welcome language and became a single shared count.

Ten.

Feli did not count aloud.

Nine.

Across the atrium, Mateo clasped his hands behind his back with the discipline of a man preventing them from interfering with a glass.

Eight.

Lukas stared at the backup line as if daring it to develop a personality.

Seven.

Janna whispered, “Oh no.”

Feli did not look away from the doors. “What?”

“I am excited.”

“Terrible timing.”

“I know.”

Six.

The exterior feed showed the station bright against dark.

Five.

Press pool B stopped fidgeting.

Four.

Somewhere in the controlled corridor beyond the doors, a child laughed.

Three.

Heinz closed his slate.

Two.

Feli breathed once.

One.

Zero.

The Arrival Control release doors opened.

Not dramatically.

Correctly.

A soft tone sounded first. Then the release indicators changed from gold to green with text, icon, and audible confirmation. Staff at the threshold smiled and did not step into the path. The first visitors crossed from controlled corridor into Hospitality Level 1.

For one second, they all slowed.

That was the moment no press package could have built.

A woman in a route-crew jacket stopped with one hand still on the strap of her bag. Her face did not become awed in the theatrical way. It became tired in reverse, as if some part of her body had believed it would remain braced forever and had just received contrary evidence.

A child in a blue scarf pointed up at the bubble elevators. “They are alive.”

“They are public lifts,” said the adult beside him automatically.

The child considered this. “Alive public lifts.”

Arrival Control, Feli thought, might have lost that theological battle.

Two tourists laughed the instant they saw the taproom sign and the curve of the atrium levels above it.

“It really is a stein,” one said.

A skeptical older traveler, still wearing the expression of someone prepared to dislike everything on principle, looked at the signage, the release path, the staff positions, the visible rest areas, the route boards, the clear public lift labels, and the taproom entrance.

Then she said, “Huh.”

Feli marked that as one of the strongest reviews possible.

A partner-colony visitor touched the nearest wall panel, listened to the audio direction, and nodded. A worker in transit asked where crew recovery was, received an answer before frustration could form, and visibly recalculated the station from joke to tool. A media-cleared guest lifted a camera, lowered it when a staffer gently pointed out the no-face zone around the first family group, and shifted to the atrium ceiling instead.

People began moving.

Not as an audience.

As users.

That was when Der Himmelskrug opened.

Not when the doors moved. Not when the countdown hit zero. Not even when the first foot crossed the threshold.

It opened when a tired pilot saw a seat, when a child asked a question nobody mocked, when a traveler found the restroom sign before needing to ask, when a worker trusted the route board, when laughter did not stop anyone from following the rules.

Feli felt her throat tighten and chose not to swallow it away.

Heinz stepped onto the speaking mark.

The atrium did not become silent all at once. It never should have. A station going silent around a founder would have made Feli suspicious. But the program tone shifted, the public display named the opening remarks, and the nearest visitors turned enough to listen without being trapped.

Heinz looked at his slate.

Then he looked up.

“Good afternoon,” he said. “Welcome to Der Himmelskrug.”

A small ripple moved through the room. Not applause yet. Recognition.

Heinz glanced toward the taproom, then back at the visitors.

“First, to clarify a matter of public confusion: this is not a mug.”

The laugh came immediately.

Heinz waited for it. He did not flinch.

“It is much too expensive to be a mug,” he said.

The laugh grew.

“And if it were a mug, Flight Control would still not let you appear behind the beer without clearance.”

This time even Amara’s remote channel produced a small, dangerous silence that Feli chose to interpret as approval.

Heinz looked down briefly. Not to hide. To find the next part.

“People have laughed at the shape since before it had pressure seals,” he said. “That is fair. Some ideas deserve laughter while they are proving they can also carry weight.”

The room settled.

“This station began as a very stupid-looking answer to a serious question. How do you make a place where routes are safer, crews can rest, families can live, workers are not invisible, visitors are welcomed without being allowed to endanger each other, and strangers can arrive tired and still be treated like people?”

Feli did not move.

“Many people built the answer,” Heinz said. “Engineers, brewers, controllers, cleaners, cooks, medical staff, transit staff, teachers, residents, inspectors, contractors, pilots who complained usefully, partners who argued before signing anything, and workers who told us when the beautiful plan was stupid in practice.”

A few staff laughed now. Differently.

Heinz’s hand tightened once around the slate.

“This is not mine,” he said.

No joke followed.

“Not alone. Not mostly. Not where it matters. A station belongs to the people who keep it true after the speech ends. So if you came for the beer, welcome. If you came for the view, welcome. If you came because your route needed a safe stop, welcome. If you live here, work here, pass through here, doubt us, inspect us, report on us, laugh at the shape, or need a chair before you can explain anything else—welcome.”

The atrium had gone very still now, but not dead still.

Listening still.

“The rules are real,” Heinz said. “The welcome is also real. Please do not confuse either one for decoration.”

Feli heard Janna’s tiny inhale beside her.

Heinz looked toward the first visitors.

“Der Himmelskrug is open.”

This time, the applause arrived.

Not enormous at first. Human. Uneven. Then bigger as people understood they were allowed to participate. Staff did not clap first; Feli noticed and loved them for it. The first public visitors did. Then the press pool. Then Hospitality. Then, finally, workers who had been holding back all day as if applause might count as neglecting a procedure.

Heinz stood inside it looking deeply uncomfortable and entirely present.

Feli did not rescue him from that either.

The program moved to first pour.

Mateo crossed to the bar with the grace of a man who had personally threatened chaos and won by smiling at it. Lukas stood behind the line, hands clean, eyes on pressure and glass angle and nothing else. The ceremonial tap remained closed beside them, visible and unused. The backup line, polished and verified and now carrying the weight of honesty, waited one meter and forty centimeters away from the original camera mark.

A producer whispered something.

Mateo did not look at them.

The first glass went under the verified line.

For a moment, the whole station seemed to narrow to beer meeting glass.

The pour was not fast.

It was not theatrical.

It was correct.

Amber-gold, steady, clean. Foam rose with infuriating discipline to the line Lukas had chosen in his head before anyone else knew there was a line to choose. Mateo did not smile until the foam settled. Lukas did not smile then either. He leaned closer, checked the bead, the clarity, the head, the way the glass held its own small weather.

Then his face changed.

Only slightly.

Enough.

Feli saw Heinz see it.

That was the family-history beat no speech needed to carry.

Mateo lifted the glass, turned it toward the room, and said, “First public pour.”

Applause again. Cameras took the image they had wanted all day without ever knowing how close they had come to wanting the wrong one.

Starlight Tonight’s host whispered into a live feed, “There it is. The first beer poured at the galaxy’s most improbable station, and yes, the joke writes itself—but look at the staff watching that glass. This is not kitsch pretending to be culture. This is craft pretending not to be nervous.”

Civic Meridian’s lower third read:

**PUBLIC OPENING BEGINS AFTER CONTROLLED ARRIVAL RELEASE**

Galaxy News, to Feli’s private satisfaction, led with:

**FIRST VISITORS ENTER THROUGH FORMAL CLEARANCE AS STATION OPENS PUBLIC OPERATIONS**

Not perfect headlines.

Good enough headlines.

True enough to stand.

The performer issue did not appear.

That did not mean it had been hidden. It meant it had been kept proportional. A tired performer rested privately while a station opened publicly. Both were true. Only one belonged to the room.

The H2 food hall began service with the corrected Veyr-Kol labels. Naya served the first guest on that line, read the allergen language exactly, and did not rush when the guest asked a follow-up question. Somewhere, Feli hoped, a training designer felt smug.

Residential stayed quiet.

Priya sent no message. That was its own message.

Flight Control released its next update: first wave stable, approach lanes disciplined, no emergency vectors, one pilot still sad about not being historic.

Amara added: **Do not include that last part.**

Feli did not.

Tariq stood at the edge of the Entertainment level overlooking the public atrium, watching the adjusted program become invisible in the best possible way. Mirelle’s rehearsal line warmed the space without requiring her body to become proof of anything. Visitors did not know what had almost happened. They did not need to. They only knew the room felt ready to receive them.

H2 guests began eating.

Taproom guests began asking questions about flights, rooms, routes, and what exactly was in the beer, in that order.

The child in the blue scarf pressed both hands to the bubble elevator glass and whispered something serious to it. The public lift responded with a polite destination tone.

The skeptical traveler asked a staffer whether the Services level was public.

“No,” the staffer said.

“Good,” the traveler said. “I hate when places let me wander into plumbing.”

Feli nearly laughed.

Across the atrium, Heinz had escaped the speaking mark but not the room. People approached him in small, manageable increments. A route-crew woman shook his hand and said something that made him look down for half a second before answering. A tourist asked whether the stein shape had been his idea. Heinz said yes in the tone of a man prepared to accept consequences. The tourist laughed and then asked where to find route updates.

That, too, was opening.

Not admiration.

Use.

Behind the bar, Mateo stopped pretending he had never been worried. He leaned one hip against the counter for half a second, exhaled, and then straightened before any guest could make eye contact with his relief.

Lukas checked the foam line twice on the second pour.

Then a third time.

Then, apparently satisfied that the universe had not personally betrayed him, he allowed himself to serve.

Janna stood beside Feli at the event console, eyes wet and furious about it.

“Do not say anything,” Janna said.

“I wasn’t.”

“You were thinking kindly.”

“I apologize.”

“Accepted.”

The opening channel, which had been a flood all day, became a thread of small confirmations.

**ARRIVAL CONTROL:** first wave released, second wave holding.

**FLIGHT CONTROL:** lanes stable.

**HOSPITALITY:** H1 guest flow good.

**TAPROOM:** first service active.

**H2:** corrected line in service.

**RESIDENTIAL:** quiet.

**ENTERTAINMENT:** room celebratory.

**MEDICAL:** no public issues.

Feli looked at the list.

The station had not stopped needing work. It never would. Already, somewhere, a sign would be confusing someone. A guest would be standing under the restroom marker and asking where the restrooms were. A pilot would be attempting philosophy. A server would be catching something a document had failed to make usable. A resident would need sleep more than symbolism.

But that was all future weather.

For this breath, Der Himmelskrug was open.

Heinz appeared beside her without announcing himself.

He had lost the slate.

Or, more likely, surrendered it to a pocket.

For a while they stood without speaking, watching visitors stop looking at the station as an object and start moving through it as a place.

A family checked the food hall map. A route crew chose a table with their backs to a wall and visibly relaxed. Two reporters compared notes beside the press boundary and did not cross it. The child in the blue scarf entered a bubble elevator with reverence. The elevator rose through the public atrium, carrying the child’s delighted face past warm lights, signs, glass, steel, and the inner curve of the most absurd serious thing Feli had ever helped defend.

Heinz said, very quietly, “Was the speech stupid?”

Feli did not answer right away.

He looked at her. “That is not reassuring.”

“Parts of it,” she said.

He closed his eyes.

“The right parts,” she added.

He opened them.

The applause had moved on without them. The room had become louder, looser, more ordinary. Someone laughed at the taproom sign. Someone asked where crew recovery was. Someone said the exterior looked even stranger from inside, which made no physical sense but felt emotionally accurate.

Heinz leaned closer, not enough for cameras to notice.

“I wanted to say more,” he said.

“I know.”

“You stopped me.”

“Yes.”

“Good.”

Feli glanced at him. “Careful. You’ll sound emotionally mature.”

“That would be off-brand.”

“Deeply.”

His hand brushed hers below the edge of the console, hidden by the angle, cables, slate shadows, and the civilized mercy of no camera access. He did not take her hand. Not quite. Just touched two fingers to hers, small and warm and there.

Feli let the contact stay.

Across the atrium, the first public pour reached a visitor’s table. The visitor lifted the glass, admired it like a person, not a symbol, and took a drink.

They smiled.

Not for cameras.

Not for Heinz.

Not for the story.

For the beer.

Feli felt Heinz see that too.

“No,” she said softly.

He looked at her.

“The speech was not stupid. It sounded like you finally stopped trying to open a station and welcomed people into one.”

Heinz looked away, fast enough that anyone else might have missed it.

Feli did not.

Below them, Der Himmelskrug kept opening in all the ordinary ways that mattered: doors guiding, signs answering, staff correcting, traffic holding, beer pouring, food labeling, children misclassifying elevators, tired people sitting down, strangers becoming guests without becoming problems.

From outside, it was still ridiculous.

From inside, it was work.

And, at last, it was a place.

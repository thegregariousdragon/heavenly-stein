# Opening the Stein — Part 2: Two Hours

Status: draft-only story prose. Not active canon until completed, audited, and promoted.

At two hours and three minutes to public opening, Feli had just convinced **Galaxy News** that “labor numbers” were not the sort of thing one could responsibly reduce to a cheerful lower-third graphic, when Hospitality Level 2 called up with the voice of someone trying not to use the word **allergen** on an opening-day channel.

“Communications,” Feli said, already reaching for the internal incident note.

A woman’s voice answered, brisk, precise, and one half-step away from swearing professionally.

“H2 food hall coordination. We have a label mismatch on the Veyr-Kol colony line.”

Feli stopped walking.

Hospitality Level 1 continued around her: press-suite lights adjusted to camera-safe warmth, taproom staff crossing and recrossing the far threshold, Arrival Control still sealed behind its release doors, countdown displays now forcibly reconciled to station time. A public-facing banner rolled through its welcome sequence in four languages and two accessibility modes.

Feli lowered her voice. “Guest impact?”

“None. Caught before service.”

“Good. Define mismatch.”

A rustle of slates and kitchen noise came through: carts, induction hum, dish-return conveyors not yet dealing with actual guests, someone calling temperatures in the background with the clipped patience of a person who trusted thermometers more than hope.

“The colony specialty is **sunroot dumplings with rauch-kelp oil**. Supplier tag says Veyr-Kol sunroot paste, general safe. Prep sheet says modified batch, festival substitute. Public-facing label says nut-free, seed-free, shell-free, standard vegetarian. The substitute binder is not standard vegetarian by H2 definition, and the trade name does not map cleanly onto the allergen slate.”

Feli put one hand against the coordination console.

“Tell me the simple version.”

“The label is wrong in three different honest ways.”

“Can it hurt someone?”

“If served under the current label, yes.”

“Has it been served?”

“No.”

“Is it in public reach?”

“No. It nearly got routed to the general line because the service tag matched the colony menu card and not the corrected prep note.”

Feli exhaled once.

That was not relief. Relief came after the fix.

“Who caught it?”

“Line server. Naya. She asked why the oil smelled different from the training batch.”

“Good nose.”

“Good training. Then Sanaa’s substitute flag caught up late and confirmed supplier wording was useless for front-line service.”

“Is Medical looped?”

“Advisory only. No exposure.”

“Current action?”

“Item isolated. General line stopped. Colony line paused. Replacement batch possible in forty minutes if we use the verified standard paste. If we relabel and keep the substitute, we need full guest-facing allergen language, server briefing, and Medical-readable slate update. If we pull it, the Veyr-Kol delegation will notice because it is the only dish on the opening menu with their home seasoning profile.”

Feli looked toward the press suite.

Through the glass, a producer was watching the H1 taproom with the hungry calm of someone hoping for either poetry or disaster. Preferably both, because disaster edited better.

Feli said, “Nobody says ‘kitchen chaos.’ Nobody says ‘minor label issue.’ Nobody says ‘colony dish problem.’ We say: H2 caught a service-label mismatch during final verification and is holding the item until guest-facing information is correct.”

A pause.

The H2 coordinator said, “That is very long.”

“That is because the truth has load-bearing parts.”

“Do you want the dish pulled?”

“I want the correct authority to decide. Who owns the line?”

“Food hall lead owns service. Sanaa owns supply chain. Medical owns advisory consequence. Colony liaison is being notified before anyone invents cultural embarrassment.”

“Good. Keep me updated. And tell Naya that Communications heard ‘good training’ before anyone turned it into luck.”

“I will.”

The channel closed.

Feli made three notes.

**H2: system caught issue before guest exposure.**

**Public language: not a blooper. Labels are infrastructure.**

**Ask Lian if Veyr-Kol line needs culturally careful wording.**

Then she opened a private message to the media stack.

**Do not pitch food safety as opening-day color. If asked: final service verification caught a label mismatch; item held pending correct guest-facing information. No guest exposure.**

She stared at the message for half a second.

Then she added:

**No jokes.**

She sent it.

Across the atrium, the main taproom doors opened hard enough to let out sound: metal, water pressure, one low curse, then Mateo’s voice saying, “We are not alarming the room.”

Lukas answered something Feli could not hear.

Mateo, louder: “That sentence alarmed the room.”

Feli touched her earpiece. “Taproom, Communications.”

Mateo came on too fast. “We are fine.”

“That remains the least reassuring sentence in any language.”

“We are contained.”

“Better.”

Lukas cut in. “The ceremonial line is not clearing cleanly.”

“Guest impact?”

“No.”

“Beer quality?”

“Protected.”

“First-pour timing?”

Mateo inhaled in a way that made the answer audible before he spoke. “At risk, if we insist on that tap, that angle, that camera mark, and that exact minute.”

Feli glanced up at the countdown.

**01:57:42.**

Two hours had sounded like a unit of time before opening day began. Now it sounded like a rumor.

“What are the options?” she asked.

Lukas answered with no decoration. “Option one: clear the original line and verify. If it behaves, we use it. Option two: use the backup line at the same bar, slightly different angle, same beer, quality verified. Option three: ceremonial pour happens later, after we know the line is right.”

Mateo said, “Option three makes the opening sequence look like we forgot what the building is shaped like.”

“Option three makes us honest,” Lukas said.

“Option two makes us honest and on time.”

“Only if the backup line verifies.”

“It will.”

“You are not allowed to encourage hardware.”

“I am encouraging you.”

“That is worse.”

Feli allowed herself half a smile. “Lukas, is option two safe and honest if verified?”

“Yes.”

“Mateo, can you move cameras and guest flow without making it look like emergency choreography?”

There was a tiny pause.

“Insulting question,” Mateo said.

“Can you?”

“Yes.”

“Then prepare option two. Do not abandon option one unless Lukas says so. Do not let a producer decide where beer comes from.”

Lukas said, “The first glass is not a hostage.”

Feli wrote that down immediately.

Mateo said, “We are putting that on a staff mug after this.”

“No,” Lukas said.

“Yes,” Mateo said.

“After opening,” Feli said.

Both of them accepted that because opening had become the gravity everyone could agree existed.

The taproom channel closed.

Feli turned back toward the media console and found Janna waiting with a face that said there was a new problem and the problem had brought paperwork.

“Which one?” Feli asked.

“H2?”

“Contained.”

“Taproom?”

“Symbolically dramatic, operationally contained.”

“Residential?”

Feli looked up.

Janna held out her slate.

The Residential Life alert had grown a thread.

**Residential Life Priority — escalation contained to Residential controlled route.**

**Issue:** quiet-wall assignment dispute / night-shift sleep block / move-in schedule misunderstanding.  
**Parties:** two staff households, one family arrival pending, one night-shift technician.  
**Public exposure:** none.  
**Media risk:** none if corridor remains controlled.  
**Priya Natarajan responding.**

Feli read it twice.

Then a new line appeared.

**Resident voice level: elevated.**

“Voice level,” Feli said.

“Residential uses that instead of ‘shouting’ in early notes,” Janna said.

“Very civilized.”

“It now says elevated twice.”

Feli handed the slate back. “Priya has it.”

“Do you want visibility?”

“I want enough to know whether it touches public language, staffing, or safety. I do not want to turn someone’s temporary housing argument into opening-day content.”

Janna nodded.

Feli looked toward the public arrival threshold again.

Every public story tried to eat private life eventually. Usually it wore the clothes of curiosity while it chewed.

“Set a privacy wall on any comms note that includes names,” Feli said. “No media language. No staff gossip. If anyone asks why Residential traffic is delayed, it is opening-day internal coordination.”

“Is that true?”

“Yes. It is also mercifully boring.”

Janna sent the update.

Feli’s earpiece switched channels before she could breathe.

Flight Control.

Amara said, “Opening coordination, Flight Control. We have a pilot attempting to become philosophy.”

Feli closed her eyes.

“Is the pilot armed, damaged, leaking, cloaked, undeclared, or currently a metaphor for a systems failure?”

“No.”

“Then I am grateful for philosophy.”

“The pilot’s claim is that if they are carrying press equipment for a media-cleared crew, they should count as part of the first public wave.”

“Do they?”

“No.”

“Did they enjoy finding out?”

“They are enjoying it continuously.”

On a wall display, the exterior feed cut to the beer-stein silhouette from a high public-safe angle: lights along the upper handle, approach lanes curving like disciplined ribbons, service arcs glinting on the working side. It was beautiful in the way large systems were beautiful when most of the danger had been made boring by competent people.

A tiny craft at the edge of one lane pulsed amber in the schematic.

Amara’s voice stayed warm.

“They have been assigned a hold. They have also been assigned silence.”

“Can you assign silence?”

“Not legally. Poetically.”

“Do you need Communications?”

“Only if a reporter asks why a media vehicle is not being allowed to arrive dramatically.”

“What should I say?”

“That docking order is determined by safety, declaration status, and traffic control, not by anyone’s preferred camera angle.”

Feli typed it.

“Good sentence,” she said.

“I had time while they were explaining history to me.”

“Are we still green enough?”

“Green enough. Not empty. Not easy. Controlled.”

“Thank you.”

“Do not thank me until they stop trying to make first mean exempt.”

The channel clicked off.

Feli let the exterior feed run for three more seconds. Starlight Tonight would love it. Civic Meridian would call it efficient. Galaxy News would ask whether traffic priority rules were published and reviewable, which was exactly why Feli had already prepared the link.

She looked down at her slate.

**HEINZ SCHMITT:**  
I removed the handle paragraph.

Feli stared at the message with suspicion.

A second one arrived.

**HEINZ SCHMITT:**  
Mostly.

A third.

**HEINZ SCHMITT:**  
It is now in a note.

Feli typed:

**FELI BRANDT:**  
Is the note in the speech?

Heinz did not answer immediately.

That was an answer.

Feli called him.

This time he picked up on the first pulse.

“I can explain,” he said.

“You always can. That is not the standard.”

“I moved the handle paragraph into the remarks about infrastructure because it is emotionally relevant.”

“How many paragraphs are now about infrastructure?”

“Define paragraph.”

“Heinz.”

“Four, but one is short.”

Feli walked away from the console again, not because she needed privacy but because if she remained near Janna, Janna would hear only half the conversation and develop a headache from context.

She moved along the H1 inner ring, past a line of public wayfinding signs under final test. Each sign spoke softly when touched: **Arrival release. Taproom. Café district. Restrooms. Press suite. Public lifts. Ask staff before entering controlled routes.**

Good.

The station could not depend on everyone guessing correctly while overwhelmed.

“Heinz,” she said, “you are doing it again.”

“I am making the speech accurate.”

“You are making it defensible.”

“That seems useful.”

“It is useful if you are being sued by a technical committee. Are you?”

“Not in the next two hours, I hope.”

“Then stop trying to prove the station is allowed to exist.”

He was quiet.

Behind Feli, a small maintenance panel chirped, accepted a staff badge, and sealed. Across the atrium, the taproom tested a line with the solemnity of a religious tribunal.

Heinz said, “Galaxy News asked governance questions.”

“Yes.”

“Civic Meridian asked accountability questions.”

“Yes.”

“People will wonder whether this is mine.”

“Yes.”

“It isn’t.”

“Then say that.”

“It sounds too simple.”

“The correct sentence often does, before people ruin it.”

He gave a short breath. “You are in a mood.”

“I am in six moods. Pick one.”

“The frighteningly helpful one.”

“That one says: do not answer every criticism in the opening speech. Welcome people into the place. Let governance documents answer governance questions. Let Flight Control answer traffic questions by keeping everyone alive. Let the staff answer labor questions by being visible as people, not props. Let the beer answer one question only after Lukas approves the line.”

“That was several moods.”

“They are collaborating.”

He was silent again.

Feli softened, but only enough to remove shrapnel.

“You do not have to prove the station is serious by draining all joy out of your first welcome.”

“It is shaped like a giant beer stein.”

“I had noticed.”

“They will laugh.”

“Yes.”

“You said some of the laughter is earned.”

“It is.”

“That does not mean it is harmless.”

“No. But if you flinch every time they laugh, you teach them the joke is a weapon. If you can stand inside the joke and still name the work, you teach them it is a doorway.”

There was a long pause.

Then Heinz said, “That is also too good.”

“You are not allowed to take that one. You already have enough lines.”

“I was not taking it.”

“You were absolutely taking it.”

“I was admiring it near a document.”

“Delete the extra infrastructure paragraphs.”

“I will reduce them.”

“Delete.”

“Reduce significantly.”

“Heinz.”

“I will delete three.”

“And the fourth?”

“Short.”

Feli considered it.

“Fine. One short infrastructure sentence. No handle separation.”

“Not even as a metaphor?”

“No.”

“That is disappointing because it is a very good metaphor.”

“It is a terrible metaphor.”

“It is load-bearing.”

“So help me.”

He laughed this time. Quiet, real, gone quickly.

Then his voice lowered.

“Is everything else holding?”

Feli looked across H1: press contained, taproom tense, Arrival Control closed, staff moving, lights warm, signs honest, countdown falling.

“Nothing is perfect,” she said. “Everything important is being caught.”

“That sounds worse than perfect.”

“It is better.”

“Yes,” he said. “Probably.”

“Speech.”

“Yes.”

The call ended.

Feli returned to the event console just as Janna received a new message, read it, and made a small face.

“Performer schedule?” Feli asked.

“No. Residential update from Priya.”

Feli took the slate.

**PRIYA NATARAJAN:**  
Dispute contained. No public exposure. I am moving both households into a temporary rest-protected schedule and correcting the assignment record before anyone apologizes with furniture. More later.

Feli smiled.

“Good,” she said.

Another message appeared almost immediately beneath it.

**PRIYA NATARAJAN:**  
Also, if any opening-day materials describe Residential as “behind the scenes,” remove them before I do.

Feli’s smile widened.

She typed back:

**FELI BRANDT:**  
Already done. Residential is not backstage.

A beat.

Priya replied:

**PRIYA NATARAJAN:**  
Good. Because backstage closes after the show. People live here.

Feli saved that line in the part of herself that recognized a speech when someone else accidentally wrote it.

The station clock crossed **01:31:06**.

Two hours had become ninety minutes without asking permission.

H2 updated: the Veyr-Kol dish would be held for corrected labeling and a verified replacement batch. Naya’s catch had been logged as successful training, not luck. Lian had approved culturally careful wording and removed one phrase that would have made an elder delegate polite in the way that predicted future trouble.

Taproom updated: original ceremonial line still under inspection; backup line verified for quality; camera mark shifting by one meter and forty centimeters; Mateo had already convinced the producer the new angle was more intimate.

Flight Control updated: media vehicle compliant after education; queue still heavy; release window viable.

Residential updated: corridor quiet.

Feli looked at the stack.

This was what opening looked like before public memory softened it. Not one miracle. Many catches. Many hands. Many people refusing to let the day become simpler than the truth.

Then her earpiece chimed with a tone from Entertainment.

Tariq Neumann did not open with a greeting.

They said, very softly, “Feli, how attached are we to the guest performer being live on camera in the next hour?”

Feli went still.

Janna looked up.

Across the atrium, the taproom lights warmed for another test. In the press suite, Starlight Tonight rehearsed delight. Above Arrival Control, the official countdown moved without mercy.

Feli touched two fingers to her earpiece.

“Tell me exactly what you mean,” she said.

Tariq exhaled.

“I mean the room is telling me the truth before the performer is.”

The opening tilted.

Not into danger.

Not yet.

But toward headline.

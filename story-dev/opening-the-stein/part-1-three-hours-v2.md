# Opening the Stein — Part 1: Three Hours v2

Status: revised draft-only story prose. Not active canon until completed, audited, and promoted.

At three hours to public opening, Feli Brandt had six official countdowns, three unofficial countdowns, and one private countdown she refused to name because it was mostly about whether Heinz would admit he had not, technically, found the speech.

The official countdowns were tasteful.

One floated above the Arrival Control release corridor in soft gold numerals, where the first public visitors would eventually pass through the controlled entrance and step into Hospitality Level 1. One glowed above the main taproom doors, big enough for cameras and small enough not to look desperate. One sat on the press-suite wall beside a rotating slate of approved feeds, interview times, route maps, access notices, and a cheerful reminder that **press credentials did not grant access to Command, Flight Control, Residential, Medical, brewery production, or anywhere else a person might plausibly cause trouble by believing a badge was a personality.**

The unofficial countdowns were less tasteful.

A server had drawn one on a napkin and taped it to a service cart with the heading **TIME UNTIL PEOPLE ASK WHERE THE RESTROOMS ARE WHILE STANDING UNDER THE RESTROOM SIGN**.

Someone in Hospitality had set a kitchen timer labeled **PANIC BUT QUIETLY**.

And the main taproom staff had started a handwritten board that currently read:

**First pour in 03:00:00. No, you may not touch the ceremonial tap. No, not even if you are emotionally invested. Especially then.**

Feli passed it without slowing, slate in one hand, earpiece murmuring in three channels, staff badge warm against her jacket from repeated door taps.

Hospitality Level 1 was lit like welcome and moving like labor.

The public-facing glow had already come up: warm light over polished floors, clean signage at eye and hand height, soft direction tones at the public thresholds, the atrium beyond held in a gold-and-starlight hush that would look effortless once the first visitors arrived. It did not smell like panic, which Feli considered a victory. It smelled like filtered air, sealed stone, fresh bread being kept politely in its own district, and the contained malt warmth of a brewery that knew where its own breath belonged.

Behind that, the station clicked and muttered.

Service doors opened and closed. A cleaning cart whispered over the floor and vanished through a staff route before any camera could mistake it for atmosphere. Two Hospitality workers argued in the careful, quiet tone of people who had already been told by three supervisors and one conscience not to shout. A maintenance drone crossed above a sign, paused as if judging the typography, and continued toward a panel no public visitor would ever notice unless it failed.

Feli noticed it.

Feli noticed almost everything, which was not the same as being calm.

“Feli,” said Janna from event coordination, falling into step beside her with a slate hugged to her chest. “Press pool B wants to know whether the founder will stand under the chandelier line for the remarks.”

“No.”

“They say the angle is better.”

“The angle can enjoy disappointment.”

“They say the exterior feeds make the station look like a novelty object.”

Feli glanced toward the nearest wall display, where the public exterior shot rotated through its approved sequence: Der Himmelskrug hanging against black space, tall and unmistakably shaped like a Bavarian beer stein if one were honest, surrounded by disciplined approach lights, docking traffic, service arcs, and the working infrastructure that kept the joke alive.

“It is a novelty object,” Feli said. “It is also a port, brewery, residential community, hospitality hub, route-safety institution, and very expensive proof that civic legitimacy can survive terrible silhouettes. We do not solve this by pretending people have no eyes.”

Janna’s mouth twitched. “So, no chandelier line.”

“No chandelier line. Also no Command cutaway unless it is the remote feed Amara already approved.”

“Press pool C asked whether someone could stand near Flight Control for atmosphere.”

Feli stopped walking.

Janna stopped too.

“Did they use the word atmosphere?”

“With confidence.”

“Tell them Flight Control has atmosphere. It is mostly oxygen and consequences. They can receive approved remote exterior and traffic feeds in the press suite.”

Janna made a note. “You want that softened?”

“I just did.”

Feli started walking again.

Her earpiece chimed once, the tone for Flight Control priority but not emergency. Everyone on the nearest staff route heard the first half-second of it and became still in the particular way station workers became still around traffic authority.

Amara Reid’s voice entered Feli’s ear warm, steady, and sharpened to a point.

“Opening coordination, Flight Control. We have twelve early arrivals trying to become eleven early arrivals and one lesson.”

Feli touched two fingers to her earpiece. “Communications receiving.”

“No danger. No declared faults. No emergency vectors. Just enthusiasm with engines.”

On a nearby display, the exterior feed shifted to a traffic schematic. The ships were not close enough to be alarming, which was the point. Colored lanes curved around the upper handle approach, each vessel held in a clean, boring, life-preserving order that made terrible television until one remembered the alternative.

Amara continued, “One charter pilot has used the phrase ‘historic first wave’ twice and ‘surely there’s flexibility’ once.”

“Was there flexibility?” Feli asked.

“There was education.”

Feli looked toward the press suite doors, where a producer in a silver jacket was trying to charm a volunteer into moving a marker three centimeters closer to a restricted corridor. The volunteer smiled with murderous training.

“Do I need language?” Feli asked.

“Only if they try to make impatience sound ceremonial. Current traffic hold is routine. Opening queue is controlled. Flight Control expects the public release window to remain viable.”

“Do you want me to say ‘viable’?”

“You may say ‘beautifully managed’ if you need the lie to be emotionally supportive.”

“I try not to lie before noon.”

“It is not noon anywhere relevant.”

“Then I’ll say controlled.”

“Good. Also, if anyone asks, you may quote me: ‘You may be important after you stop moving.’”

Feli smiled despite herself. “That sounds familiar.”

“I improved it for the occasion.”

The Flight Control channel clicked back to monitored quiet.

Janna, who had only heard Feli’s side and understood enough from experience to fear the rest, said, “Traffic?”

“Controlled.”

“Good controlled or terrifying controlled?”

“Amara controlled.”

“That is not an answer.”

“It is the safest answer we have.”

They reached the event coordination point: a long temporary console set along the inner edge of Hospitality Level 1, not in the public path and not hidden enough for staff to pretend the work was magical. From there Feli could see the public arrival threshold, the press suite, the main taproom across the atrium, and three different countdown displays disagreeing by two seconds because someone in staging had synced one of them to the media package instead of station time.

She pointed.

Janna followed the line of her finger and winced. “I’ll fix the countdown.”

“Thank you.”

“Before or after I tell press pool C that Command is not a decorative backdrop?”

“Before. Time is objective. Journalists are recurring.”

Janna left.

Feli set her slate on the console, opened the public language stack, and watched the first media montage roll through the internal monitor.

The first feed was **Starlight Tonight**, which had committed fully to delight.

“Three hours from now,” said a host with perfect hair and the brave grin of someone speaking from a studio nowhere near docking traffic, “the galaxy’s most improbable hospitality landmark opens its doors. Is it a brewery? Is it a port? Is it, as one viral post put it, ‘a beer mug with docking authority’? We’ll be live as Der Himmelskrug welcomes its first public visitors.”

Feli exhaled through her nose.

Not bad. Joke first, but not only joke.

The second feed was **Civic Meridian**, and it sounded as if someone had ironed a government report until it developed manners.

“Der Himmelskrug’s opening represents a notable expansion in partner-backed route infrastructure. Station officials describe the facility as a combined hospitality, transit, residential, and safety-support node. Observers will be watching whether the station’s private founding structure can operate with sufficient public accountability.”

Feli tapped a note into the media slate.

**Good. Dry enough to store grain in. Use for legitimacy clip if needed.**

The third feed was **Galaxy News**. The reporter stood in the H1 press suite, exactly where she was allowed to stand, with the main taproom visible across the atrium behind her and a staff route very deliberately not visible to her left.

“Behind the jokes about the shape, today’s opening raises serious questions: who controls traffic priority, how are workers protected during high-demand public events, what authority does Flight Control hold over wealthy private traffic, and how transparent will station governance be when spectacle meets daily life?”

Feli paused the feed.

“That one,” she said.

No one answered because Janna had gone to fix time and the volunteer was still winning a border war with the silver-jacketed producer.

Feli saved the clip anyway.

That one was dangerous in the useful way. It asked the questions that made the station stronger if the answers held.

Her earpiece chimed again.

This tone was not Flight Control.

It was taproom.

Feli closed her eyes for exactly one breath.

“Communications,” she said.

Mateo Alvarez’s voice arrived smooth and bright, which meant something had gone wrong and was being escorted away from guests before it learned confidence.

“Feli, small taproom note.”

“Small notes do not come in on priority.”

“Emotionally small.”

“Mateo.”

“We have a minor issue with the ceremonial line.”

From somewhere across the atrium, the main taproom doors opened and closed. Feli could not see the ceremonial tap from here, because Mateo had been very firm about sightlines, dignity, and not letting cameras fall in love with unfinished work. But she could see two servers stop smiling at the same time.

“Define issue,” Feli said.

There was a pause.

Behind Mateo, someone said, faintly and with Bavarian grief, “It is clogged.”

Mateo covered the pickup badly enough that Feli heard a palm hit fabric.

“Not clogged,” he said. “Flow-resistant.”

“Is flow resisting because something is clogging it?”

“Lukas has asked me not to use public-facing language before he has insulted the line privately.”

Another voice entered, lower, younger, and thoroughly unimpressed by ceremony.

“This line does not care what day it is.”

Feli looked toward the taproom.

“Hello, Lukas.”

“Feli.”

“Is the ceremonial line safe?”

“Yes.”

“Is the beer compromised?”

“No.”

“Is the first pour compromised?”

“Only if someone stupid makes time more important than beer.”

Mateo said, “He means we are evaluating a process-preserving adjustment.”

“I mean if anyone touches that tap for a camera before I clear it, I will remove the hand from the decision.”

“Lukas,” Mateo said, still too smooth, “remember we discussed threat verbs.”

“I used remove.”

Feli pinched the bridge of her nose.

Above the taproom doors, the countdown changed to **02:58:14**.

“All right,” she said. “Operational answer.”

Lukas answered first. “We isolate the line, check pressure, clear or reroute, verify quality, then pour. If it is not right, we use the backup line and say so.”

Mateo added, “Hospitality answer: guests will not notice unless someone tells them before we have a solution.”

“Media answer,” Feli said, “is that no one says ‘clogged’ on a channel that can be overheard by a bored camera operator.”

A brief silence followed.

Mateo said, “Agreed.”

Lukas said, “I make no promise to spare the feelings of the line.”

“You may hurt the line privately.”

“Good.”

The taproom channel closed.

Feli made a note.

**First pour risk: contained. Do not let symbolism bully process.**

Then she made a second note.

**Check Heinz reaction if he hears. Family pressure likely. Do not let him go to taproom unless useful or funny. Prefer not useful.**

Her slate vibrated under her hand.

The private countdown had found her.

**HEINZ SCHMITT:**  
I have the speech.

Feli looked at the message.

Then the next one arrived.

**HEINZ SCHMITT:**  
Probably.

Then:

**HEINZ SCHMITT:**  
Do not react.

Feli reacted.

She turned away from the event console before anyone could study her face and walked toward the quieter edge of the coordination point, where a carved wall panel broke the sound from the press suite and the atrium became a murmur instead of a room trying to become history.

She called him.

He answered on the third pulse, which meant he had considered not answering and decided that was strategically worse.

“Feli,” Heinz said.

“Heinz.”

“I said do not react.”

“I did not react. I relocated.”

“That is reacting with geography.”

“You sent three messages in a row and one of them was ‘probably.’ Where are you?”

A pause.

“Near the upper brewery.”

“Near the upper brewery as in appropriately near, or near as in you are hiding behind process equipment while wearing opening clothes?”

“I am not hiding. I am confirming whether there is an operational issue with the ceremonial line.”

“Lukas is handling the line.”

“Yes.”

“Mateo is handling the room.”

“Yes.”

“Therefore you are handling the speech.”

Another pause.

Feli heard machinery hum behind him, muted by distance and doors that knew better than to turn production into ambiance. He was not inside brewery production. He would not have violated that boundary for nerves. He was near enough to feel useful and far enough to avoid the thing that needed him.

“I have several versions,” he said.

“How many is several?”

“That depends on whether fragments count as versions.”

“They do when they are hiding from you in different folders.”

He sighed. “There is ‘Opening remarks final revised human.’ There is ‘Opening remarks less stupid v2 because Feli will ask.’ There is ‘Public station welcome technical-emotional bridge attempt.’”

Feli closed her eyes.

“And?”

“And one called ‘Founding remarks load-bearing truth draft do not use.’”

“That one.”

“No.”

“Yes.”

“It is not polished.”

“I did not ask for polish.”

“It is too sentimental.”

“You built a beer-stein-shaped station with a residential community and public bubble elevators. Sentiment left the room with dignity months ago.”

“It has a paragraph about the handle.”

“Delete the paragraph about the handle.”

“I like the handle.”

“The handle can enjoy disappointment.”

He made a sound that almost became a laugh and stopped before it had to admit anything.

Feli softened her voice by one degree. Not more. More would make him bolt into engineering language.

“Heinz,” she said, “people will laugh at the shape.”

“Yes.”

“Let them.”

“I know.”

“No. You know in the part of your head that reads permits. I mean let them laugh while you tell them what the shape is carrying.”

He was quiet.

Behind her, Hospitality Level 1 kept working. Press markers shifted. A public sign spoke its route test in two languages and touch tones. Somewhere across the atrium, the taproom was negotiating with physics.

Heinz said, very flatly, “What if it becomes real and I am not enough for it?”

There it was.

Not the speech problem.

The speech.

Feli did not rescue him from the sentence.

“Then it is good you are not opening it alone,” she said.

He breathed once. The connection caught the sound and gave it to her without mercy.

Her slate flashed another alert.

Residential Life priority.

H2 food hall pending final verification.

Taproom line contained.

Flight Control queue controlled.

Press asking for more access than they would receive.

Countdown: **02:52:09**.

Feli looked across H1 at the sealed Arrival Control release corridor, the taproom doors, the press suite, the warm public signs, the hidden staff routes, the absurd serious station preparing to let strangers become guests.

The opening was no longer a countdown.

It was a stress test with music.

“Find the truth draft,” she told Heinz. “Delete the handle paragraph. Then call me when you are ready to stop proving this place deserves to exist.”

“And if I am not ready?”

“Then call me anyway.”

She ended the call before he could make gratitude into a technical appendix.

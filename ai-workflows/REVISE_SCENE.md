# Scene Revision Workflow

Purpose: revise Heavenly Stein story material without breaking canon, flattening character behavior, or accidentally promoting draft facts.

Use this workflow for prose, outlines, dialogue, beat sheets, and scene plans.

## Revision rule

Improve the scene while preserving the user's intent.

Do not use revision as an excuse to:

- retcon active canon;
- revive old chronology;
- make competent characters stupid;
- erase consequences;
- turn private grief, labor concerns, Medical details, or active investigations into public spectacle;
- force every scene onto Der Himmelskrug when the premise is off-station.

## Step 1: Identify revision type

Choose one or more:

- Clarity pass.
- Voice pass.
- Continuity pass.
- Sensory/accessibility pass.
- Comedy/rhythm pass.
- Stakes/consequence pass.
- Station-blocking pass.
- Off-station location/jurisdiction pass.
- Scene compression.
- Expansion.
- Canon-risk review.

## Step 2: Preserve the premise

Before revising, state the intended scene in one sentence.

Example:

```text
This scene is about Feli trying to keep a public-facing station process humane while a bureaucratic safety rule makes everyone move three meters left.
```

If the scene is off-station, state that clearly.

Example:

```text
This scene is off-station and should use route, jurisdiction, and communications checks rather than station level-blocking.
```

## Step 3: Select checks

Use only relevant checks.

Always consider:

- `ai-workflows/CONTEXT_PACKS.md`
- `ai-workflows/CONTINUITY_AUDIT.md`
- `ai-workflows/CANON_PROMOTION.md`

Use when relevant:

- `ai-workflows/CHARACTER_VOICE_CHECK.md`
- `ai-workflows/STATION_SCENE_CHECK.md`
- station blocking guide and mechanics cheat sheet;
- character quick cards;
- law, labor, media, economy, transport, ship, or weapons canon.

## Step 4: Revise for character competence

Characters can be funny without being stupid.

Check:

- Is the character acting from pressure, duty, desire, confusion, fatigue, grief, institutional constraints, or cultural mismatch?
- Does the joke preserve dignity where dignity matters?
- Does the person know what they should know?
- Does the scene respect public/private boundaries?
- Is authority procedural rather than magical?

## Step 5: Revise for infrastructure

If the scene uses systems, make them behave like systems.

Check:

- transport;
- communications;
- money/fines/liens;
- destination law;
- Flight Control;
- Arrival Control;
- clean/restricted routing;
- station public/restricted boundaries;
- ship systems;
- media delay and privacy;
- labor rules and guild consequences.

Only apply station-specific checks if the scene is on or directly routed through the station.

## Step 6: Revise for sensory access

Keep Markdown and scene description screen-reader friendly.

Check:

- sound;
- smell;
- surface;
- air;
- crowding;
- lighting without relying only on color;
- route clarity;
- who can hear, see, smell, touch, or be blocked by the environment.

## Step 7: Mark candidate canon

If revision adds a reusable detail, mark it.

Use:

```text
Candidate canon: <detail>
Suggested owner file: <file>
Reason: <why it may matter later>
Risk: <conflict or uncertainty>
```

Do not silently add durable world facts.

## Step 8: Report changes

A revision response should include:

```text
What changed:
- <summary>

Continuity notes:
- <notes>

Candidate canon:
- <items or none>

Remaining risks:
- <risks or none>
```

## Revision principle

A good revision makes the scene clearer, warmer, sharper, and safer without sanding off the absurd civic-infrastructure premise.

# Weapons, Combat, and Force Rules

Canon navigation: [Home](README.md) | [Previous: 17 Character Personality and Scene Fingerprints](17-character-personality-and-scene-fingerprints.md) | [Next: 19 Tested Story Precedents](19-tested-story-precedents.md)


These rules define the baseline for personal weapons, starship weapons,
shields, force escalation, and the limits that keep combat from becoming
unlimited convenience. They do not replace existing ship capability
profiles; they provide the shared weapons language those profiles draw
from.

## Purpose

Combat in this canon should remain serious, mechanically grounded, and
consequence-aware. Weapons can be funny in naming, paperwork, policy, or
character reaction, but force itself is not a toy.

Core goals:

- distinguish stun, restraint, disruption, disablement, wounding,
  killing, and destruction;
- prevent unlimited weapons, infinite power, or consequence-free
  disabling shots;
- make Heinz's custom weapons fit the same rules as everyone else's;
- give ship combat a standard vocabulary without returning to brittle
  numerical stat blocks;
- support Janelle, Amara, Heinz, the IFP, and station policy with a clear
  force ladder.

## Core Principles

- Nonlethal does not mean harmless.
- Disabling is usually harder than destroying.
- Every weapon has limits: power, heat, ammunition, cooldown, firing arc,
  target lock, legal authorization, collateral risk, maintenance state,
  and user judgment.
- Personal shields and ship shields buy time. They do not make the user
  invulnerable.
- Stun, disrupt, disable, wound, kill, and destroy are different actions
  with different legal and moral consequences.
- Starship weapons near stations, traffic lanes, races, festivals,
  convoys, or civilian habitats are politically and operationally serious
  even when fired defensively.
- A station is not a street. Every missed shot has plumbing, pressure
  seals, children, workers, oxygen, or traffic behind it.

## Effects Vocabulary

- **Stun:** temporarily incapacitate a living target. Intended nonlethal,
  but not harmless. Stun effects can fail through armor or shields, kill
  vulnerable targets, trigger medical implants, cause falls, or become
  lethal in crowds, low gravity, stairwells, or machinery spaces.
- **Restrain:** physically immobilize, bind, slow, separate, or contain a
  target. Restraint tools can injure when misused or when the target is
  moving fast.
- **Disrupt:** interfere temporarily with shields, electronics, sensors,
  locks, drones, comms, weapons, or control systems. Disruption is usually
  nonlethal to people, but it can become dangerous around implants,
  life-support systems, medical equipment, pressure controls, or active
  traffic systems.
- **Disable:** stop a machine, drone, weapon, limb actuator, vehicle, ship
  subsystem, or ship without necessarily destroying it. Disablement is
  precision work and may still kill through secondary failures.
- **Wound:** injure a living target without immediate intended death.
- **Kill:** use lethal force against a living target.
- **Destroy:** catastrophically damage a machine, structure, drone, ship,
  cargo system, or habitat system.

## Personal Weapon Classes

Personal weapons are commonly described by practical class rather than
brand. Local colonies may use different legal codes, but these categories
are understood across IFP, partner-colony, and station contexts.

- **Class S — stun weapon:** stun pistol, stun baton, security pulse
  sidearm, close-range neural/muscular interrupter. Intended to
  incapacitate living targets. Nonlethal intent does not guarantee a
  harmless result.
- **Class R — restraint weapon:** foam restraint, restraint net, shock
  cuff, tether launcher, door-frame containment web. Used to immobilize,
  separate, slow, or secure targets.
- **Class D — disruptor weapon:** shield disruptor, electronics pulse
  tool, drone-stopper, lock disruptor, weapon-safety pulse. Used to
  interfere with shields, electronics, drones, locks, comms, and weapon
  systems.
- **Class L — laser sidearm:** compact directed-energy sidearm for short
  to medium range. Lethal-capable. Some models have low-output stun or
  heat-warning modes, but they remain dangerous weapons.
- **Class C — laser carbine or rifle:** general combat directed-energy
  longarm. Used for accurate medium-range force, shield stress, drone
  defeat, and lethal combat.
- **Class P — precision laser:** long-range or high-focus directed-energy
  weapon for armor weak points, shield stress, overwatch, anti-drone work,
  and precision disablement. Lethal-capable and heat-intensive.
- **Class B — breaching/cutting weapon:** industrial cutter, breaching
  laser, plasma torch, emergency rescue cutter. Often a tool first, but
  highly dangerous as a weapon.
- **Class H — heavy personal weapon:** anti-armor, anti-drone,
  anti-vehicle, breaching launcher, portable missile, heavy pulse weapon.
  Restricted and not appropriate for public carry.

## Personal Shields and Power

Personal shields reduce, deflect, or spread energy impacts until they are
overloaded, depleted, disrupted, or bypassed. They are not perfect
protection.

General shield behavior:

- strongest against fast impacts, directed-energy strikes, fragments, and
  brief high-energy contact;
- weaker against grappling, slow pushes, sustained heat soak, gas,
  flooding, environmental hazards, and attacks that begin inside the
  shield boundary;
- vulnerable to repeated heavy strikes, precision shield-disruption,
  power depletion, or poor maintenance;
- likely to flare, shimmer, spark, or produce a local distortion when hit;
- dangerous to rely on in crowds, because impacts and deflections can
  create secondary harm.

Power limits:

- Personal laser weapons draw from internal cells, external packs,
  harness power, or compatible belt systems.
- High-output shots drain more power.
- Rapid fire creates heat.
- Overheated weapons throttle, lock out, degrade accuracy, or risk damage.
- Stun settings usually consume less power but have shorter range and are
  less reliable through armor or shields.
- Disruptor pulses may require cooldown and can interfere with friendly
  equipment.
- Any weapon that shares a power source with a shield competes with that
  shield for endurance.

## Heinz's Custom Schmitt-Pattern Weapons

Heinz's three personal weapons are custom Schmitt-pattern
directed-energy weapons integrated with his personal combat power harness.
Current canon classifies Heinz's personal weapons as the **Schmitt
Pattern Field Carbine**, **Schmitt Pattern Longbeam**, and **Schmitt
Pattern Scatterbeam**.

Use the Schmitt-pattern names or the relevant weapon classes in narration, planning, dialogue guidance, station policy, IFP records, maintenance notes, and incident reports.

### Schmitt Pattern Field Carbine

Class: custom Class C combat laser.

Role:

- general-purpose personal combat weapon;
- accurate medium-range fire;
- drone defeat;
- shield stress;
- controlled lethal force;
- limited low-output stun or disabling modes when target, distance, and
  environment allow.

Limits:

- not ideal for crowds;
- high-output shots drain harness power quickly;
- sustained fire creates heat;
- stun mode is unreliable through armor or active shields;
- lethal setting is never casual in station spaces.

Informal nickname option: **Die Vernünftige**, “the sensible one.”

### Schmitt Pattern Longbeam

Class: custom Class P precision laser.

Role:

- long-range precision;
- high-focus beam work;
- armor weak-point targeting;
- shield puncture or shield stress;
- anti-drone and anti-sensor work at distance;
- overwatch.

Limits:

- slower cycling than the Field Carbine;
- high heat load;
- requires stable aim and clean line of sight;
- dangerous in pressurized public environments;
- poor close-quarters weapon;
- disablement shots require excellent target knowledge.

Informal nickname option: **Der Lange Grund**, “the long reason.”

### Schmitt Pattern Scatterbeam

Class: custom close-pattern Class C/D directed-energy weapon.

Role:

- short-range multi-emitter burst;
- close-quarters defense;
- drone swarm suppression;
- shield flare or overload at close range;
- corridor denial;
- controlled low-output crowd-control use only under strict conditions.

The Scatterbeam is not a projectile firearm. It uses a multi-emitter
pulse pattern or rapidly phased cone of directed energy. It is terrifying
up close, falls off quickly with distance, and carries serious collateral
risk in pressurized public spaces.

Limits:

- rapid range falloff;
- serious risk near civilians, glass, pressure seals, exposed conduits,
  restaurant equipment, school spaces, medical areas, and artifacts;
- can cause burns, eye injury, shield flare, secondary ignition, or
  equipment damage;
- Station Security strongly dislikes it near hospitality, retail,
  residential, or education areas.

Informal nickname option: **Die Schlechte Idee**, “the bad idea.”

Useful Janelle-style summary: “A lawsuit with a trigger.”

## Heinz's Personal Combat Power Harness

Heinz's black leather weapon belt is more precisely a **personal combat
power harness** with large silver clips and compatible holsters. It
powers and coordinates his holstered Schmitt-pattern weapons and his
personal shield.

The harness supports:

- weapon power balancing;
- personal shield draw;
- holstered weapon charging;
- diagnostic checks;
- limited cooling support;
- safety locks;
- IFP or station authorization handshakes when required;
- compatible personal gear recharge.

Limits:

- weapon fire and shield protection draw from the same endurance pool;
- heavy shield impacts reduce available weapon endurance;
- high-output Longbeam shots drain power sharply;
- Scatterbeam bursts create heat and power spikes;
- overheated or depleted harness systems can throttle weapons;
- a compatible personal gear recharger recharges the harness, attached
  weapons, and linked shield as one system;
- the nanite healer remains separate and does not recharge from the
  harness.

## Der Himmelskrug Personal Weapons Policy

Der Himmelskrug is a pressurized station with public hospitality,
residential areas, children, alcohol, dirty-side hazards, dense transit,
medical facilities, festivals, and active flight operations. Its weapons
policy is stricter than many planetary public spaces.

Normal operations:

- Public visitors declare weapons at Arrival Control.
- Public carry requires authorization.
- Legal personal weapons may be sealed, checked, locked aboard, or
  route-restricted depending on person, role, event, destination, and
  weapon class.
- Station Security, IFP personnel, and authorized protective details may
  carry under logged authority.
- Dirty-side tools that can function as weapons remain dirty-side or in
  controlled service routes unless specifically cleared.
- Rearming alcoves are secure service points, not public shopping.

Festival and major event operations:

- personal weapons are banned from public hospitality, retail, cultural,
  service, residential, and education areas;
- weapons must be locked aboard under verified control or checked into
  station security storage;
- crew-service corridor access is more tightly controlled;
- Security, IFP, and authorized protective details remain logged and
  controlled;
- emergency exceptions must be logged and reviewed.

Useful policy line: “Don't bring lasers to beer.”

## Starship Weapon Families

Starship weapons are described by family and mission role rather than
exact numerical damage values. The same family can vary enormously by
ship size, power system, fire-control quality, maintenance state, legal
license, and military/civilian grade.

- **PD — point-defense laser:** defensive lasers for missiles, drones,
  debris, boarding pods, and close threats. Defensive does not mean safe;
  point defense can still kill if misused.
- **LB — light beam laser:** precision shield/hull pressure, common on
  armed civilian ships and frontier craft.
- **HB — heavy beam laser:** shield breaking, hull cutting, and serious
  anti-ship work. Usually warship, IFP, or heavily restricted civilian
  equipment.
- **PC — pulse cannon:** high-impact shield and hull strikes. Less
  delicate than beam work, often stronger per shot, and less suited to
  fine disablement.
- **DB — disabling beam:** targets engines, sensors, weapons,
  maneuvering systems, control surfaces, or external modules. Requires
  strong sensors, good lock, relative position, and user skill.
- **EM — electromagnetic disruptor:** interferes with sensors, drones,
  comms, light shielding, or electronics. Dangerous near fragile systems,
  medical ships, station approaches, or traffic-control equipment.
- **GR — grapple/tractor system:** capture, tow, stabilize, rescue,
  docking assist, or debris control. Nonlethal intent, but risky when
  targets tumble, resist, or carry hazardous cargo.
- **MS — missile system:** guided standoff attack. Ammunition-limited,
  legally serious, and dangerous near civilian traffic.
- **TP — torpedo system:** heavy anti-ship or anti-fortification ordnance.
  Politically serious escalation.
- **AP — armor-piercing kinetic shell:** physical penetrator for armor or
  hull work. Dangerous near stations, habitats, and unknown cargo.
- **DR — combat drone bay:** remote or automated defense/offense platform.
  Requires command bandwidth, recovery planning, and strong identification
  discipline.
- **MN — mine or denial charge:** area denial weapon. Highly restricted
  because routes, rescue lanes, and civilian traffic make abandoned or
  poorly logged denial weapons intolerable.

## Ship Shield Families

- **Civilian deflector:** debris, micrometeorites, light impacts, docking
  accidents, and routine environmental protection.
- **Reinforced civilian shield:** frontier hazards, raiders, stronger
  debris fields, minor weapons fire, and rougher route conditions.
- **Combat shield:** ship combat, repeated energy impacts, active
  tactical maneuvering, and serious hostile contact.
- **Heavy layered shield:** fleet, fortress, or heavy command defense.
  Can absorb major punishment but creates massive power, heat, and
  tactical-management demands.
- **Directional shield reinforcement:** temporarily hardens one arc or
  sector at the cost of power and vulnerability elsewhere.
- **Emergency hull-hugging shield:** last-resort survival layer. Buys
  time, not victory.

## Standard Ship Loadout Tiers

These tiers are story guidance, not exact stat blocks.

### Tier 0 — Unarmed civilian craft

Examples: shuttles, passenger pods, school/service transports, small
private craft.

Typical systems:

- civilian deflector;
- emergency beacon;
- collision avoidance;
- no weapons.

May include docking grapples, flare/decoy systems, or panic transponders.

Doctrine: travel safely, obey traffic control, and call for help.

### Tier 1 — Light defensive civilian craft

Examples: couriers, small freighters, family haulers, long-range utility
craft.

Typical systems:

- reinforced civilian shield;
- point-defense laser;
- emergency decoys;
- possibly one light beam laser where lawful.

Doctrine: survive, evade, call for help, and do not fight fair.

### Tier 2 — Armed frontier utility craft

Examples: upgraded freighters, salvage craft, explorers, hazardous-route
service ships.

Typical systems:

- reinforced shield;
- point defense;
- light beam lasers;
- disabling beam or electromagnetic disruptor;
- tractor/grapple;
- limited missiles only under license and story need.

Doctrine: deter raiders, disable if possible, survive long enough for
help or escalation.

### Tier 3 — Armed expedition or patrol cutter

Examples: Nur Für Den Fall, route patrol cutters, IFP light response
craft, serious private expedition craft.

Typical systems:

- combat-capable reinforced shield;
- point defense;
- light/heavy beam mix;
- disabling beam;
- electromagnetic disruptor;
- tractor/grapple;
- limited missiles;
- strong sensors and secure comms.

Doctrine: survive, shield, disable, escape, rescue, and escort.

### Tier 4 — Fast response combat craft

Examples: Das Geht Schnell, interceptors, rescue-strike craft,
anti-raider ships.

Typical systems:

- combat shield;
- point defense;
- beam lasers;
- pulse cannons;
- disabling systems;
- limited missiles;
- fast intercept sensors.

Doctrine: arrive first, intercept, rescue, disable, and stop the disaster
before it finishes.

### Tier 5 — Dedicated strike ship

Examples: Niemand Wird Dich Vermissen.

Typical systems:

- combat shield;
- high-end fire control;
- heavy beam lasers;
- pulse cannons;
- disabling beam;
- missiles;
- armor-piercing kinetic option;
- stealth/cloak systems where lawful and mission-appropriate.

Doctrine: hunt, break shields, and end isolated hostile threats quickly.

Limits: not diplomatic, not cargo, not family transport, and not a public
symbol of restraint.

### Tier 6 — Heavy command / fortress combat ship

Examples: Komm Her Und Stirb Schnell.

Typical systems:

- heavy layered shields;
- multiple point-defense grids;
- heavy beam batteries;
- pulse cannon batteries;
- missile and torpedo systems;
- combat drone support;
- command/fire-control network;
- fleet coordination;
- heavy sensors;
- emergency rescue/support capacity.

Doctrine: arrive and change the battle, anchor allied ships, absorb major
punishment, coordinate allied response, and control battlespace.

Limits: political consequences, traffic consequences, escalation
consequences, and never a casual appearance.

## Starship Ordnance Rearming

Consumable starship weapons are treated as restricted hazardous work, not ordinary garage shopping.

- Live missiles, torpedoes, mines/denial charges, combat-drone munitions, armor-piercing kinetic reloads, and comparable consumable starship ordnance are handled only through Der Himmelskrug's Restricted Works restricted ordnance/rearming facilities or equivalent authorized sites.
- The Starship Garage may handle safed inspection, diagnostics, declaration paperwork, and non-live weapon-system checks. It does not casually handle open ordnance crates or live reloads.
- A ship that is clean in the contamination sense may still be routed to Restricted Works for ordnance handling. Restricted does not always mean contaminated; it can mean hazardous, legally controlled, or high-consequence.
- Rearming requires authorization checks, destination/route legality, Flight Control release interface, Security/Compliance awareness, safing logs, and separation from public, Hospitality, Retail, Residential, school, museum, and normal clean cargo routes.
- Impairment, cannabis use, alcohol use, active public-event status, unresolved weapons irregularities, or legal holds can block or delay rearming and departure.

## Starship Weapon Limits

- Ships cannot fire starship weapons while cloaked.
- Shields can remain active while cloaked, but shield impacts may reveal
  approximate presence.
- Starship weapons near stations, docking queues, traffic lanes, race
  courses, solar arrays, convoys, and civilian habitats require clear
  authorization except immediate defense against imminent harm.
- Disabling a hostile ship is usually harder than destroying it.
- Precision disablement requires sensor lock, relative position, target
  knowledge, shield state, time, and room for secondary effects.
- Missiles, torpedoes, mines, denial charges, armor-piercing kinetics,
  and drone swarms are legally and politically serious.
- Point defense is defensive, but it can still kill.
- Ship shields can absorb, flare, collapse, redirect, or bleed damage,
  but not forever.
- Weapon fire is limited by capacitor recharge, heat rejection,
  ammunition, firing arcs, sensor lock, power allocation, crew workload,
  and maintenance state.
- Traffic safety and collateral risk can make the technically best shot
  legally or morally unavailable.

## Force Escalation Ladder

This ladder is story guidance for Security, Flight Control, IFP response,
Heinz's personal action, and ship combat. Emergencies may skip steps, but
skipped steps should have reasons and later review when appropriate.

1. Presence, observation, and positioning.
2. Verbal command, traffic order, warning, or PA instruction.
3. Physical barrier, lockdown, sealed door, traffic hold, vector denial,
   or route closure.
4. Restraint tool or containment measure.
5. Stun.
6. Disrupt or disable equipment, shields, weapons, drones, or systems.
7. Lethal personal force.
8. Starship disabling fire.
9. Starship lethal or destructive force.
10. Strategic combat deployment or heavy fleet response.

## Continuity Notes

- Older exact numerical ship-stat entries remain deprecated. Use tiers,
  weapon families, doctrine, preparation, crew skill, tactical position,
  surprise, terrain, support, and mission objective.
- Use Field Carbine, Longbeam, and Scatterbeam for Heinz's personal weapons; do not revert to older informal labels.
- The Scatterbeam is not a projectile firearm.
- Nonlethal tools should create moral choices, not remove them.
- Disabling should be treated as a skilled, conditional outcome, not a
  default button.
- Der Himmelskrug's weapons policy should treat public hospitality and event spaces as high-risk civilian infrastructure, not as places where weapons can be casually carried or brandished.
- Cloak rules remain controlled by the ship canon: cloaks hide ships;
  they do not hide consequences.

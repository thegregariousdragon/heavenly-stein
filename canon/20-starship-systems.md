This file defines non-weapon starship systems for story generation and continuity. It complements `canon/05-ships.md`, `canon/06-transport-and-movement.md`, `canon/18-weapons-combat-and-force-rules.md`, and `quick-reference/MECHANICS_CHEATSHEET.md`.

## Scope

Use this file when a scene involves propulsion, hyperdrive use, navigation, traffic clearance, ship power, heat, sensors, communications, life support, cargo containment, maintenance diagnostics, tool cartridges, ship disablement, or artifact interference.

This is not a hard technical manual. The canon should create constraints and consequences, not exact stat blocks.

## Core rule: systems create consequences

Starship systems should create constraints, not loopholes.

A ship's ability to move, jump, cloak, scan, transmit, survive, or rescue others depends on power, heat, maintenance state, crew workload, route data, traffic control, legal clearance, cargo state, local hazards, and damage isolation.

Do not solve scenes by inventing a system capability that bypasses Flight Control, dirty-side rules, transport limits, jurisdiction, evidence preservation, or cost.

Travel time remains narratively flexible, but not consequence-free.

## Propulsion overview

Ships normally use three broad movement families:

- **Sublight drive:** high-speed normal-space propulsion below faster-than-light travel.
- **Hyperdrive:** faster-than-light interstellar drive.
- **Maneuvering and fine-control systems:** thrusters and control systems for docking, attitude, station-keeping, rotation, fine positioning, and close operations.

These systems can share power, sensors, controls, and safety interlocks, but they are not interchangeable.

## Sublight drive

A sublight drive moves a ship through normal space at high but non-FTL speeds. It is used for in-system travel, station approach and departure, traffic-lane maneuvering, combat maneuvering, rescue positioning, and travel between nearby objects when hyperdrive is unnecessary, unsafe, or unavailable.

In open space, a healthy sublight drive can achieve extremely fast normal-space travel, including high fractions of lightspeed where route geometry, traffic, heat, crew safety, local law, and ship condition permit.

Near stations, colonies, traffic lanes, debris fields, gravity hazards, docking zones, or dirty-side handling zones, sublight output is heavily restricted. Flight Control instructions override pilot convenience.

Sublight drives may have initiation harmonics or other startup conditions. The Stillstone precedent establishes that some artifacts can interfere with sublight-drive initiation without physically destroying the drive.

## Hyperdrive

A hyperdrive moves a ship faster than light through established or computable interstellar routes. It is not teleportation.

Hyperdrive use requires stable primary power, charged field systems, correct route geometry, valid navigation data, safe activation separation, confirmed drive/access-path integrity, and clearance where applicable.

A ship may have reactor power and still be unable to jump safely.

Hyperdrive activation is a high-consequence state. A small problem that appears tolerable during dockside inspection can become catastrophic under activation load.

Maintenance-cartridge note: trusted maintenance tooling can create serious risk if firmware, tolerance logic, authentication, or custody controls fail. Such failures should be treated as systems problems, not as simple bomb plots.

## Maneuvering thrusters and fine-control systems

Maneuvering thrusters and fine-control systems handle docking, undocking, attitude control, station-keeping, cargo alignment, rescue holds, rotation, berth approach, and precise local positioning.

Sublight drive is for meaningful normal-space movement. Fine-control systems are for exact placement and orientation.

Close operations should use the appropriate controlled movement mode. A ship that can cross a system quickly should still move slowly and predictably near people, stations, docks, cargo locks, and debris.

### Attitude-control axes

When describing starship attitude or precision handling, remember the three basic rotational control axes: **pitch**, **yaw**, and **roll**. A maneuver may emphasize one axis, but tight gates, asteroid-threading, docking, evasive attitude changes, and high-performance race lines often require coordinated control across all three.

Do not reduce a three-dimensional starship handling problem to yaw/roll only unless the scene deliberately establishes why pitch is irrelevant or locked out.

## Navigation, route data, and clearance

Navigation is safety infrastructure.

Ship movement depends on route data, traffic instructions, local advisories, hazard flags, transponder identity, destination acceptance, and corridor clearance. IFP navigation data, known routes, stable jump corridors, colony approach lanes, and station Flight Control instructions all matter.

A ship may be physically capable of travel but not legally or safely cleared to travel.

Exclusion corridors, such as Charcoal-Nine, are controlled spaces used to reduce risk during dangerous tests or high-consequence maneuvers. A clear corridor means the known traffic and hazard conditions are controlled; it does not make bad maintenance safe.

## Reactors and primary power

Most starships use certified primary reactors sized to their class, route role, and mission profile. Reactor type varies by manufacturer, era, colony standard, military or civilian certification, and upgrade history. Canon does not require a single universal reactor model unless a specific story locks one.

Reactors feed propulsion, hyperdrive charging and activation, life support, sensors, communications, shields, cargo containment, gravity and acceleration compensation, medical systems, heat management, and weapons where applicable.

A reactor is not a magic battery. Available power does not mean safe power. Output is limited by heat, conduits, containment state, safety interlocks, crew workload, local traffic law, cargo condition, and damage isolation.

Hyperdrive activation requires stable primary power, charged field systems, correct route geometry, and confirmed hyperdrive access-path integrity.

Reactor trouble does not have to mean explosion. Rising heat, power rationing, life-support margin loss, shield collapse, cargo containment risk, comm degradation, radiation alerts, or emergency shutdown can all make a ship a rescue priority.

Writing rule: use reactor limits to create consequences, not shortcuts. Do not solve a scene by inventing extra power unless the cost, heat, risk, or certification problem is addressed.

## Power allocation and heat management

Power must be generated, routed, allocated, and cooled.

A ship can have a strong reactor and still be constrained by damaged conduits, heat buildup, fragile cargo, crew safety, traffic law, or a disabled subsystem. High output can make a problem worse if heat, containment, or structural stress is already near limits.

Ships must dump, store, route, radiate, mask, or otherwise manage heat. Cloaks can reduce, distort, or hide some signatures within limits, but they do not erase heat-management needs.

Power allocation choices should create tradeoffs: shields versus drive, sensors versus stealth, life support versus cargo containment, comms versus emissions control, or hyperdrive charge versus local maneuvering safety.

## Sensors and transponders

Ship sensors include navigation sensors, docking sensors, threat detection, rescue scans, cargo and environmental scans, maintenance diagnostics, and specialized research or artifact-detection tools.

Sensors are powerful but not magic. Cloaks, debris, traffic density, heat, radiation, active jamming, local geometry, legal privacy limits, and bad maintenance can degrade certainty.

Transponders and traffic comms are safety infrastructure, not etiquette. A ship that hides, spoofs, disables, or misdeclares identity creates a traffic and legal problem even if it does not fire a weapon.

## Communications and distress systems

Ship communications include local traffic channels, Flight Control links, distress beacons, transponder signals, encrypted private channels, route advisories, emergency challenge-response, and ship/station console traffic.

A distress call does not automatically grant the caller every requested action. Flight Control, Medical, Security, dirty-side handling, destination law, and rescue safety still matter.

Comms degradation can be a serious emergency even when engines and life support remain functional, because it can prevent clearance, coordination, identity confirmation, and rescue routing.

## Life support and habitability

Life support includes atmosphere, pressure, temperature, water, waste, medical emergency support, safe compartments, and habitable volume.

A disabled ship is not harmless if life support, heat, pressure, gravity comfort, medical capacity, crowding limits, cargo stability, or rescue access is compromised.

Emergency sheltering can buy time. It does not erase the underlying rescue obligation.

## Gravity and acceleration compensation

Most interstellar ships have gravity and acceleration-compensation systems sufficient for normal travel and safe maneuvering, but they have limits.

Sudden impacts, uncontrolled tumble, disabling fire, cargo shear, drive failure, bad repairs, or excessive acceleration can exceed comfort and safety margins.

These systems should support story consequences. A ship may survive a hit while passengers, cargo, medical patients, or fragile equipment still suffer harm.

## Cargo, containment, and hazardous loads

Cargo is part of ship safety.

A ship can be technically intact but still in crisis if cargo is unstable, undeclared, leaking, contaminated, legally frozen, quarantine-relevant, artifact-adjacent, explosive, toxic, culturally sensitive, or hazardous under acceleration or heat.

Hazardous cargo requires declared handling, routing, containment, inspection, and clearance. Dirty-side rules exist because cargo problems can become station problems.


## Maintenance, diagnostics, and tool cartridges

Maintenance systems include diagnostic logs, sealed components, drones, tool cartridges, firmware checks, tool-path records, calibration histories, and authorization chains.

Maintenance tools are safety-critical systems. A valid-looking maintenance action can be dangerous if its authorization, firmware, context, or physical effect is wrong.

Recommended high-risk maintenance controls include serialized sealed tool cartridges, firmware verification, dual authentication, maintenance-drone behavior baselines, anomaly escalation, independent review for hazardous-cargo drive inspections, and stronger chain-of-custody rules.

A tool cartridge may be physically small and procedurally ordinary while still having catastrophic potential if it acts inside a safety-critical path.

## Cloaks and emissions

Cloaks can hide, blur, reduce, or distort many sensor signatures, depending on ship quality, local conditions, power state, emissions, heat, and observer capability.

Cloaks do not eliminate mass, gravity effects, collision risk, heat needs, drive wake, legal consequences, or the need for safe traffic behavior.

Ships do not dock while cloaked. Der Himmelskrug and responsible ports treat cloaked approach as a safety and legal emergency unless explicitly cleared under extraordinary conditions.

## Damage, disablement, and rescue state

Disabling a ship is not automatically safe. A disabled ship may lose maneuvering, comms, heat control, reactor stability, life-support margin, cargo containment, medical capacity, or rescue access.

Force used against ships should preserve the principle from the mature canon: nonlethal does not mean harmless, and disabling does not mean cost-free.

The question after any disablement is not only "Did it explode?" but also "Who is now trapped, cold, hot, drifting, injured, legally frozen, cargo-compromised, or unable to call for help?"

## Artifact interference

Artifacts can affect ship systems in unusual ways, but they should not become generic magic solutions.

The Stillstone precedent establishes that an artifact can interfere with sublight-drive initiation harmonics without physically damaging engines. Movement near such artifacts should use low-emission, low-vibration, non-drive methods until cleared.

Future artifact effects should define what system is affected, under what conditions, how certainty is established, who has authority to move it, and what containment or route rules apply.


## Station garage interfaces

Der Himmelskrug uses two formal bottom-level ship/industrial interfaces.

- **Starship Garage:** clean-side normal berthing, recharge, clean cargo, crew transition, inspection, safed diagnostics, and non-hazardous ship support. Garage Ops owns local berth status, docking collars, utility hookups, cargo timing, and local response; Flight Control owns approach, traffic, and release windows.
- **Restricted Works:** restricted dirty-side / hazardous industrial handling: decon, quarantine, hazardous salvage, dirty repair, artifact/anomaly intake, restricted ordnance/rearming, evidence/compliance, industrial drones, and heavy engineering.
- A ship can be physically clean but still require Restricted Works if the task is ordnance handling, weapons irregularity, battle-damage containment, suspicious cargo, artifact-adjacent material, or high-consequence repair.
- Transfer between Starship Garage and Restricted Works uses sealed transfer, tow cradles, controlled service locks, or authorized industrial routes. There is no casual circulation between them.

## Writing rules

- Use ship systems to create process, risk, delay, cost, and accountability.
- Do not invent exact speeds, fuel formulas, reactor physics, or universal range limits unless a story needs a specific locked fact.
- Keep sublight drive, hyperdrive, and fine-control systems distinct.
- Treat Flight Control clearance as operational law, not politeness.
- Treat maintenance records, tool cartridges, firmware, and diagnostics as evidence when something goes wrong.
- Treat cargo as part of ship safety.
- Treat heat and power as constraints even on advanced ships.
- Keep cloaks useful but limited.
- Make rescue state broader than hull state.
- When in doubt, preserve consequences over convenience.
